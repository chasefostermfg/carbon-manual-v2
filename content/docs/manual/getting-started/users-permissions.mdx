A real shop has more than one person in it — a salesperson quoting work, a buyer raising
purchase orders, operators on the floor, a bookkeeper closing the month. Carbon lets you
invite all of them and then control, precisely, what each one can see and change. This page
shows you how to invite people by email, choose the right **employee type** for each, and
fine-tune **module-level permissions** so everyone has exactly the access they need — and
nothing more.

<Callout type="info">
  **Before you start.** This is a foundation step, best done right after you
  [set up your company](/manual/getting-started/setup-company) and before you
  [migrate your data](/manual/getting-started/migrating-data). You'll need to be an
  **admin** to invite people. If you set up the company yourself, you already are one —
  in Acme's account that's **Chase Foster** (`chase@carbon.ms`).
</Callout>

## The two ideas: who you are, and what you can touch

Access in Carbon comes from two layers that work together. Keep them separate in your head
and the whole thing gets simple.

- A **user** is one login — one person, one email address. Inviting someone creates their
  user account.
- An **employee type** (Carbon's word for a [role](/manual/overview/glossary)) is a named
  bundle of [permissions](/manual/overview/glossary) you attach to a user. Instead of
  setting access person-by-person, you define a type once — say *Sales* — and assign it to
  everyone who sells.
- **Permissions** are the actual switches. They're granted **per module** (Sales,
  Purchasing, Items, Production, and so on) and, within each module, **per action**:
  **view**, **create**, **update**, and **delete**.

<Mermaid

  U[User · one email] --> T[Employee type · a role]

  P --> Up[Update]

So the flow is always the same: invite the **user**, give them an **employee type**, and let
that type's **permissions** decide what they can do in each module. Let's walk through it.

## Invite your team

You add people by sending an email invitation. They click the link, set a password, and land
in your company with whatever access their employee type grants.

## Choose an employee type (the role)

An **employee type** is the role you hand someone. It carries a default set of module
permissions so you don't configure each person from scratch. A new Carbon company starts
with a few sensible types, and you can add your own to match how your shop is organized.

### Suzie's view, in practice

When you give **Suzie Sells** the *Sales* type, Carbon opens up the selling side of the app
for her — customers, [quotes](/manual/selling/quoting) and
[sales orders](/manual/selling/sales-orders) — and leaves the rest read-only or hidden. She
can build a quote like [Q000061](/manual/walkthrough) and turn it into an order, but she
can't change a supplier price or post to the ledger. That's the role doing its job.

## Set module-level permissions

This is the heart of access control. Within an employee type, you turn on four switches for
each module: **View**, **Create**, **Update**, and **Delete**. They stack the way you'd
expect — you can't create something you can't view, and *Delete* is the one you hand out most
sparingly.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the type and find the module grid.** From **Settings → Employee Types**, click a type
(say *Sales*) and open its **Permissions** tab. You'll see one row per module with four
checkboxes each.
</Step>

<Step>
**Tick the actions this role needs.** For a salesperson, you'd give full access to **Sales**,
let them **view** Items so they can pick parts onto a quote, and leave Purchasing,
Production and Accounting off entirely.

Read a column to understand one role; read a row to see who can touch one module. Notice the
**Accountant** can *view* Sales and Purchasing (they need to see the orders behind an
invoice) but only has **full** control over Accounting — and that only the **Administrator**
reaches **Settings**, where users and types live.

<Callout type="warn">
  **Grant least privilege.** Give each person the *minimum* access their job needs, not the
  most. It's tempting to make everyone an Administrator to avoid "access denied" friction —
  don't. Over-broad permissions are how a salesperson accidentally edits a supplier price, or
  an operator deletes a sales order. Start narrow, then add a permission when someone actually
  hits a wall. **Delete** in particular should be rare: most roles never need it, because
  Carbon prefers to *cancel* or *deactivate* records rather than erase the history other
  records depend on.
</Callout>

## A note on AI and API access

Carbon is **API-first** and ships with a built-in [MCP server](/manual/overview/glossary), so
an AI assistant can read and act in your company on your behalf. That access runs through the
same permission system you just set up — an AI agent or an API key inherits the access of the
user it acts as. The least-privilege rule matters even more here: scope a key to exactly the
modules a script or assistant needs. There's a whole section on this in
[Developing with Carbon](/manual/developing/using-the-api).
