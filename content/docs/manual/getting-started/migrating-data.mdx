If you're moving to Carbon from spreadsheets or another system, you don't have to retype
everything by hand. This page shows you **what to bring in, in what order, and how** — using
Carbon's built-in CSV importers. The golden rule of any migration is simple: **import the
things other records depend on first.** Get the order right and the rest falls into place.

We'll use Acme Inc.'s own data as the worked example throughout — the same parts, customers
and suppliers you'll see everywhere else in this manual.

<Callout type="info">
  **Before you start.** This page assumes your company is already set up
  ([Set up your company](/manual/getting-started/setup-company)) and that you've added the
  people who'll be doing the work ([Users & permissions](/manual/getting-started/users-permissions)).
  Migration is the last step of getting started — once your data is in, you're ready to
  [define and use items](/manual/items/defining-an-item) for real.
</Callout>

## Why order matters

Carbon records are linked. An [item](/manual/overview/glossary) points to a
**unit of measure**. A piece of **opening inventory** points to an item *and* a
[location](/manual/overview/glossary). A **bill of materials** points to the parent item
*and* every child item. An **open sales order** points to a customer *and* an item.

So if you import opening stock before the item exists, Carbon has nothing to attach it to and
the row fails. Import items before their units of measure exist, and the `unitOfMeasureCode`
column has nothing to match. The fix is to load your data in **dependency order** — each
layer building on the one below it.

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't have to migrate history. Most shops bring in their **master data** (items,
  customers, suppliers) plus a snapshot of **opening inventory** and any **open orders**, and
  leave closed jobs and paid invoices in the old system. A clean start beats a perfect copy.
</Callout>

## Step 1 — Foundations: units of measure & locations

A **unit of measure** (UoM) is how you count an item — `EA` for "each", `LB` for pounds, `IN`
for inches. A **location** is a physical site that holds stock. These are the codes every
later import leans on, so they go in first. Carbon ships with the common ones already defined,
so usually you're just confirming they exist rather than creating them.

<Callout type="warn">
  Decide your UoM and location **codes** now and never change them later. Every imported row,
  every BOM line and every stock balance refers to these codes by exact text — rename `EA` to
  `ea` afterward and you'll orphan thousands of references. Pick the casing once and stick with it.
</Callout>

## Step 2 — Items (parts, materials, consumables, tools)

[Items](/manual/items/defining-an-item) are the heart of Carbon — a part you make, a material
you buy, a consumable, a tool. Almost everything else points at an item, so this is the
biggest and most important import. Get your spreadsheet clean here and the rest of the
migration is easy.

Each row becomes one item. The columns map to real Carbon fields:

<Callout type="tip">
  Keep your `readableId` values consistent — Acme uses patterns like
  `[abbrev]-[material]-[rev]` for parts (`CWH-A36-15`) and

  (`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`). A predictable scheme makes parts findable and your BOM import
  in Step 5 far easier to wire up.
</Callout>

## Step 4 — Opening inventory

Now that items and locations both exist, you can record what's **physically on your shelves
today** — your opening balances. Each row says: *this item, this quantity, at this location*
(and, if you track bins, which [shelf](/manual/overview/glossary)).

Carbon turns each row into an inventory adjustment that sets the item's on-hand quantity. After
the import, every part with stock shows a real on-hand figure you can build jobs against.

<Callout type="warn">
  Import opening inventory **once**, ideally on go-live day, and pick a clear cutover moment.
  If you import balances and then keep transacting in your old system, the two drift apart fast.
  Freeze the old system, count, import, and run forward in Carbon from there.
</Callout>

## Step 5 — BOMs & methods

A [**method**](/manual/items/methods) is how a made item is built: its **bill of materials**
(what it's made of) plus its **routing** (the operations that make it). Methods only make sense
once the parent and all the child items already exist — which is exactly why they come after
Step 2.

For a made-to-order part like the Counterweight Hook, the method is short: one material, three
operations. Here's how that recipe looks once it's in Carbon.

To import methods by CSV, you typically prepare **two related files** and load them in order:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **BOM lines** — one row per material, linking a parent `readableId` to a child `readableId`
    with a quantity. For the hook: parent `CWH-A36-15`, child `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`, qty
    `40` LB.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Routing operations** — one row per operation, with its sequence, the
    [process](/manual/overview/glossary) (Flame Cut, Sandblasting, Quality Inspection) and the
    [work center](/manual/overview/glossary) it runs at (Burn Table — PA2, Sand Blasting Room,
    Inspection Station), plus times like `45` minutes per piece.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Carbon stitches the two together into the parent item's method. From then on, any
    [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) for that part copies this recipe automatically.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Multi-level assemblies (a part made of sub-assemblies that are themselves made) import the
  same way — just make sure **every** sub-assembly already exists as an item, and import the
  deepest level's BOM first so each parent can find its children.
</Callout>

## Step 6 — Open orders

Last, bring in the work that's still in flight: open **quotes**, **sales orders** awaiting
fulfillment, and **purchase orders** awaiting receipt. These reference items, customers and
suppliers, so by now everything they point at already exists.

## The CSV import workflow

Every importer in Carbon works the same way, so once you've done one you've done them all. The
files in a typical migration look like this — loaded top to bottom:

<Files>
  <Folder name="acme-migration" defaultOpen>
    <File name="01-units-of-measure.csv" />
    <File name="01-locations.csv" />
    <File name="02-items.csv" />
    <File name="03-customers.csv" />
    <File name="03-suppliers.csv" />
    <File name="04-opening-inventory.csv" />
    <File name="05-bom-lines.csv" />
    <File name="05-routing-operations.csv" />
    <File name="06-open-sales-orders.csv" />
    <File name="06-open-purchase-orders.csv" />
  </Folder>
</Files>

To load any one of them:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Open the importer.** Go to the module that owns the data — Items, Sales, Purchasing,
    Inventory — and choose **Import**. Carbon offers a template CSV with the exact column
    headers it expects; download it and pour your data into those columns.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Upload the file.** Drop your `.csv` in. Carbon reads the header row and previews the first
    few rows so you can confirm it parsed the way you expected.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Map columns to Carbon fields.** For each of your columns, pick the Carbon field it feeds —
    your "Part No." to `readableId`, your "UoM" to `unitOfMeasureCode`, your "Make/Buy" to
    `replenishmentSystem`. If your headers already match Carbon's template, the mapping is
    auto-filled.

    <AppShot title="Column mapping" url="app.carbon.ms/x/items/import" caption="Mapping spreadsheet columns to Carbon item fields." flush>
      <DataTable
        columns={[
          { key: "src", label: "Your column", mono: true },
          { key: "arrow", label: "" },
          { key: "field", label: "Carbon field", mono: true },
          { key: "ok", label: "", align: "right" },
        ]}
        rows={[
          { src: "Part No.", arrow: "→", field: "readableId", ok: <StatusBadge tone="green">Mapped</StatusBadge> },
          { src: "Description", arrow: "→", field: "name", ok: <StatusBadge tone="green">Mapped</StatusBadge> },
          { src: "UoM", arrow: "→", field: "unitOfMeasureCode", ok: <StatusBadge tone="green">Mapped</StatusBadge> },
          { src: "Make/Buy", arrow: "→", field: "replenishmentSystem", ok: <StatusBadge tone="green">Mapped</StatusBadge> },
          { src: "Notes", arrow: "→", field: "(skip)", ok: <StatusBadge tone="neutral">Ignored</StatusBadge> },
        ]}
      />
    </AppShot>
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Validate.** Carbon checks every row before changing anything — flagging blank required
    fields, a `unitOfMeasureCode` that doesn't exist yet, or a duplicate `readableId`. Fix the
    flagged rows in your spreadsheet and re-upload.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Import.** When the preview is clean, confirm. Carbon creates the records and shows a
    summary — how many created, how many skipped. Spot-check a few against the app, then move
    on to the next file in the sequence.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  Always run a **small test batch first** — five or ten rows — before importing thousands. A
  tiny run surfaces a wrong column mapping or a bad UoM code in seconds, instead of after
  you've created 5,000 mis-mapped parts. Carbon validates before writing, but a real test
  import is the surest check.
</Callout>

## Large or repeatable migrations: use the API

<Callout type="tip">
  Carbon is **[API-first](/manual/overview/glossary)** — every screen in the app is backed by a
  documented API, and anything you can type in, you can script. For a big migration, a one-time
  data move from a legacy ERP, or a sync you'll run more than once, scripting it against the API
  is far more reliable than hand-mapping CSVs over and over. You control the order, you can
  re-run safely, and you get a clean log of exactly what was created. See
  [Using the API](/manual/developing/using-the-api) to get started.
</Callout>

The same dependency order applies whether you click or script: create units and locations, then
items, then customers and suppliers, then balances, then methods, then open orders. The API
just lets you automate each layer. Because Carbon also ships a built-in
[**MCP server**](/manual/developing/mcp-and-ai), you can even have an AI assistant drive parts of
the migration for you — reading your old data and creating the Carbon records in order.
