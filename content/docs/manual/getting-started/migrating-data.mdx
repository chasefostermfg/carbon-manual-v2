Moving from spreadsheets or another system? You don't retype everything by hand. You import
it with Carbon's built-in **CSV importers**. The one rule: **import the things other records
depend on first.**

We'll use Acme Inc.'s real data as the example throughout.

## Where this lives in Carbon

Each importer lives in the **module that owns the data** — Items, Sales, Purchasing,
Inventory. You open that module, click **Import**, and upload a CSV. Imported parts land in
the **Items** module, on the **Parts** list.

## Import in this order

Carbon records are linked. A part needs a **unit of measure**. Opening stock needs the part
*and* a **location**. A sales order needs a customer *and* an item. So you load the bottom
layer first and build up.

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't have to migrate history. Most shops bring in **master data** (items, customers,
  suppliers) plus a snapshot of **opening inventory** and any **open orders** — and leave closed
  jobs and paid invoices in the old system. A clean start beats a perfect copy.
</Callout>

## 1 — Units & locations

A **unit of measure** (UoM) is how you count an item: `EA` for each, `LB` for pounds. A
**location** is a site that holds stock. Carbon ships with the common UoMs, so usually you're
just confirming they exist.

Acme keeps it small — `EA` for parts, `LB` for raw materials — and runs two sites,
**Headquarters** and **Texas**.

<Callout type="warn">
  Pick your UoM and location **codes** now and never rename them. Every imported row points at
  these codes by exact text — change `EA` to `ea` later and you orphan thousands of references.
</Callout>

## 2 — Items

[Items](/manual/items/defining-an-item) are the heart of Carbon — a part you make, a material
you buy, a tool. This is the biggest import. Each CSV row becomes one item.

Two decisions matter on every row: its `itemType` (part, material, tool?) and its
`replenishmentSystem` — do you **Make** it or **Buy** it?

  Keep `readableId` values consistent. Acme uses `[abbrev]-[material]-[rev]` for parts
  (`CWH-A36-15`) and a longer pattern for materials (`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`). A predictable
  scheme makes parts findable and the BOM import in Step 5 far easier.
</Callout>

Now record what's **on your shelves today**. Each row says: *this item, this quantity, at this
location* (and which [shelf](/manual/overview/glossary), if you track bins).

Each row becomes an inventory adjustment that sets the item's on-hand quantity.

<Callout type="warn">
  Import opening inventory **once**, ideally on go-live day. If you import balances and keep
  transacting in the old system, the two drift apart fast. Freeze, count, import, run forward.
</Callout>

## 5 — BOMs & methods

A [**method**](/manual/items/methods) is how a made item is built: its **bill of materials**
(what it's made of) plus its **routing** (the operations that make it). Methods only work once
the parent and all child items already exist — which is why they come after Step 2.

The Counterweight Hook's method is short: one material, three operations.

<AppShot
  src="/screenshots/manual/item-method.png"
  alt="The Counterweight Hook's Manufacturing view: method tree, bill of material, and 3D model"
  caption="A made part's Manufacturing view — its bill of material (A36 Steel Plate ×40) and routing, set to Make to Order."
/>

You import methods with **two files**, loaded in order:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **BOM lines** — one row per material, linking a parent `readableId` to a child
    `readableId` with a quantity. For the hook: parent `CWH-A36-15`, child
    `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`, qty `40` LB.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Routing operations** — one row per operation, with its sequence, process (Flame Cut,
    Sandblasting, Inspection) and the work center it runs at, plus a time per piece.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Carbon stitches the two into the parent's method. From then on, any
    [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) for that part copies the recipe automatically.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Multi-level assemblies import the same way — just make sure **every** sub-assembly exists as
  an item first, and import the deepest level's BOM before its parents.
</Callout>

## How every importer works

Every importer follows the same five steps, so once you've done one, you've done them all.

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Open the importer.** Go to the module that owns the data — Items, Sales, Purchasing,
    Inventory — and click **Import**. Download Carbon's template CSV and pour your data into
    its columns.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Upload the file.** Drop in your `.csv`. Carbon previews the first rows so you can confirm
    it parsed correctly.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Map columns.** Point each of your columns at a Carbon field — your "Part No." to
    `readableId`, "UoM" to `unitOfMeasureCode`, "Make/Buy" to `replenishmentSystem`. If your
    headers match the template, mapping auto-fills.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Validate.** Carbon checks every row before changing anything — flagging blanks, a missing
    UoM code, or a duplicate `readableId`. Fix flagged rows and re-upload.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Import.** When the preview is clean, confirm. Carbon creates the records and shows a
    summary. Spot-check a few, then move to the next file.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  Always run a **small test batch first** — five or ten rows — before importing thousands. A
  tiny run surfaces a wrong mapping or bad UoM code in seconds, not after you've created 5,000
  mis-mapped parts.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Big or repeatable migration?** Carbon is [API-first](/manual/overview/glossary) — anything
  you can type in, you can script. For a large move or a recurring sync, scripting against the
  API beats hand-mapping CSVs. You can even have an AI assistant drive it through Carbon's
  built-in [MCP server](/manual/developing/mcp-and-ai). See
  [Using the API](/manual/developing/using-the-api).
</Callout>
