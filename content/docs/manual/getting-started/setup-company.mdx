title: "Setting up your company"
description: "Company profile, locations, units of measure and number sequences."

Before you can quote a job or buy a sheet of steel, Carbon needs to know a few things about
*your* business: who you are, where you keep stock, the units you measure things in, how your
documents should be numbered, and when you expect to get paid. This page walks an
administrator through that one-time setup, using the real settings behind **Acme Inc.**, the
example company you'll see throughout this manual. Do this once, up front, and every page
after it will feel natural.

<Callout type="info">
  **Before you start.** This is the very first thing you do in a brand-new Carbon company —
  before you [add users](/manual/getting-started/users-permissions) or
  [import any data](/manual/getting-started/migrating-data). Everything you create later
  (parts, customers, orders) attaches to the company, locations, units and terms you define
  here, so it pays to get them right first. You'll need an
  [**administrator**](/manual/overview/glossary) account to change these settings.
</Callout>

## Where these settings live

All of the foundational settings on this page live in the **Settings** area of Carbon (the
gear icon in the navigation rail). Settings is split into sections — *Company*, *Locations*,
*Units of Measure*, *Sequences*, *Payment Terms*, and more. You don't have to fill in every
section on day one, but the five below are the ones worth doing before anything else.

<Callout type="tip">
  You can come back and change most of these later. The big exception is your **base
  currency** — pick it carefully, because it's the currency your books are kept in and it
  isn't meant to be changed once you start transacting. Acme uses **USD**.
</Callout>

## 1. Company profile

Your **company profile** is your business's identity card. It holds your legal name, address,
logo and base currency. Carbon stamps these onto the documents you send out — quotes, order
confirmations, purchase orders and invoices all carry your name, address and logo — so this is
the first thing to fill in.

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Open Settings → Company.** You'll land on a single form with your company's core
    details.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Enter your name and address.** Acme is "Acme Inc." at 2400 Anvil Way, Springfield, OH.
    This address is what prints in the header of every outbound document.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Upload your logo.** Drop in a PNG or SVG. Carbon places it on PDF documents and in the
    top-left of the app. A transparent-background logo looks best.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Set your base currency.** Acme keeps its books in **USD**. Every record's amounts roll
    up into this currency for reporting. If you sell or buy in other currencies later, Carbon
    converts them back to base using an exchange rate — but the base currency itself stays
    fixed.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Save.** That's the whole profile. You can always return to swap the logo or fix an
    address typo.
  </Step>
</Steps>

  Your **base currency** drives all of accounting. Confirm it before you create your first
  customer, supplier or item. Changing it after you've posted transactions is not a supported
  flow — if you picked wrong, start a fresh company rather than trying to convert in place.
</Callout>

## 2. Locations

A **[location](/manual/overview/glossary)** is a physical site where you make things, hold
stock, or ship from — typically a plant or a warehouse. Carbon tracks inventory, work centers,
and documents *per location*, so most companies set up at least one. Acme runs two:

**Headquarters** is Acme's home base — it's where the burn tables, the sandblasting room and
the inspection station live, and it's the location stamped on the whole Counterweight Hook
thread you'll follow in this manual ([SO000069](/manual/selling/sales-orders),
[J000097](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) and [PO000057](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) all run
out of Headquarters). **Texas** is a second site with its own burn table, laser table and parts
cleaner.

### Shelves and bins live inside a location

Within a location you can get as detailed as you like about *where* stock physically sits, using
**storage units** (think racks, rows, levels and bins). Acme's are named with a
Rack-Row-Level-Bin convention — for example the 1-inch A36 steel plate for the Counterweight
Hook job lives in bin **R02-B01-01**. You don't have to set these up on day one; a single
catch-all bin per location is enough to start, and you can add structure as your warehouse grows.

<Callout type="tip">
  Add a location for every site that holds inventory or ships product. If you only have one
  building, one location is perfectly fine — but don't lump two physically separate warehouses
  into one, or your on-hand quantities won't match reality. More on stock and bins in
  [Locations & stock](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock).
</Callout>

## 3. Units of measure

A **unit of measure** (UoM) is *how you count a thing*: each, pounds, gallons, feet. Every
[item](/manual/overview/glossary) in Carbon has one. The Counterweight Hook is counted in
**EA** (Each); the A36 steel plate it's cut from is measured in **LB** (Pound). Carbon ships
with a sensible starter list, and Acme runs 16 of them.

Acme's full set also includes BG (Bag), CS (Case), DR (Drum), LT (Liter), MT (Meter), OZ
(Ounce), PK (Pack) and YD (Yard). To add your own, open **Settings → Units of Measure** and
give it a short **code** (the abbreviation that shows on documents) and a **name**.

<Callout type="warn">
  Get the unit right on an item *before* you start transacting with it. An item's unit of
  measure feeds into pricing, inventory counts and purchasing — switching it after you've
  recorded stock or orders gets messy. When you buy in one unit and stock in another (say, buy
  steel by the pound but receive it as plates), Carbon handles that with separate purchase and
  inventory units rather than by changing the item's base unit.
</Callout>

## 4. Number sequences

Every document in Carbon gets a short, human-readable ID — `Q000061`, `SO000069`, `J000097`,
`PO000057`, `AR000025`. You don't type these in; Carbon generates them automatically from a
**number sequence**: a prefix plus a running counter. This keeps IDs unique, ordered, and easy
to say out loud on the phone.

Each document type has its own sequence. Here's how Acme's are configured, with the next number
each one will hand out:

A sequence has three parts you can tune:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Prefix** — the letters in front. Acme uses `Q` for quotes, `SO` for sales orders, `J` for
    jobs, `PO` for purchase orders, `AR` for customer (sales) invoices and `AP` for supplier
    (purchase) invoices. These follow common accounting shorthand: **AR** is *accounts
    receivable* (money owed to you), **AP** is *accounts payable* (money you owe).
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Padding** — how many digits, zero-filled. Acme pads to six, so the 69th sales order reads
    `SO000069` rather than `SO69`. Consistent width keeps lists sorting cleanly.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Next number** — the counter that increments by one each time a document is created. Acme's
    most recent quote is `Q000063`, so its next one becomes `Q000064`, and the counter ticks up
    on its own from there.
  </Step>
</Steps>

So when you trace Acme's hero order — quote **Q000061** becomes sales order **SO000069**, which
spawns job **J000097**, which triggers purchase order **PO000057** and finishes with invoice
**AR000025** — every one of those IDs came from a sequence defined right here.

<Callout type="tip">
  Set your prefixes and padding **before** you create your first document. The next-number
  counter only ever goes up, and you generally shouldn't reset it backwards — that risks
  duplicate IDs. Changing a *prefix* mid-stream is fine, but it makes older and newer documents
  look inconsistent, so decide on a scheme early and stick with it.
</Callout>

## 5. Payment terms

**Payment terms** spell out *when* an invoice is due — and sometimes offer a discount for paying
early. You attach a default term to each customer and supplier, and Carbon copies it onto their
orders and invoices, then calculates the due date for you. Acme keeps seven on file:

**Net 30** is Acme's default and the one on the hero thread: it's the term on Radiant
Aerospace's sales order **SO000069**, on the Ryerson purchase order **PO000057**, and on the
invoice **AR000025**. The "Net" terms are straightforward — pay the whole balance within *N*
days. The discount terms like **2% 10 Net 30** are an incentive: a customer who pays within 10
days knocks 2% off, otherwise the full amount is due at 30.

<Callout type="info">
  Payment terms don't have to be set per document by hand. Set one as the company default,
  optionally override it on a specific customer or supplier, and Carbon fills it in
  automatically on every new order and invoice for them. You can still change it on an
  individual document when a deal calls for it.
</Callout>

## A quick mental model

Here's the order these settings stack up in — each layer depends on the one before it:

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  C[Company profile and currency] --> L[Locations and bins]
  L --> U[Units of measure]
  U --> N[Number sequences]
  N --> P[Payment terms]
  P --> R[Ready to add data]`}
/>

Get these five squared away and your company is ready for the next steps: inviting your
team, and bringing in your existing parts, customers and suppliers.

<Callout type="warn">
  **Do this before you import.** [Migrating data](/manual/getting-started/migrating-data)
  attaches everything to the company, locations and units you defined here. If you import parts
  before your units of measure exist, or before your locations are set up, the import has
  nowhere to put them. Foundation first, then data.
</Callout>
