Carbon has a lot of screens, but it's built on a small set of ideas. Learn these eight and
the rest of the manual is just detail. This page defines each one in plain language and shows
it on the same real example you met in the [walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough) — a
**Counterweight Hook** that Acme makes for **Radiant Aerospace**. Read it once, top to
bottom, and the vocabulary you'll see everywhere else in Carbon will already make sense.

<Callout type="info">
  **Before you start.** This is the mental-model page. You don't need to do anything in the
  app yet — just read along. If you'd rather see the whole flow in motion first, the
  [15-minute walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough) follows one order from quote to cash. Either
  order works; this page goes a level deeper on the *why*.
</Callout>

## The big picture

Almost everything in Carbon is a **record** — an item, a quote, a job, a purchase order — and
the records **link to each other**. You enter information once, and it follows the work
everywhere it goes. Here's how the main records connect for our hook:

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  ITEM[Item CWH-A36-15] --> METHOD[Method BOM plus Routing]
  Q[Quote Q000061] --> SO[Sales Order SO000069]
  SO --> JOB[Job J000097]
  METHOD --> JOB
  JOB --> PO[Purchase Order PO000057]
  JOB --> INV[Sales Invoice AR000025]`}
/>

The rest of this page walks each box. Don't worry about memorizing the diagram — by the end
it'll feel obvious.

## 1. The Item

An **[item](/manual/overview/glossary)** is anything you make, buy, store, or sell. It's the
noun the whole system is built around. Every part on a quote, every material on a purchase
order, every line on an invoice points back to an item.

Our hero is the item `CWH-A36-15`, the Counterweight Hook. Two settings on it matter most:

- **Item type** — *what kind of thing it is* (see the six types below).
- **Replenishment** — *how you get it*: you either **make** it or **buy** it (covered in
  section 3).

Every item is exactly one of these types. The type tells Carbon how the item behaves — what

<Callout type="tip">
  Don't overthink the type at first. The two you'll use most are **Part** (the things you
  sell) and **Material** (the raw stock you cut them from). Full detail on every field lives
  in [Defining an item](/manual/items/defining-an-item).
</Callout>

### Unit of measure and tracking

Two more item settings show up constantly:

- **Unit of measure (UoM)** — how you count it. The hook is counted in `EA` (Each); its steel
  is measured in `LB` (pounds). Carbon converts between a purchase UoM and a stock UoM when
  they differ.
- **Tracking type** — whether Carbon keeps a stock balance for it. The hook is *Inventory*
  tracked (Carbon counts how many are on hand). Some items are *Non-Inventory* (bought
  straight to a job and never counted on a shelf).

## 2. The Method — BOM + Routing

A **[method](/manual/overview/glossary)** is the recipe for a *made* item. It has two halves,
and you'll hear both terms constantly:

<Callout type="info">
  **Bill of Materials (BOM)** — *what the item is made of.* The list of materials and
  sub-parts that go into one unit, with quantities. The hook's BOM is one line: 40 lb of A36
  steel plate.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
  **Routing** — *how the item is made.* The ordered list of [operations](/manual/overview/glossary)
  (shop steps), each run at a [work center](/manual/overview/glossary). The hook's routing is
  three operations: Flame Cut, then Sandblasting, then Inspection.
</Callout>

Carbon shows the BOM and routing together as a single **method tree** — materials and
operations nested under the item they build. Here's the hook's method:

<AppShot title="Method · CWH-A36-15" url="app.carbon.ms/x/part/item_2ejw…/manufacturing" caption="One material plus three operations — the complete recipe for one hook." flush>
  <MethodTree
    nodes={[
      {
        kind: "assembly",
        label: "CWH-A36-15",
        detail: "Counterweight Hook — make 1 EA",
        qty: "1 EA",
        children: [
          { kind: "material", label: "A36-STEEL-PLT-1\"", detail: "A36 Steel Plate 1″ — pulled from stock", qty: "40 LB" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "10 · Flame Cut", detail: "Burn Table — PA2", qty: "45 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "20 · Sandblasting", detail: "Sand Blasting Room", qty: "2 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "30 · Inspection", detail: "Inspection Station", qty: "10 min/pc" },
        ],
      },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

Two things to notice, because they explain a lot of Carbon's "magic":

- **The method drives cost and price.** Carbon adds up the material (40 lb of steel) and the
  shop time (45 + 2 + 10 minutes per piece, at each work center's labor and machine rates) to
  estimate what one hook costs to make. That estimate is what you price a quote against.
- **The method drives the plan.** When you make this part, Carbon copies the method onto the
  job — so the shop knows exactly what to consume and which steps to run, in order.

<Callout type="tip">
  A method can nest. If a part is built from sub-assemblies that you *also* make, each one
  brings its own BOM and routing, and the tree grows another level deep. The hook is simple —
  one material, three steps — but the same structure scales to complex assemblies. See
  [Methods: BOM & routing](/manual/items/methods).
</Callout>

## 3. Make vs Buy (replenishment)

**[Replenishment](/manual/overview/glossary)** answers one question for every item: *when you
need this, how do you get more?* There are exactly two answers, and they decide which part of
Carbon does the work.

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "mode", label: "Replenishment" },
    { key: "means", label: "What it means" },
    { key: "needs", label: "What the item needs" },
    { key: "creates", label: "Demand creates a…" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { mode: <Tag>Make</Tag>, means: "You produce it in-house.", needs: "A method (BOM + routing).", creates: "Job" },
    { mode: <Tag>Buy</Tag>, means: "You purchase it from a supplier.", needs: "A supplier and a price.", creates: "Purchase Order" },
  ]}
/>

This is why our two items behave differently:

- The **hook** `CWH-A36-15` is **Make** — so demand for it produces a **job** (section 5).
- Its **steel** `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240` is **Buy** — so demand for it produces a
  **purchase order** (section 7).

A single order can pull both levers at once: making the hook *creates* demand for the steel,
which Carbon then suggests buying. You'll see that chain play out below.

<Callout type="tip">
  Replenishment isn't only Make-vs-Buy — it also governs *when* and *how much* to reorder
  (reorder points, lead time, lot sizing). Those settings live in
  [Replenishment & planning](/manual/items/replenishment).
</Callout>

## 4. Demand — Quote and Sales Order

Everything Acme makes or buys starts with **demand**: someone wants something. On the sell
side, demand comes in two records that link together.

<Callout type="info">
  **Quote** — a *priced offer*, before the customer commits. You add lines (item, quantity,
  price) and send it. Carbon's [configurators](/manual/selling/quoting) can even price
  made-to-order parts from the customer's choices. Our hook was quoted as `Q000061`.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
  **Sales Order (SO)** — the customer's *commitment*. It records who's buying, what, how
  many, when it's due, and at what price. When a quote is accepted you convert it to a sales
  order in one click — no re-typing. Our quote became `SO000069`.
</Callout>

The **fulfillment method** on the line — *Make to Order* — is the hinge between selling and
making. Because the hook is a *Make* item, that setting tells Carbon the next step is a job,
not a pick from the shelf. Learn the sell side in [Quoting](/manual/selling/quoting) and
[Sales orders](/manual/selling/sales-orders).

## 5. The Job — a production order

A **[job](/manual/overview/glossary)** is a *production order*: the instruction to actually
make a quantity of a part. When you release a job, Carbon copies the item's method onto it, so
the job carries its **own** snapshot of the BOM and routing. (That copy is why changing a
part's master method later doesn't rewrite jobs already in progress.)

For our order, Carbon created job `J000097` for 500 hooks. The operations are the routing from
the method; the shop floor reports against them.

right behind this one. Full detail in [Jobs](/manual/manufacturing/jobs); the operator side
is the [shop floor (MES)](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor).

## 6. Supply — the Purchase Order

A job needs materials. The hook's BOM calls for 40 lb of A36 steel per piece — **20,000 lb**
for the batch of 500 — and the steel is a **Buy** item. So Carbon raises supply with a
purchase order.

<Callout type="info">
  **Purchase Order (PO)** — your *commitment to a supplier* to buy specified items at agreed
  prices and dates. It's the buy-side mirror of a sales order. The steel for our hook was
  ordered on `PO000057` from **Ryerson**.
</Callout>

When the steel arrives, a **receipt** adds it to inventory. That single act does two things:
it raises the on-hand balance, and — because A36 plate is batch-tracked — it records exactly
which batch of steel came in. Hold that thought; it's the foundation of traceability in
section 8. Learn the buy side in [Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics).

## 7. Location and Shelf — where stock lives

Physical items have to *be somewhere*. Carbon answers "where?" at two levels of detail:

<Callout type="info">
  **Location** — a site or warehouse. Acme has two: **Headquarters** and **Texas**. Stock,
  work centers, and orders all belong to a location. Everything in our hero thread happens at
  Headquarters.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
  **Shelf** (also called a bin or storage unit) — a specific spot *inside* a location: a rack,
  shelf, or bin. Acme's A36 steel lives on shelf `R02-B01-01`; finished hooks land on shelf
  `8-1-H`.
</Callout>

<AppShot title="Inventory · A36 Steel Plate" url="app.carbon.ms/x/inventory/item_Mqv4…" caption="The same material, located down to the shelf at the Headquarters site.">

## 8. Traceability — the genealogy

The last big idea ties the others together. **[Traceability](/manual/overview/glossary)** (or
*genealogy*) is Carbon's ability to answer, after the fact: *what exactly went into this?* and
*where did this end up?*

It works because of two kinds of tracking you can turn on for an item:

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "kind", label: "Tracking" },
    { key: "what", label: "What it records" },
    { key: "when", label: "When you use it" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { kind: <Tag>Batch</Tag>, what: "A lot number shared by many units made or received together.", when: "Bulk material like steel plate, paint, chemicals." },
    { kind: <Tag>Serial</Tag>, what: "A unique number for each individual unit.", when: "High-value or regulated parts tracked one-by-one." },
  ]}
/>

A36 steel plate is **batch-tracked**. So when Ryerson's steel was received against `PO000057`,
Carbon stamped that incoming lot with a batch number. When job `J000097` consumed the steel,
Carbon linked that *exact batch* to those *exact 500 hooks* — and through the sales order and
shipment, to *Radiant Aerospace*.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  PO[PO000057 Ryerson] --> RCPT[Receipt batch number]
  RCPT --> JOB[Job J000097 consumes steel]
  JOB --> HOOKS[500 hooks]
  HOOKS --> CUST[Radiant Aerospace SO000069]`}
/>

That unbroken chain is worth real money. If Ryerson later reports a defect in that heat of
steel, Acme can answer "which finished parts are affected, and which customer got them?" in
seconds — instead of recalling everything. The same chain runs the other way too: from a
customer complaint back to the raw material. Quality records like inspections and
[non-conformances](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) hang off this genealogy.

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't have to track everything. Turn on batch or serial tracking only for items where it
  earns its keep — regulated, high-value, or warranty-sensitive parts. For everything else,
  plain inventory tracking is enough.
</Callout>
