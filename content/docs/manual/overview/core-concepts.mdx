Carbon has a lot of screens, but only a few ideas. Learn these and the rest is just detail.
We'll show each one on the same real example: a **Counterweight Hook** (`CWH-A36-15`) that
Acme makes for **Radiant Aerospace**.

## Where this lives in Carbon

These ideas span three modules. You'll spend most of your time in:

- **Items** — the things you make, buy, store, and sell, plus how they're made.
- **Sales** — quotes and orders from customers.
- **Production** — the jobs that actually build parts.

The chain that ties them together looks like this:

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  ITEM[Item] --> METHOD[Method]
  Q[Quote] --> SO[Sales Order]
  SO --> JOB[Job]
  METHOD --> JOB
  JOB --> PO[Purchase Order]
  JOB --> INV[Invoice]`}
/>

## 1. Item

An **item** is anything you make, buy, store, or sell. It's the noun everything points to.

Every item is **Make** or **Buy** — that one setting decides how you get more of it:

- **Make** — you build it. It needs a **method** (below) and demand creates a **Job**.
- **Buy** — you order it from a supplier. Demand creates a **Purchase Order**.

Our hook `CWH-A36-15` is a **Make** part. Its steel is a **Buy** material.

<Callout type="tip">
  Items come in six types — **Part**, **Material**, **Tool**, **Consumable**, **Service**,
  **Fixture**. You'll use **Part** and **Material** most. Don't overthink the rest.
</Callout>

## 2. Method (BOM + routing)

A **method** is the recipe for a Make item. It has two halves:

- **Bill of Materials (BOM)** — *what it's made of.* The hook's BOM is one line: **40 LB of
  A36 steel plate**.
- **Routing** — *how it's made.* The hook's routing is three steps: **Flame Cut → Sandblasting
  → Inspection**.

Carbon shows both together as one **method tree** in the **Items** module.

<AppShot
  src="/screenshots/manual/item-method.png"
  alt="The Counterweight Hook's method: a BOM line for A36 steel plate plus a 3D model"
  caption="Items → a part's Manufacturing view. The method tree shows the BOM (A36 Steel Plate) and routing. Note Replenishment = MAKE."

The method earns its keep two ways:

- **It sets cost and price.** Carbon adds up the steel plus the shop time to estimate what one
  hook costs, so you can price a quote.
- **It drives the plan.** When you build the part, Carbon copies the method onto the job.

## 3. Quote and Sales Order

Selling happens in the **Sales** module. Two records carry demand:

- **Quote** — a *priced offer*, before the customer commits. Ours was `Q000061`.
- **Sales Order (SO)** — the customer's *commitment*: who, what, how many, when, and price.
  Accept a quote and it becomes an SO in one click. Ours is `SO000069`.

<AppShot
  src="/screenshots/manual/sales-order-detail.png"
  alt="Sales Order SO000069 for Radiant Aerospace"
  caption="Sales → Orders → SO000069. Radiant Aerospace committed to 500 hooks at $73.14 each — $36,820 total, Paid."
/>

The line says **Make to Order**. Because the hook is a Make item, the next step is a **Job** —
not a pick from the shelf.

## 4. Job

A **job** is a *production order* — the instruction to actually make a quantity. It lives in
the **Production** module.

When you release a job, Carbon copies the item's method onto it. The job keeps its **own** copy
of the BOM and routing — so editing the master method later won't rewrite jobs already running.

For our order, Carbon made job `J000097` for **500 hooks**. The shop floor reports progress
through its operations: **Draft → Ready → In Progress → Completed**.

## 5. Purchase Order

A job needs materials. The hook's BOM calls for 40 LB of steel each — **20,000 LB** for 500 —
and steel is a **Buy** item. So Carbon raises a **Purchase Order (PO)** in the **Purchasing**
module: your commitment to a supplier.

Acme ordered the steel on `PO000057` from **Ryerson**. When it arrives, a **receipt** adds it
to inventory and stamps the incoming batch — which sets up the last idea.

## 6. Traceability

**Traceability** (or genealogy) answers, after the fact: *what went into this, and where did
it end up?* It works through two kinds of tracking you can switch on per item:

- **Batch** — a lot number shared by units made or received together (steel, paint).
- **Serial** — a unique number per individual unit (high-value or regulated parts).

A36 steel is **batch-tracked**. So Carbon links the exact lot received on `PO000057` to the
exact 500 hooks on `J000097`, and through the order to **Radiant Aerospace**.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  PO[PO000057 Ryerson] --> RCPT[Receipt batch]
  RCPT --> JOB[Job J000097]
  JOB --> HOOKS[500 hooks]
  HOOKS --> CUST[Radiant Aerospace]`}
/>

If Ryerson later reports a bad heat of steel, Acme can name the affected parts and customer in
seconds — no blanket recall.

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't have to track everything. Turn on batch or serial only where it pays off —
  regulated, high-value, or warranty-sensitive parts.
</Callout>
