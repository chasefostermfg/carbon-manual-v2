Carbon is software for running a manufacturing business. If you've never used a tool
like this before, here's the short version: Carbon keeps track of everything your shop
does — the orders you take, the parts you make, the materials you buy, and the money that
moves — all in one place. When you write something down once, Carbon carries it everywhere
it's needed, so nobody has to re-type it.

Most shops cobble this together from spreadsheets, a separate accounting program, paper
travelers on the shop floor, and a folder of inspection forms. Each one holds part of the
truth, and none of them talk to each other. Carbon replaces that pile with a single
**system of record** — one trusted place where the real, current state of the business
lives.

## Meet Acme — our running example

Every page in this manual uses one real demo company so the examples stay concrete.
**Acme Inc.** is a metal-fabrication shop in Springfield, OH. It cuts, machines, and
finishes steel and aluminum parts for customers in aerospace, defense, energy, mining,

## Three jobs, one system

People often describe business software with three acronyms — ERP, MES, and QMS. Plenty of
companies buy three separate programs to cover them. Carbon does all three, and the magic
is that they're not three programs bolted together: they're three views of the **same
data**. Here's what each one means in plain language.

### ERP — the business

**ERP** stands for *Enterprise Resource Planning*. Ignore the jargon — it just means the
office side of the business. In Carbon, that's where you:

- Quote a customer and turn the quote into a **sales order** when they say yes.
- Raise **purchase orders** to suppliers for the materials and services you need.
- Track **inventory** — what's on the shelf, where, and how much.
- Roll up **cost** automatically, so you know what a part really costs to make and whether
  the price you quoted leaves a profit.

For Acme, this is the layer that took Radiant Aerospace's order for 500 Counterweight Hooks
(sales order `SO000069`, $36,820) and the layer that bought the steel to make them from
Ryerson (purchase order `PO000057`).

### MES — the shop floor

**MES** stands for *Manufacturing Execution System*. It's the part operators actually use,
out on the floor, to do the work. When the office releases a **job** (a production order to
make a specific quantity of a part), the job appears in the MES app. An operator opens it,
**clocks on** to an operation, and records how many good pieces they finished. That's what
turns a planned order into real parts — and it's what feeds true labor time back into the
cost.

For Acme's hook, the MES side ran three operations in order: **Flame Cut**, then
**Sandblasting**, then **Inspection**. Each was clocked and completed on job `J000097`.

### QMS — quality

**QMS** stands for *Quality Management System*. This is how you make sure each part is right
and can prove it later. In Carbon that means:

- **Inspections** — a quality check, often built right into a job as an operation (notice
  "Inspection" was the last step on the hook).
- **Non-conformances (NCRs)** — a record you raise when something is wrong, so it gets
  reviewed and fixed instead of slipping through.
- **Traceability** (also called *genealogy*) — the linked chain from finished part back to
  the exact batch of raw material it came from. Because Acme's A36 steel is batch-tracked,
  Carbon can answer "which heat of steel went into Radiant's hooks?" in seconds.

## One database underneath

The reason the three sides feel like one tool is simple: they sit on **one database**. A
sales order, the job that fulfills it, the purchase order for its material, the inspection
that passed it, and the invoice that bills it are all linked records pointing at each other.

  This is the single biggest difference from running on spreadsheets. In a spreadsheet world

Because it's all one system, the hand-offs between these roles are automatic. The estimator
doesn't email the order to the floor; the order *becomes* a job the operator can open. The
inspector doesn't keep a separate quality binder; the inspection is a step on that same job.

## Why Carbon is different

Plenty of software does ERP, MES, or QMS. A few things make Carbon stand apart.

<Tabs items={["One connected system", "API-first", "AI-native", "Open source"]}>
  <Tab value="One connected system">
    Everything lives together, so there's **no re-keying** and nothing drifts out of sync.
    A quote becomes a sales order becomes a job becomes a shipment becomes an invoice — each
    one inheriting from the last. The [walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough) follows exactly that
    chain for Acme's hook, from price request to paid invoice, without anyone copying a
    number by hand.
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="API-first">
    An **API** (Application Programming Interface) is a way for other software to read and
    write your data. Carbon is *API-first*: every screen you click is doing the same thing a
    program could do through the API. That means you can connect Carbon to your other tools,
    build custom reports, or automate the boring parts. More in
    [Using the API](/manual/developing/using-the-api).
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="AI-native">
    Carbon ships with a built-in **MCP server** — a standard doorway that lets an AI
    assistant safely operate Carbon for you (look things up, draft a quote, check a job's
    status) in plain language. The same demo data in this manual was pulled through it. See
    [MCP & AI](/manual/developing/mcp-and-ai).
  </Tab>
  <Tab value="Open source">
    Carbon's code is **open source** — anyone can read it, run it, and extend it. You're not
    locked into a black box: you can self-host, audit how it works, and shape it to your
    shop. The integration story continues in
    [Integrating with Carbon](/manual/developing/integrating).
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't need to understand the API or AI features to get full value from Carbon. They're
  there when you're ready. Day one, you can quote, order, make, buy, inspect and invoice
  entirely by clicking — exactly as the rest of this manual shows.
</Callout>

## The one idea to remember

If you take away a single thing: **Carbon is one connected system of record for the whole
business.** ERP runs the office, MES runs the floor, QMS keeps it good — but they're three
windows into the same data, not three programs. Information goes in once and follows the
work from the first quote to the final payment.
