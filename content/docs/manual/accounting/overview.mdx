The shop earns the money; the **Accounting** module counts it. This page covers the two kinds
of invoice and how they get paid — no accounting background needed.

## Where this lives in Carbon

Invoices live in the **Accounting** module. Carbon builds them from what already happened —
what shipped, what you received — so the numbers always match the work.

You can also see an invoice's status right on the document it came from. Sales order
**SO000069** below is **Invoiced** and **Paid** — the same money that became sales invoice
**AR000025**.

## The two kinds of invoice

There are only two, and they mirror each other:

- **Sales invoice (AR)** — bills a **customer** for what you shipped. `AR` means *Accounts
  Receivable*: money owed **to** you.
- **Purchase invoice (AP)** — records a bill a **supplier** sent **you**. `AP` means *Accounts
  Payable*: money **you** owe.

That's it. Acme has 25 sales invoices (`AR000001`–`AR000025`) and 27 purchase invoices
(`AP000001`–`AP000027`).

## Sales invoices — billing the customer

A sales invoice is how you get paid. You rarely type one from scratch — you **create it from a
shipment**, and Carbon copies the customer, parts, quantities and prices off what shipped.

When Acme shipped 500 Counterweight Hooks to **Radiant Aerospace**, that became sales invoice
**AR000025** for **$36,820.00**.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the source.** Go to **Sales Invoices** and click **New**, then pick the shipment (or
sales order) to bill — here, the shipment behind **SO000069**. Carbon pulls in the lines
automatically.
</Step>

<Step>
**Review the lines.** Check the quantities and prices match what shipped. Last chance to catch
a wrong price before the customer sees it.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post it.** Posting assigns the number (`AR000025`) and turns it into a live receivable — money
the customer now owes.
</Step>

<Step>
**Send it.** Email or download the PDF. Carbon stamps the date and starts the payment-terms
clock — here, **Net 30**.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Purchase invoices — recording what you owe

A purchase invoice is the mirror image: the bill a *supplier* sends *you*. When steel arrives
from Ryerson, you enter their bill so the books know what's owed.

The key idea is **matching**. Carbon checks the supplier's bill against two things that already
exist:

- The **purchase order** — the price and quantity you agreed to.
- The **receipt** — what actually showed up on the dock.

If they agree, approve with confidence. If not — they billed for 100 but you got 90 — Carbon
shows the gap *before* you pay.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Start from the PO.** Go to **Purchase Invoices** → **New** and select the purchase order —
for example, Ryerson's A36 steel from **PO000057**. Carbon pre-fills supplier, lines and prices.
</Step>

<Step>
**Match against the receipt.** Confirm the billed quantities match what you received. Carbon
flags any mismatch.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the supplier's bill number.** Type their invoice reference and date — what you'll quote
if you call them about it.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post it.** The invoice moves to **Open**, records the payable, and sets the due date from the
supplier's terms.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Recording payments

An invoice says money *should* move; a **payment** records that it *did*. They're separate
steps on purpose — the gap between them is the time an invoice is "outstanding."

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the invoice** you're settling — say `AR000025`, or a supplier bill like `AP000023`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Record the payment.** Enter the amount, the date, and the method — check, ACH, wire, card.
Radiant paid the full **$36,820.00** on **2026-06-11**.
</Step>

<Step>
**Carbon updates the status.** A full payment flips it to **Paid**; a partial payment leaves a
balance. The cash entry posts to the books automatically.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Payment terms drive the due date.** Each invoice carries a payment term that Carbon uses to
  set when payment is due. **Net 30** is Acme's default. Terms flow down from the customer or
  supplier record onto every order and invoice, so you set them once.
</Callout>

## It all posts to the books for you

Every posted invoice and payment writes entries to the **chart of accounts** — the numbered
buckets the business tracks money in (cash, receivables, payables, sales, cost of goods). Acme's
chart has **93 accounts**, and you never post to them by hand.

When Acme posts `AR000025`, two things move at once so the books always balance:

<StatGrid cols={2}>
  <Stat label="Accounts Receivable (1110)" value="+ $36,820" hint="Radiant now owes Acme" />
  <Stat label="Sales (4010)" value="+ $36,570" hint="parts revenue earned; freight posts separately" />
</StatGrid>

When Radiant pays, the $36,820 moves out of **Accounts Receivable** and into **Bank - Cash
(1010)**. On the buying side it's the reverse.

<Callout type="info">
  **You don't need to memorize any of this.** Every shipment, receipt and payment lands in the
  financials automatically and correctly — no spreadsheet reconciliation, no double data entry.
</Callout>

## Already using QuickBooks?

Keep it. Carbon can **sync** the invoices and payments it generates out to **QuickBooks** so
your accountant stays in the tool they know. Carbon owns *operations* (what shipped, what was
received); QuickBooks owns the *financials*.

<Callout type="warn">
  **Pick your books-of-record up front.** Choose *one* financial source of truth — Carbon or
  QuickBooks — and set up the integration before posting real invoices. See [Integrating with
  Carbon](/manual/developing/integrating).
</Callout>
