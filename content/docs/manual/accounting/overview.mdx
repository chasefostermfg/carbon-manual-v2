Manufacturing is where the money is *earned*; accounting is where it's *counted*. This page
covers the finance side of Carbon — the **invoices** that bill customers and record what you
owe suppliers, the **payments** that settle them, and the **chart of accounts** that quietly
keeps score in the background. You don't need to be an accountant to follow along. The core
idea is simple: when work happens in the shop, Carbon writes the matching numbers to the
books for you, so the financials always match reality.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Accounting is the *last* stop in the order-to-cash chain you've been
  following: a [shipment](/manual/selling/sales-to-shipment) goes out, then a **sales
  invoice** bills for it. On the buying side, a [receipt](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics)
  comes in, then a **purchase invoice** records what you owe. This page picks up right where
  those two leave off.
</Callout>

## The two kinds of invoice

There are only two flavors of invoice in Carbon, and they mirror each other:

- A **sales invoice** (the "AR" in `AR000025` stands for *Accounts Receivable* — money owed
  **to** you) bills a customer for what you shipped them.
- A **purchase invoice** (the "AP" stands for *Accounts Payable* — money **you** owe) records
  the bill a supplier sends you for what they delivered.

[Accounts Receivable](/manual/overview/glossary) and [Accounts
Payable](/manual/overview/glossary) are just the bookkeeping names for "money coming in" and
"money going out." Acme has 25 sales invoices (`AR000001` through `AR000025`) and 27 purchase
invoices (`AP000001` through `AP000027`) on file.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  SH[Shipment to customer] --> AR[Sales Invoice AR]

The thing that makes Carbon different from a standalone accounting app: the invoice doesn't
start from a blank page. It's built from what already happened in the system — what shipped,
what was received — so the numbers can't drift apart from the work.

## Sales invoices — billing the customer

A **sales invoice** is how you get paid. In Carbon you rarely type one from scratch; you
**create it from a shipment**. The invoice copies the customer, the parts, the quantities and
the prices straight off what went out the door, so the bill always matches the delivery.

Follow the hero thread to see it: when Acme shipped 500 Counterweight Hooks to **Radiant
Aerospace**, that shipment became sales invoice **AR000025**.

### Creating and sending a sales invoice

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the source.** Go to **Sales** → **Sales Invoices**, click **New**, and pick the
shipment (or sales order) to bill. For the hero thread that's the shipment behind
**SO000069**. Carbon pulls in the customer, the line for 500 `CWH-A36-15`, the $73.14 price
and the $250 shipping automatically — nothing is re-keyed.
</Step>

<Step>
**Review the lines.** Check the quantities and prices match what actually shipped. This is
your last chance to catch a wrong price or a partial shipment before the customer sees it.
You can add a one-off charge (like a rush fee) as an extra line if needed.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post it.** Posting changes the invoice from **Draft** to a live receivable and writes the
accounting entries (more on that below). The invoice number — `AR000025` — is assigned, and
the amount now counts as money the customer owes you.
</Step>

<Step>
**Send it.** Email the PDF to the customer or download it to send yourself. Carbon stamps the
**date issued** and starts the payment-terms clock — here, **Net 30**, meaning payment is due
30 days out.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Purchase invoices — recording what you owe

A **purchase invoice** is the mirror image: it's the bill a *supplier* sends *you*. When the
steel arrives from Ryerson, Ryerson emails Acme a bill, and Acme enters it as a purchase
invoice so the books know money is owed and when it's due.

The key idea here is **matching**. A purchase invoice is checked against two things that
already exist in Carbon:

1. The **purchase order** — the price and quantity you agreed to.
2. The **receipt** — what actually showed up on the dock.

If the supplier's bill agrees with both, you approve it with confidence. If it doesn't —
they billed for 100 but you only received 90, or the price crept up — Carbon shows the
mismatch so you can query it *before* you pay. This is the classic **three-way match** that
stops a shop from overpaying.

<Callout type="info">
  **Tying back to the PO.** Acme's purchase order **PO000057** to Ryerson was for A36 steel
  plate at $0.25/LB. When that steel is received and Ryerson's bill comes in, the matching
  purchase invoice records the payable against that same PO — keeping buying and accounting
  in lockstep. See [Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) for the receiving
  side.
</Callout>

### Entering a purchase invoice

<Steps>

<Step>
**Start from the PO or receipt.** Go to **Purchasing** → **Purchase Invoices** → **New** and
select the purchase order the bill relates to. Carbon pre-fills the supplier, lines and
agreed prices — for example, Ryerson's A36 steel from **PO000057**.
</Step>

<Step>
**Match against the receipt.** Confirm the billed quantities match what you actually
received. Carbon highlights any gap between *ordered*, *received* and *billed* so a mismatch
can't slip through unnoticed.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the supplier's bill number.** Type the supplier's own invoice reference and the date
they issued it. This is what you'll quote if you ever need to call them about the bill.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post it.** Posting moves the invoice to **Open**, records the payable, and sets the due
date from the supplier's payment terms. It's now a live bill waiting to be paid.
</Step>

</Steps>

## Recording payments

An invoice tells you money *should* move; a **payment** records that it *did*. Posting an
invoice and paying it are two separate steps on purpose — that gap is exactly the period an
invoice is "outstanding."

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the invoice** you're settling — say `AR000025`, or a supplier bill like `AP000023`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Record the payment.** Enter the amount received (or paid), the date, and the method —
check, ACH, wire, card. For AR000025, Radiant paid the full $36,820.00 on **2026-06-11**, the
same day it was issued.
</Step>

<Step>
**Carbon applies it and updates the status.** A full payment flips the invoice to **Paid**
and clears its balance to zero. A partial payment leaves a remaining balance and (on the
payables side) shows as **Partially Paid**. The matching cash entry posts to the books
automatically.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Payment terms drive the due date.** Each invoice carries a [payment
  term](/manual/overview/glossary) that Carbon uses to calculate when payment is due. Acme has
  seven configured — **Net 30** is the default — plus early-payment discounts like **2% 10 Net
  30** (take 2% off if you pay within 10 days) and **COD**. Terms flow down from the customer
  or supplier record onto every order and invoice, so you set them once.
</Callout>

## How posting flows to the chart of accounts

Here's the part that makes a non-accountant nervous — and it shouldn't. The **chart of
accounts** is just a numbered list of buckets the business tracks money in: cash, what
customers owe, what you owe suppliers, sales, cost of goods, and so on. Acme's chart has
**93 accounts**. You don't post to them by hand. When you post an invoice or a payment,
Carbon writes the entries to the right buckets for you, following rules set up once during
[company setup](/manual/getting-started/setup-company).

So what actually happens when Acme posts `AR000025`? In plain language, two things move at
once — that's the "double entry" idea, and it's nothing more than *every change has a matching
side so the books always balance*:

<StatGrid cols={2}>
  <Stat label="Accounts Receivable (1110)" value="+ $36,820" hint="Radiant now owes Acme" />
  <Stat label="Sales (4010)" value="+ $36,570" hint="parts revenue earned (freight posts separately)" />
</StatGrid>

When Radiant pays, a second pair of entries moves the $36,820 out of **Accounts Receivable**
and into **Bank - Cash (1010)** — the receivable is cleared and the cash is real. On the
buying side it's the reverse: posting `AP000023` increases **Accounts Payable (2010)** and
inventory or expense; paying it draws down cash.

<Callout type="info">
  **You don't need to memorize any of this.** The point is that every shipment, receipt and
  payment lands in the financial statements *automatically and correctly*. Your accountant
  can open the books at month-end and trust that the numbers tie back to real shop activity —
  no spreadsheet reconciliation, no double data entry.
</Callout>

## Integrating with QuickBooks

Plenty of shops already run their books in **QuickBooks** (or another accounting package) and
want to keep doing so. Carbon supports that. Rather than forcing you to abandon your existing
accounting system, Carbon can **sync** the financial documents it generates — invoices,
payments, and the accounts behind them — out to QuickBooks. For the step-by-step setup, see
[Integrating with Carbon](/manual/developing/integrating).

<Tabs items={["Carbon as the books", "Carbon + QuickBooks"]}>

<Tab value="Carbon as the books">
Use Carbon's built-in chart of accounts as your system of record. Everything posts in one
place, and your financial statements come straight out of Carbon. Best when you want a single
tool from shop floor to balance sheet.
</Tab>

<Tab value="Carbon + QuickBooks">
Keep QuickBooks as the books of record and let Carbon **push** sales and purchase invoices
across so your accountant works in the tool they know. Carbon stays the source of truth for
*operations* (what shipped, what was received); QuickBooks owns the *financials*. Map Carbon's
accounts to your QuickBooks accounts once, and the sync keeps them aligned.
</Tab>

</Tabs>

<Callout type="warn">
  **Decide your books-of-record up front.** Pick *one* place to be the financial source of
  truth — Carbon or your accounting package — and configure the integration before you start
  posting real invoices. Switching mid-stream means reconciling two sets of numbers. Because
  Carbon is [API-first](/manual/developing/using-the-api), other accounting systems can be
  integrated the same way; QuickBooks is just the most common.
</Callout>
