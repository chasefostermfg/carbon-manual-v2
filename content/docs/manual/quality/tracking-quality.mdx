The **Quality** module keeps bad parts from leaving the building — and lets you *prove* later that what you shipped was good. It has four jobs: **inspections**, **non-conformances** (NCRs), **gauges**, and **traceability**.

## Where this lives in Carbon

Open the **Quality** module from the home screen. The dashboard shows your open work and a sub-nav for **Issues**, **Gauges**, **Records**, and **Inspections**.

You don't have to use all four tools to start. Many shops begin with one inspection on their most important part and grow from there.

## Inspections: a gate inside the job

In Carbon an **inspection** isn't a separate thing to set up — it's just an **operation** in a routing, run at an inspection work center. The hero Counterweight Hook shows it: its [method](/manual/items/methods) ends with an **Inspection** operation, so no part ships before it's checked.

How it runs on the shop floor:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Clock onto the inspection operation.** In the [shop floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor), the inspector opens the job, taps the **Inspection** operation, and clocks on — like any other step.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Check parts against the spec** using calibrated gauges. The procedure says what to measure.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Record good and scrap counts.** Good pieces become **quantity complete**; failures become **scrap** with a reason.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Raise an NCR for failures.** Bad parts trigger a non-conformance (next section), and the job can be held so they can't ship by mistake.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Want first-article or incoming inspection instead of a final check? Put the inspection operation **first** in the routing. Same mechanism — only the position changes.
</Callout>

## Non-conformances (NCRs): record what went wrong

An **NCR** — also called an **Issue** in Carbon — is the record you open when something is off-spec: a bad part, a bad batch of steel, a customer complaint. It captures what happened, how bad it is, what you did, and how you proved the fix worked.

**Two settings shape every NCR:**

- **Type** — the cause, so you can spot patterns later (e.g. three *Material Issue* NCRs all from one supplier). Acme ships with ten types like *Manufacturing Defect*, *Material Issue*, and *Customer Complaint*.
- **Workflow** — the playbook, matched to severity. It lists the required actions you must finish before closing.

<Callout type="info">
  **Corrective vs preventive.** A **corrective action** fixes the parts in front of you. A **preventive action** changes the process so it can't happen again. Heavier workflows make you do both — and Carbon won't let you close the NCR until every required action is checked off.
</Callout>

### Raise an NCR

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Start from the problem.** Open the failing operation, or go to **Issues → New**. Starting from the operation pre-fills the job, part, and work center.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Pick the type and workflow.** Choose the cause (e.g. **Manufacturing Defect**) and the right playbook (**CAPA** for a recurring defect, **8D** if parts reached the customer).
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Describe and quantify.** Write what you saw, attach a photo, and record the **non-conforming quantity** with a **scrap reason** if you're scrapping.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Assign and work the actions.** Each required action is a checklist item with an owner and status.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Close it.** Once every action is done — and MRB has signed off if the workflow needs it — close the NCR. It stays linked to the job and part forever.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  Don't reach for **8D** on every scratch. Over-classifying buries your team and hides what truly matters. Most shop-floor defects are a 5 Why 2 How; save 8D for problems that reached the customer or carry safety risk.
</Callout>

## Gauges and calibration: trust your measurements

An inspection is only as good as the tool doing the measuring. A **gauge** is any measuring instrument — a micrometer, height gauge, or thread plug. Gauges drift over time, so each one is **calibrated** on a schedule. Open **Gauges** from the Quality sub-nav.

Acme registers ten gauges (`G00001`–`G00010`), each on a six-month interval. Carbon computes the next-due date and flips overdue tools to **Out of Calibration** on its own:

To keep gauges current:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Register the gauge** in **Gauges → New** — a number, description, type, and home location.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Set the calibration interval** (Acme uses six months). Carbon uses it to compute the next-due date.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Log each calibration.** Carbon rolls the due date forward and flips the status back to **In Calibration**.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Work the overdue list.** Filter by **Out of Calibration** to see what needs attention this week.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  An **out-of-calibration gauge taints every measurement made with it.** If `G00003` was used to accept parts after its 2026-03-03 due date, you can't trust those results — which can itself become a *Testing Failure* NCR.
</Callout>

## Traceability: prove what went where

**Traceability** (or **genealogy**) follows a part backward and forward: which raw material went in, which job made it, which customer it shipped to. The enabler is **tracking** set on the item:

- **Batch tracking** groups material by lot. A36 Steel Plate has batch tracking on, so every receipt records the **heat** of steel from the mill.
- **Serial tracking** gives each finished unit its own number — for parts a customer needs to trace one by one.

Because every record links to the next, you can read the chain either way:

- **Backward (recall):** "A bad heat shipped — which orders?" Follow the batch forward to `J000097`, then `SO000069`, and you land on **Radiant Aerospace** in seconds.
- **Forward (provenance):** "Radiant wants the material certs." Walk back from the shipment through the job to the exact heat of A36 steel and the Ryerson PO.

<Callout type="tip">
  Turn on tracking *before* you receive material. Set batch (or serial) tracking on the [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) up front, so every receipt, job, and shipment records the lot automatically.
</Callout>
