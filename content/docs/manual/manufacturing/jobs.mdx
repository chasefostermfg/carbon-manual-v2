A **job** is a production order: Carbon's instruction to the shop floor to *make* a
specific quantity of a part by a specific date. If a [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders)
is the promise you made to a customer, the job is how you keep it. This page follows one
real job — `J000097`, where Acme makes **500 Counterweight Hooks** for Radiant Aerospace —
so you can see exactly how a job is born, what it carries, and how you turn it loose on the
shop floor.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** A job sits in the middle of the end-to-end flow:
  Quote → Sales Order → **Job** → Purchasing → Quality → Shipment → Invoice. The job we follow here
  comes straight from [sales order SO000069](/manual/selling/sales-orders) and feeds
  [purchase order PO000057](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) (the steel it needs).
  Once it's released, operators run it in the [shop-floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor),
  and you track its day-to-day progress in [managing production](/manual/manufacturing/managing-production).
</Callout>

## What a job is

When you make something rather than buy it, that work has to be planned, tracked, costed
and finished. A job is the record that holds all of it together. A single job answers:

- **What** are we making? (the part — here, `CWH-A36-15`, the Counterweight Hook)
- **How many?** (the production quantity — 500 each)
- **By when?** (the due date — 2026-06-26)
- **How is it made?** (the [method](/manual/items/methods) — a recipe of materials and steps)
- **Where are we?** (the status, and how many good pieces are done)

The big idea: **a job carries its own copy of the method.** When the job is created, Carbon
*snapshots* the part's bill of materials and routing onto the job. From that moment the job
is self-contained — even if someone later edits the part's master method, this job keeps the
recipe it was created with. That's what lets you trust a finished job's cost and
[traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) months later.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Method = BOM + Routing.** The [**bill of materials (BOM)**](/manual/overview/glossary)
  is *what the part is made of*; the **routing** is *the ordered steps to make it*. A job
  copies both. We cover authoring methods in depth in
  [Methods: BOM & routing](/manual/items/methods).
</Callout>

## How a job gets created

There are two ways a job comes into being. Most jobs at Acme are created the first way,
without anyone lifting a finger.

### 1. Automatically, from a Make-to-Order sales order line

When a sales order line is set to **Make to Order** — meaning "don't ship this from stock,
make it fresh for this order" — Carbon offers to spin up a job for that line directly from
the sales order. The job is pre-filled with the part, the quantity, the customer and the due
date from the order, and it stays linked back to the line it came from.

This is exactly how `J000097` was born. Look at the source line on `SO000069`:

<Callout type="info">
  The same automatic link happens for the other two Radiant orders for this part:
  `SO000070` created job `J000098` (1,500 EA) and `SO000071` created job `J000099`
  (3,000 EA). One part, three orders, three independent jobs.
</Callout>

### 2. Manually, when you need a job on its own

Sometimes you want a job that *isn't* tied to a customer order — for example, building parts
to stock, a rework batch, or a one-off prototype. In that case you create the job yourself.
Acme's job `J000094` (10,000 Thermal Exhaust Nozzles, built to stock) is a manual job like
this; it has no sales order behind it.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the Jobs list and start a new job.** Go to the **Jobs** area under the production
module. You'll see every job in the system — Acme has 89 of them, `J000001` through
`J000099`. Click **New Job**.
</Step>

<Step>
**Pick the part.** Choose the [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) you want to make — for
example `CWH-A36-15`, the Counterweight Hook. Carbon checks that the part is a *Make* item
(its `replenishmentSystem` is **Make**, not **Buy**) and pulls in its description and unit of
measure (EA).
</Step>

<Step>
**Set the quantity, due date and priority.** Enter how many to make, when they're needed,
and how urgent the job is relative to others. We'll look at each of these fields next.
</Step>

<Step>
**Save.** Carbon assigns the next job number and snapshots the part's method onto the new
job. The job opens in the **Planned** status, ready for you to review before you release it.
</Step>

</Steps>

## The fields that drive a job

When you open `J000097` you land on its **Details** tab. A handful of fields do most of the
work; everything else on the shop floor flows from them.

      <Field label="Location" value="Headquarters" />

    </FieldGrid>
  </RecordPanel>

**Production quantity** is how many good pieces you intend to finish — here, 500. If you
expect to lose some to scrap, you can also set a separate scrap quantity so Carbon plans
enough material; `J000097` ran with a scrap quantity of 0.

**Due date** is when the parts must be done. The **deadline type** tells Carbon how to treat
that date: a **Hard Deadline** (used here) is a firm commitment, versus a soft target. This
feeds the scheduling and the at-a-glance "is this job late?" view in
[managing production](/manual/manufacturing/managing-production).

**Priority** ranks this job against every other open job competing for the same machines and
people. `J000097` is priority **1** — the highest — so it jumps the queue at the burn table.
Lower-priority jobs wait their turn.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Location matters.** A job is made at one [location](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock).
  `J000097` runs at **Headquarters**, so it can only use Headquarters work centers (like
  Burn Table — PA2) and pulls material from Headquarters stock. Acme's other location, Texas,
  has its own burn tables and laser tables.
</Callout>

## The method the job carries

Click the **Method** tab and you'll see the recipe Carbon copied onto the job. For the
Counterweight Hook it's short: one material, three operations. The root node is the part
itself; everything indented under it is what goes into making it.

<AppShot title="Job · J000097 — Method" url="app.carbon.ms/x/job/job_8m3YTfc2oBy8xkdJX3wWD8/method" caption="The job's own copy of the BOM + routing." flush>
  <MethodTree
    nodes={[
      {
        kind: "assembly",
        label: "CWH-A36-15",
        detail: "Counterweight Hook · Make to Order",
        qty: "500 EA",
        children: [
          { kind: "material", label: "A36-STEEL-PLT-1\"", detail: "A36 Steel Plate 1″ · pulled from inventory", qty: "40 LB/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "10 · Flame Cut", detail: "Burn Table — PA2", qty: "45 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "20 · Sandblasting", detail: "Sand Blasting Room", qty: "2 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "30 · Inspection", detail: "Inspection Station", qty: "10 min/pc" },
        ],
      },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

Two things to notice. First, the BOM has a single material line: **40 LB** of A36 steel
plate *per piece*. Multiply by 500 and the job needs **20,000 LB** of steel — Carbon does
that math for you. Second, the material is set to **Pull from Inventory**, meaning the job
expects to draw the steel from stock; the [purchase order to Ryerson](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics)
exists to make sure that stock is there.

<Callout type="info">
  Because the job carries its *own* copy of this method, you can tweak it for this run only —
  swap a material, add a deburring step, change a time — without touching the part's master
  method or any other job. Edits here affect `J000097` and nothing else.
</Callout>

### Operations: the steps, in order

The **Operations** tab lists the routing as a sequence. Each operation runs at one **work
center** (a specific machine or station) and has a time per piece. Operations run in order —
each one here is set to start **After Previous**, so Sandblasting can't begin until Flame Cut
is done.

<AppShot title="Job · J000097 — Operations" url="app.carbon.ms/x/job/job_8m3YTfc2oBy8xkdJX3wWD8/operations" caption="Three operations, run in sequence on the shop floor." flush>

      { key: "wc", label: "Work center" },

      { key: "labor", label: "Labor time", align: "right" },
      { key: "done", label: "Complete", align: "right" },
      { key: "status", label: "Status", align: "right" },

      { seq: "20", op: "Sandblasting", wc: "Sand Blasting Room", type: <Tag>Inside</Tag>, labor: "2 min/pc", done: "500 / 500", status: <StatusBadge tone="green">Done</StatusBadge> },
      { seq: "30", op: "Inspection", wc: "Inspection Station", type: <Tag>Inside</Tag>, labor: "10 min/pc", done: "500 / 500", status: <StatusBadge tone="green">Done</StatusBadge> },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

Every operation here is **Inside** (done in Acme's own shop) rather than outside
(subcontracted). The labor time is *per piece*, so Carbon can estimate the total: 45 minutes
of flame cutting × 500 pieces is the bulk of the work, with a quick sandblast and a final
inspection gate at the end. As operators clock on and report finished pieces in the
[shop-floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor), the **Complete** and **Status** columns
fill in — that's how all three reached *Done* and the whole job rolled up its real recorded
cost.

<Callout type="tip">
  The last operation, **Inspection**, is a quality step. Putting an inspection at the end of
  the routing makes it a gate: the parts aren't considered finished until they pass. See
  [Tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).
</Callout>

## Releasing a job to the floor

A new job starts life as **Planned** — a draft you can still edit freely. Nothing reaches the
shop floor until you **release** it. Releasing is the deliberate "go" that says *this job is
real, schedule it and let operators start clocking on*.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Review the job.** Confirm the quantity, due date and priority on **Details**, and skim the
**Method**, **Operations** and **Materials** tabs to make sure the recipe is right for this
run.
</Step>

<Step>
**Check material availability.** Carbon flags any shortages. For `J000097`, the 20,000 LB of
A36 steel wasn't all on hand, which is why Acme raised
[purchase order PO000057](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) to Ryerson. Buying and making can
run in parallel — you don't have to wait for steel to release the job.
</Step>

<Step>
**Release the job.** Click **Release**. Carbon stamps the released date — for `J000097` that
was 2026-06-11 — moves the job into the active queue, and makes its operations available in
the [shop-floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor).
</Step>

<Step>
**The floor takes over.** Operators clock onto each operation in sequence and report finished
pieces. When the last operation's good quantity reaches the production quantity, the job is
**Completed** — `J000097` completed the same day, with all 500 pieces done and later shipped.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  **A Planned job does nothing on its own.** It won't appear on the shop floor and operators
  can't book time to it until you **release** it. If a job seems "stuck" with no activity,
  the first thing to check is whether it's still in *Planned*.
</Callout>

## Where the numbers go next

Radiant Aerospace, and billed on [invoice AR000025](/manual/accounting/overview). The job's

what you *planned* and what it *actually* took.
