This is where planned work gets **done**. An operator picks an operation, clocks on, records good and scrap counts, and clocks off. Every tap feeds real labor time and cost back to the [job](/manual/overview/glossary) — no clipboards.

## Where this lives in Carbon

The shop floor is the **Shop Floor** module, delivered as its own app: the **MES** (Manufacturing Execution System).

- The **ERP** is the full office app — Sales, Purchasing, Items, **Production**. Jobs are planned and costed there.
- The **MES** is a stripped-down, touch-friendly app for a shop-floor tablet. It shows an operator only what they need: the operations waiting at their [work center](/manual/overview/glossary), the instructions, and big **Start**, **Pause** and **Finish** buttons.

Both read the same job, so you never enter anything twice. A manager watches it all from the **Production** module in the ERP.

<Callout type="tip">
  Operators open the MES at its own address (think `mes.carbon.ms`) and sign in with their own account. A worker who only runs machines never has to learn the full ERP.
</Callout>

We'll follow one real job: **J000097** — 500 **Counterweight Hooks** (`CWH-A36-15`) for Radiant Aerospace. It runs three operations in order: **Flame Cut → Sandblasting → Inspection**.

## What an operator sees

The MES shows only the operations queued at **the operator's work center** — not the whole factory. Each row is one operation on one job. Fred Flame works the burn tables, so his queue leads with the Flame Cut on J000097.

Two things to know:

- The queue filters by **work center**, not by job. Fred sees Flame Cut work from several jobs at once. An operator at the Sand Blasting Room sees a different list.
- An operation turns **Ready** only after the step before it finishes. So a job's three operations light up one at a time, in order.

## Run an operation, start to finish

Fred runs the Flame Cut on J000097: 500 hooks, on Burn Table - PA2.

<Steps>

<Step>
### Open the operation

Tap the **Flame Cut** row. The MES shows an *operation card* — what to make, how many, from what, and how.

- The **Materials** tab shows what to pull: **40 LB** of *A36 Steel Plate 1″* per piece, from bin **R02-B01-01**.
- The **Tools** tab lists any tooling the step needs.

Because the steel is batch-tracked, the operator picks which batch they used — that's what makes [traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) possible later.
</Step>

<Step>
### Read the standard procedure

Most operations have a **procedure** attached — Carbon's name for a standard work instruction. The operator ticks off each step as they go.

Steps aren't only checkboxes. A step can also ask the operator to **type a measurement** (with a min and max, so bad values are caught on the spot), **pick from a list**, or **attach a photo**. A laminated sheet becomes recorded, checkable data.

<Callout type="tip">
  Procedures are **versioned**. Operators always run the Active version; managers can revise a draft without disrupting the floor. Build them in the ERP's Production module.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Clock ON

Tap **Start**. This **clocks the operator ON** and starts a timer. From here, every minute counts against the job — at Burn Table - PA2's rate of **$50/hour labor**, that time is becoming real cost on J000097.

Tap **Pause** to step away — the timer stops, so breaks aren't billed to the job.

<Callout type="warn">
  An operator can only be clocked on to **one operation at a time**. If Fred forgets to clock off before lunch, his time keeps running. Habit: **Pause** when you step away, **Finish** when the step is done. A manager can fix a bad entry later in the ERP, but it's cleaner to get it right on the floor.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Record good and scrap

As pieces come off the table, the operator reports two numbers:

- **Good** — pieces that passed and move to the next operation.
- **Scrap** — pieces that failed. Each scrap needs a **reason** (Acme uses **Damaged**, **Defective**, **Quality**) so the shop can see *why* parts are lost.

On J000097 every hook was good — **500 made, 0 scrapped**. When scrap does happen, reasons roll up into reports, and repeated failures can feed the [quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) side of Carbon as a non-conformance (NCR).
</Step>

<Step>
### Clock OFF and complete

When the count is in, the operator taps **Finish**. This **clocks them off**, stops the timer, and marks the operation **Done**. The good quantity is now available to the next operation — so when Fred finishes Flame Cut, **Sandblasting flips to Ready** in the blast-room queue.

</Step>

## What clocking off feeds back

Each clock-on/clock-off pair is a **production event** — a timestamped record of *who* worked *which* operation, *how long*, and *how many* good and scrap pieces resulted. These events do three jobs at once.

<StatGrid cols={3}>
  <Stat label="Good pieces" value="500" hint="across 3 operations" />
  <Stat label="Scrap" value="0" hint="nothing lost" />
  <Stat label="Status" value="Completed" hint="J000097" />
</StatGrid>

1. **They cost the job.** Recorded minutes times the work center's labor and machine rates post to the job as *actual* cost — so Carbon can compare estimated vs. real cost.
2. **They advance the work.** Reported good quantity flows to the next operation.
3. **They build the record.** Who ran each step and when is stored for good — for traceability, payroll, and answering "what happened on this job?" later.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  A[Clock ON] --> B[Follow procedure]
  B --> C[Record good and scrap]
  C --> D[Clock OFF Done]
  D --> E[Production event]
  E --> F[Cost on job]
  E --> G[Next operation Ready]`}
/>

### The last operation finishes the job

When the inspector clocks off the final **Inspection** operation, all three steps are Done and all 500 pieces are made. Carbon rolls the job up: the finished hooks are received into inventory, actual cost is locked in, and the job moves to **Completed**.

That's the hand-off back to the office: a Completed job means the parts exist, ready to ship against [sales order SO000069](/manual/selling/sales-to-shipment). The shop floor did its part, and every minute it spent is now visible to the rest of Carbon.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Why clock time at all?** Guessed labor is the most common reason a shop loses money without knowing it. When operators clock real time on the MES, your next quote rests on what work *actually* takes — not a hopeful estimate.
</Callout>
