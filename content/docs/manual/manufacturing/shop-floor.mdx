So far the manual has planned the work — quoting it, ordering it, releasing a [**job**](/manual/overview/glossary).
This page is where the work actually gets *done*. It covers Carbon's **MES** — the
*Manufacturing Execution System* — the part of Carbon a machine operator opens at a station
on the shop floor. The MES turns a planned job into real activity: an operator picks an
operation, **clocks on**, follows the written instructions, records how many good and scrap
pieces they made, and **clocks off**. Every one of those taps feeds real labor time and cost
back to the job — no clipboards, no end-of-day data entry.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** A [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) is *planned* by a scheduler and
  *run* by operators. [Managing production](/manual/manufacturing/managing-production) is the
  manager's view — releasing and tracking jobs. **This page is the operator's view**: the MES
  is the screen on the floor where the hours actually get logged. The last operation here is
  usually a quality gate, which leads into [tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).
</Callout>

## The MES is its own app

Carbon ships as two front ends that share one database:

- The **ERP** — the full office app you've seen on every other page (Sales, Purchasing,
  Items, Production). This is where jobs are planned, scheduled and costed.
- The **MES** — a stripped-down, touch-friendly app built for a shop-floor tablet or kiosk.
  It shows an operator only what they need: the operations waiting at their
  [work center](/manual/overview/glossary), the instructions for each, and big buttons to
  start, pause and finish.

Both look at the same job. When an operator finishes an operation in the MES, a manager sees
it update in the ERP a moment later. You don't enter anything twice.

<Callout type="tip">
  Operators reach the MES at its own address (think `mes.carbon.ms`) and sign in with their
  own account. A worker who only runs machines never needs to learn the full ERP — they live
  entirely in this one focused screen. Permissions are covered in
  [Users & permissions](/manual/getting-started/users-permissions).
</Callout>

We'll follow the same real job as the rest of the manual: **J000097**, the batch of 500
**Counterweight Hooks** (`CWH-A36-15`) for Radiant Aerospace. It runs three operations in
order — Flame Cut, Sandblasting, then Inspection — and Carbon recorded the whole thing
finishing in about **282 seconds** of logged shop time.

## What an operator sees

When an operator signs in, the MES doesn't show them the whole factory. It shows the
**operations queued at the work center(s) they're assigned to**, newest deadlines first. Each
row is one operation on one job. Operator Fred Flame works the burn tables, so his queue is led
by the Flame Cut on J000097.

A couple of things to notice:

- The MES filters by **work center**, not by job. Fred sees Flame Cut work from several
  different jobs together, because they all run on his Burn Table. A different operator at the
  Sand Blasting Room (here, Carlos Axis) sees a completely different list.
- An operation only becomes **Ready** once the operation before it is done (the routing on
  J000097 says each step runs *After Previous*). Sandblasting won't show as Ready in the
  blast-room queue until Flame Cut is finished. This is why the same job's three operations
  light up one at a time, in sequence.

<Callout type="info">
  Carbon defines an [**operation**](/manual/overview/glossary) as one step in a job's
  *routing*, performed at one *work center*. J000097 has three: Flame Cut at **Burn Table -
  PA2**, Sandblasting at the **Sand Blasting Room**, and Inspection at the **Inspection
  Station**. Each has a planned time per piece, which is what Carbon expects the operation to
  take.
</Callout>

## Run an operation, start to finish

Here's the full loop an operator does for one operation. Fred will run the Flame Cut on
J000097: 500 hooks, 45 minutes planned per piece, on Burn Table - PA2.

<Steps>

<Step>
### Open the operation

Tap the **Flame Cut** row for **J000097**. The MES opens an *operation card* — everything for
this one step on one screen. It tells the operator what to make, how many, from what, and how.

    status={<StatusBadge tone="blue">Ready</StatusBadge>}
    tabs={["Instructions", "Materials", "Tools", "Activity"]}
    activeTab="Instructions"
  >
    <FieldGrid cols={3}>
      <Field label="Quantity to make" value="500 EA" />
      <Field label="Planned time" value="45 min / piece" />
      <Field label="Work center" value="Burn Table - PA2" />
    </FieldGrid>
  </RecordPanel>
</AppShot>

The **Materials** tab shows what to pull and from where — for this part, **40 LB** of
*A36 Steel Plate 1″* per piece, drawn from bin **R02-B01-01**. The **Tools** tab lists any
tooling or fixtures the step needs. Because the steel is batch-tracked, the operator will be
asked which batch they consumed — that's what makes full
[traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) possible later.
</Step>

<Step>
### Read the standard procedure

Most operations have a **procedure** attached — Carbon's name for a standard work instruction.
Acme keeps **12 procedures**, one for each kind of process, so the same job done by two
different operators is done the same way. The procedure for a Flame Cut / CNC-style operation
walks through setup and run as a checklist the operator ticks off:

<AppShot title="MES — Procedure: 3-Axis CNC - Standard Procedure" url="mes.carbon.ms/x/operation/jo_Bf5EXUYE…/procedure" caption="A procedure is a checklist of steps. The operator checks each one off as they go." flush>
  <DataTable
    columns={[
      { key: "n", label: "#", align: "right" },
      { key: "step", label: "Step" },
      { key: "type", label: "Type" },
      { key: "done", label: "", align: "right" },
    ]}
    rows={[
      { n: "1", step: "Load Tools and Workpiece", type: <Tag>Checkbox</Tag>, done: <StatusBadge tone="green">Done</StatusBadge> },
      { n: "2", step: "Set offsets", type: <Tag>Checkbox</Tag>, done: <StatusBadge tone="green">Done</StatusBadge> },
      { n: "3", step: "Load the Program", type: <Tag>Checkbox</Tag>, done: <StatusBadge tone="green">Done</StatusBadge> },
      { n: "4", step: "Run a Simulation", type: <Tag>Checkbox</Tag>, done: <StatusBadge tone="amber">In progress</StatusBadge> },
      { n: "5", step: "Execute", type: <Tag>Checkbox</Tag>, done: <StatusBadge tone="neutral">To do</StatusBadge> },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

Steps aren't only checkboxes. A procedure step can also ask the operator to **type a
measurement** (with an allowed minimum and maximum, so an out-of-range value is caught on the
spot), **pick from a list**, or **attach a photo or file**. That's how a written instruction
becomes recorded, checkable data instead of a laminated sheet on the wall.

<Callout type="tip">
  Procedures are **versioned**. Acme's *Quality Inspection* procedure, for example, has an
  Active version 1 and a newer Draft version 2 — operators always run the Active version, and
  managers can revise the draft without disrupting the floor. Build and manage them from the
  ERP's Production module.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Clock ON

When the operator is ready to cut, they tap **Start**. This is the moment that matters: it
**clocks them ON** to the operation and Carbon starts a timer. From here, every minute counts
against this job — at Burn Table - PA2's labor rate of **$50/hour**, that time is becoming real
cost on J000097 as the operator works.

<AppShot title="MES — Flame Cut · clocked on" url="mes.carbon.ms/x/operation/jo_Bf5EXUYE…/run">

    title="Flame Cut — J000097"

    tabs={["Run", "Instructions", "Materials"]}

    <FieldGrid cols={3}>
      <Field label="Elapsed" value="00:38:12" mono />

      <Field label="Scrap so far" value="0" />

If the operator stops for a break or to switch tasks, they tap **Pause** — the timer stops, so
breaks don't get billed to the job. They can clock off one operation and onto another at any
time; Carbon keeps each operation's time separate.

<Callout type="warn">
  An operator can only be clocked on to **one operation at a time**. If Fred forgets to clock
  off Flame Cut before lunch, his time keeps running against J000097. Encourage the habit:
  **Pause** when you step away, **Finish** when the step is done. A manager can correct a
  bad time entry later from the ERP, but it's far cleaner to get it right on the floor.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Record good and scrap quantities

As pieces come off the table, the operator reports them. Carbon tracks two numbers:

- **Good quantity** — pieces that passed and move on to the next operation.
- **Scrap quantity** — pieces that failed and won't continue. Each scrap is tagged with a
  **reason** so the shop can see *why* parts are being lost.

Acme keeps three scrap reasons — **Damaged**, **Defective**, and **Quality** — and the
operator picks one whenever they scrap a piece.

<AppShot title="MES — Report quantities" url="mes.carbon.ms/x/operation/jo_Bf5EXUYE…/report" caption="The operator enters good and scrap counts; scrap requires a reason." flush>
  <DataTable
    columns={[

      { field: "Scrap quantity", value: "0", note: "—" },
      { field: "Scrap reason", value: "—", note: "Damaged · Defective · Quality" },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

On J000097 every hook was good — **500 made, 0 scrapped** — so the job's `scrapQuantity` stayed
at zero. When scrap *does* happen, those reasons roll up into reports so a manager can spot, say,
a run of *Defective* parts at one work center and dig into the cause.

<Callout type="info">
  Scrapping a piece does more than count it. It can also feed the
  [quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) side of Carbon — repeated failures of the same
  kind are exactly what a *non-conformance* (NCR) is for. Scrap reasons and failure modes are
  the raw material for quality analysis.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Clock OFF and complete the operation

When the count is in and the work is finished, the operator taps **Finish**. This **clocks them
off**, stops the timer, and marks the operation **Done**. The finished good quantity is now
available to the *next* operation — so the moment Fred finishes Flame Cut, Sandblasting flips
to **Ready** in the blast-room queue.

The other two operators do the same thing in turn. Carlos Axis runs the Sandblasting operation
(2 min/piece) at the Sand Blasting Room and clocks off; then Terry Tolerance, the inspector at
the Inspection Station, runs the **Quality Inspection** procedure (10 min/piece) and clocks off
the last operation.
</Step>

</Steps>

## What clocking off feeds back

Each clock-on/clock-off pair is a **production event** — a timestamped record of *who* worked
*which* operation, for *how long*, and *how many* good and scrap pieces resulted. These events
are the heart of the MES. They do three jobs at once:

<StatGrid cols={3}>
  <Stat label="Labor time" value="~282 s" hint="total recorded on J000097" />
  <Stat label="Good pieces" value="500" hint="across 3 operations" />
  <Stat label="Scrap" value="0" hint="nothing lost" />
</StatGrid>

1. **They cost the job.** Recorded minutes are multiplied by the work center's labor and
   machine rates (Burn Table - PA2 bills **$50/hr labor + $25/hr machine**) and posted to the
   job as *actual* cost. That's how Carbon compares what a job was *estimated* to cost against
   what it *really* cost — and the gap shows up as a variance you can act on.
2. **They advance the work.** Reported good quantity flows to the next operation; the routing's
   *After Previous* rule means the floor naturally runs steps in order.
3. **They build the record.** Who ran each step and when is stored for good — useful for
   [traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality), payroll, and answering "what happened on
   this job?" months later.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  A[Operator clocks ON] --> B[Follow procedure]
  B --> C[Record good and scrap]
  C --> D[Clock OFF operation Done]
  D --> E[Production event]
  E --> F[Labor time and cost on job]
  E --> G[Next operation Ready]`}
/>

### Finishing the last operation finishes the job

The operations run in sequence, and the **last** one is special. When the inspector clocks off
the final **Inspection** operation on J000097, there's nothing left to do — all three
operations are Done and all 500 pieces are made. Carbon rolls the job up: the finished hooks are
received into inventory, the job's actual cost is locked in from the production events, and the
job moves to **Completed**.

This is the hand-off back to the office: a Completed job means the parts exist, ready to be
picked and shipped against [sales order SO000069](/manual/selling/sales-to-shipment) and billed
on [invoice AR000025](/manual/accounting/overview). The shop floor did its part, and every
minute it spent is now visible to the rest of Carbon.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Why clock time at all?** Because guessed labor is the most common reason a shop loses money
  without knowing it. When operators clock real time on the MES, every quote you build next
  rests on what work *actually* takes — not a hopeful estimate. The MES is where Carbon's costing
  gets honest.
</Callout>
