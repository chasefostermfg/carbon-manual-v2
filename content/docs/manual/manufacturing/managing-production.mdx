A single job is easy to follow. The real challenge starts when you have dozens of them at
once — some waiting on steel, some halfway through the shop, all competing for the same
machines. This page is about that bird's-eye view: how Carbon turns customer demand into a
plan, tells you what to **make** and what to **buy**, schedules the work across your
machines, and shows you — at a glance — whether everything is on track.

Acme runs 89 jobs through **19 work centers** and **16 processes**, so we'll use its real
shop to show how it all hangs together.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** A [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders) creates demand, and a
  [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) is how you satisfy it. This page sits *between* the two:
  it's the planning and oversight layer that decides **which** jobs to run, **when**, and
  **on which machine** — then watches them finish. Once a job hits the floor, operators run
  it in the [shop floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor).
</Callout>

## The big picture: demand in, plan out

Every manufacturing system has to answer one question over and over: *given everything
customers have ordered, what do we need to do today?* Carbon answers it by comparing
**demand** against **supply**.

- **Demand** is everything that needs material or capacity: open sales orders, the materials
  jobs will consume, safety-stock targets.
- **Supply** is everything that will satisfy demand: stock on hand, open purchase orders
  already on their way, and jobs already in progress.

Where demand outruns supply, Carbon raises a [**suggestion**](/manual/overview/glossary).
For a part you **buy**, that's a suggestion to raise a [purchase
order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics). For a part you **make**, it's a suggestion to
create a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs). This demand-vs-supply engine is what most ERP
systems call **MRP** (Material Requirements Planning) — you don't have to call it that, but
it's the idea behind the planning screen.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  D[Demand: sales orders and job materials] --> P[Planning compares demand vs supply]
  S[Supply: stock, open POs, jobs in progress] --> P
  P --> Buy[Shortage on a Buy item becomes a purchase suggestion]
  P --> Make[Shortage on a Make item becomes a make suggestion]
  Buy --> PO[Purchase Order]
  Make --> J[Job]`}
/>

Walk the Counterweight Hook through it. The order [SO000069](/manual/selling/sales-orders)
needs 500 hooks, each hook needs **40 LB** of A36 steel plate, so the demand is **20,000 LB**
of steel. Acme doesn't keep that much on the shelf — so Carbon sees the shortage and suggests
buying it. That suggestion became purchase order
[PO000057 to Ryerson](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics). Meanwhile, because the hook is a
**Make** part, the order itself became make-suggestion → job **J000097**. One screen, two
kinds of action.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Make vs Buy** is a setting on every [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) — its
  `replenishmentSystem`. The Counterweight Hook is *Make*; its A36 steel plate is *Buy*.
  That one flag is what decides whether a shortage turns into a job or a purchase order.
  See [Replenishment](/manual/items/replenishment) for how to set it.
</Callout>

## The planning view

Open **Production → Planning** to see demand and supply side by side, one row per item that
needs attention. Each row shows what's required, what's available, the resulting shortage,
and the action Carbon suggests. You review the suggestions, adjust quantities or dates if you
need to, and then turn the ones you accept into real jobs and purchase orders.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Set your planning horizon.** At the top of the screen, choose how far ahead to look — a
common starting point is the next few weeks. Carbon only raises suggestions for demand that
falls inside the horizon, so a longer horizon surfaces more (and earlier) suggestions.
</Step>

<Step>
**Read each row.** *Demand* is everything pulling on the item; *Supply* is everything already
covering it. When *Short* is greater than zero, Carbon proposes an action in the
**Suggestion** column. For the A36 steel plate, demand of 20,000 LB with nothing on hand
produces a *Buy → PO* suggestion.
</Step>

<Step>
**Adjust before you commit.** You can change a suggested quantity (for example, round a steel
buy up to a full sheet) or pull a date earlier. Acme's PO000057 ended up at **54,001 LB** —
more than the 20,000 LB this one job needed — because the buyer rounded up to cover the next
two Radiant orders too.
</Step>

<Step>
**Convert the ones you accept.** Select the suggestions you're happy with and convert them:
*Buy* suggestions become draft [purchase orders](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics), *Make*
suggestions become [jobs](/manual/manufacturing/jobs). Carbon links the new record back to the
demand that created it, so the trail stays intact.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  A suggestion is a *proposal*, never an automatic action. Carbon will not raise a PO or
  release a job on its own — you always review and confirm. That's deliberate: planning
  should inform your decisions, not make them for you.
</Callout>

## Scheduling jobs across work centers

Once jobs exist, the next question is *when does each one run, and on which machine?* In
Carbon, every [operation](/manual/overview/glossary) in a job's routing points at a
[**work center**](/manual/overview/glossary) — a specific machine or station that performs a
[**process**](/manual/overview/glossary). Acme's shop has 19 of them; here are the ones the
Counterweight Hook touches, plus a few others to show the range.

Why this matters for scheduling: each operation carries a **time** (Flame Cut is 45 min per
piece, Sandblasting 2 min, Inspection 10 min), and operations run **After Previous** — step 20
can't start until step 10 finishes. Multiply the per-piece time by the job quantity and Carbon
knows how long each operation occupies its work center. Lay those out across the calendar and
you have a schedule.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Find the bottleneck.** Some work centers are in higher demand than others. Acme has three
Burn Tables (PA1, PA2, TX1) for Flame Cut but only one Sand Blasting Room — so sandblasting is
the constraint everything flame-cut has to squeeze through. The schedule view shows each work
center's load so you can spot where work is piling up.
</Step>

<Step>
**Balance the load.** When a process has more than one work center, you can move an operation
to a less-busy machine. Flame Cut work, for instance, can run on Burn Table PA1, PA2 *or* the
Texas table — so a backlog at PA2 can shift to PA1 without changing the method.
</Step>

<Step>
**Respect the sequence.** Because operations are *After Previous*, scheduling step 20 earlier
does nothing if step 10 hasn't finished. Carbon keeps operations in order automatically; your
job is to decide where the *whole chain* fits relative to other jobs.
</Step>

<Step>
**Watch the location.** Work centers belong to a [location](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock)
— Acme's Headquarters or Texas. A job at Headquarters schedules onto Headquarters work centers.
J000097 ran entirely at Headquarters (`loc_CZUGEFrKS1CCvUruMgY9CA`).
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Work-center rates aren't just for scheduling — they're how Carbon costs your jobs. Every
  minute booked against Burn Table PA2 absorbs labor at $50/hr and machine time at $25/hr.
  That's why J000097's tiny per-piece cost rolled up from *recorded shop time*, not a guess.
  More on costing in [Accounting overview](/manual/accounting/overview).
</Callout>

## Prioritizing what runs first

When more work wants a machine than the machine can handle, something has to go first. Carbon
gives every job a **priority** (a number — lower means more urgent) and a **due date** with a
**deadline type**: a *Hard Deadline* can't slip, a *Soft Deadline* has some give. Together with
status, these tell you — and the scheduler — what to push to the front.

Here's Acme's live Counterweight Hook backlog. Three jobs for the same part, the same customer,
stacked up:

Read that as a plan: J000097 finished first (earliest due date), J000098 is running now, and
J000099 is staged and *Ready* to release once capacity frees up. Because all three flow through
the single Sand Blasting Room, sequencing them by due date keeps that bottleneck from becoming
a missed deadline.

<Callout type="warn">
  A job has to be **released** before the shop floor can touch it. The statuses you'll see
  walk in order: *Draft* → *Planned* → *Ready* → *In Progress* → *Completed*. *Ready* means
  "everything's lined up, release it when you want it to start" — J000099 above is sitting at
  *Ready*. Releasing is covered on the [Jobs](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) page.
</Callout>

## Tracking progress across the shop

The payoff of all this planning is a single place to answer "how are we doing?" The jobs list
is your production dashboard: filter by status, location or due date, and read completion at a
glance. The numbers update themselves as operators record finished pieces in the
[shop floor app](/manual/manufacturing/shop-floor) — you never type a percentage by hand.

A few things to read off this screen:

- **Completion is real, not estimated.** J000097 shows 500 of 500 because operators clocked
  off all 500 pieces. The *In Progress* jobs show a partial count that climbs on its own as the
  floor reports finished pieces — you never type a number in by hand.
- **Status tells you where to look.** *In Progress* jobs are on the floor right now —
  J000095 and J000098 are where today's attention goes. *Planned* and *Ready* jobs aren't
  consuming capacity yet.
- **Due dates flag risk.** J000095 is due **2026-04-23** — already in the past — while it's
  still *In Progress*. A row whose due date has slipped behind today is your cue to investigate
  before it turns into a missed commitment.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Filter to what matters.** Narrow the list to *In Progress* at *Headquarters* to see only
the work happening in front of you right now. Save the filter and it becomes your daily view.
</Step>

<Step>
**Drill into a job.** Click any row to open the [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs). Its
**Operations** tab shows which step is live and which work center it's on; the **Materials**
tab shows whether the parts it needs are on hand.
</Step>

<Step>
**Trace a shortage back.** If a job is stalled waiting on material, follow the link to the
[purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) covering it — for J000097 that's PO000057
to Ryerson — and check its expected receipt date.
</Step>

<Step>
**Re-plan when things change.** New orders arrive, dates move, a job scraps a batch. Re-run
the planning screen and Carbon recomputes demand vs supply against the latest reality,
surfacing any *new* shortages as fresh suggestions.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="info">
  **The loop, in one sentence.** Demand creates suggestions, you turn suggestions into jobs
  and purchase orders, you schedule those jobs across work centers, operators run them, and
  the dashboard reflects progress in real time — then new demand restarts the loop. Planning
  isn't a one-time setup; it's the rhythm of running the shop.
</Callout>
