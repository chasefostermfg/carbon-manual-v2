A **supplier** (also called a *vendor*) is any company you buy from — the steel mill that
sells you plate, the electronics distributor that ships you resistors, the anodizer that
finishes your parts. Before you can raise a [purchase order](/manual/overview/glossary),
Carbon needs to know who the supplier is, how to reach them, where to send the order, and
how you've agreed to pay. This page walks you through creating a supplier and filling in
those details, using Acme's real vendor list as the example.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Suppliers are the foundation of the whole **buying** flow. Once a
  supplier exists, you can raise purchase orders against it
  ([Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics)), receive what they send, and pay
  their invoices. In the [hero thread](/manual/walkthrough), Acme buys A36 steel plate from
  **Ryerson** on purchase order `PO000057` — and that only works because Ryerson was set up
  as a supplier first.
</Callout>

## What a supplier record holds

A supplier in Carbon is a small folder of related information. You don't have to fill it all
in at once — a name is enough to start — but the more you add, the less you re-type later.

Carbon gives every supplier a short **readable ID** like `SUP000013` so you can refer to it
quickly, while still showing the friendly name everywhere. You'll see both throughout the app.

## Supplier types and statuses

Two small fields shape how a supplier behaves in the app.

**Supplier type** is a label you assign so you can group and filter vendors. It's purely
organizational — it doesn't change pricing or accounting. Acme uses five types:

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "type", label: "Supplier type" },
    { key: "example", label: "Example vendors" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { type: <Tag>Metals and Raw Materials</Tag>, example: "Ryerson, Olympic Steel, MetalMart, Reliance Steel" },
    { type: <Tag>Electronics and Components</Tag>, example: "DigiKey, Arrow Electronics" },
    { type: <Tag>Hardware and Fasteners</Tag>, example: "Fastenal, McMaster-Carr" },
    { type: <Tag>Maintenance, Repair, and Operations</Tag>, example: "Grainger, MSC Industrial" },
    { type: <Tag>Subcontractors and Service Providers</Tag>, example: "Sendcutsend, Allen's Anodizing, Zebra Painters" },
  ]}
/>

**Status** controls whether you can buy from a vendor right now. An **Active** supplier is
available everywhere you pick a vendor; an **Inactive** one is hidden from new purchase
orders but kept for history. This is how you retire a supplier you no longer use without
deleting years of records.

<Callout type="tip">
  You define the list of supplier *types* yourself in **Settings**, so you can make them
  match how your shop thinks about its vendors. There's no fixed list — these five are just
  the ones Acme chose.
</Callout>

## Create a supplier

Let's add a new vendor from scratch. We'll use **Ryerson**, Acme's steel mill, as the
worked example.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Open the new-supplier form

Go to **Purchasing → Suppliers** and click **New**. Carbon opens a short form. The only
field you *must* fill in is the **Name** — everything else can wait.

</Step>

<Step>

### Fill in the core details

Give the supplier a name, pick its **type**, and confirm the **currency** you'll trade in.
Carbon defaults the currency to your company's base currency (USD for Acme); change it only
if you pay this vendor in another currency.

<AppShot title="New Supplier" url="app.carbon.ms/x/supplier/new" caption="Creating the Ryerson supplier record.">
  <FieldGrid cols={2}>
    <Field label="Name" value="Ryerson" />
    <Field label="Supplier type" value="Metals and Raw Materials" />
    <Field label="Currency" value="USD" />
    <Field label="Status" value="Active" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>

Click **Save**. Carbon assigns the next ID (`SUP000013` for Ryerson) and opens the full
record, where the remaining tabs — Contacts, Locations, Payment, and more — become available.

</Step>

<Step>

### Add a contact

A **contact** is a person at the supplier you actually deal with — a salesperson who quotes
you steel, or an accounts-receivable clerk you send payment questions to. Open the
**Contacts** tab and click **New** to add one with a name, email, and phone.

The contact you mark as the **purchasing contact** is the one Carbon emails when you send a
purchase order, so make sure it's the person who takes orders. A supplier can have as many
contacts as you like — keep the right people on file and you never have to dig through old
emails for an address.

</Step>

<Step>

### Add a location

A **location** is a supplier address — typically where they ship from, or where you send
remittance. Open the **Locations** tab, click **New**, and enter the address. If a vendor
ships from several plants, add one location per site and pick the right one on each purchase
order.

<Callout type="tip">
  For Acme, Ryerson and most vendors have a single location, so Carbon fills it in
  automatically on every purchase order. You only have to choose when a supplier has more
  than one.
</Callout>

</Step>

<Step>

### Set payment terms

**Payment terms** describe *when* you've agreed to pay — for example, **Net 30** means the
invoice is due 30 days after it's issued. Setting a default here means every new purchase
order and supplier invoice starts with the right terms, so your accounting team isn't
guessing.

Open the **Payment** tab and choose from the terms your company has defined:

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "term", label: "Payment term" },
    { key: "meaning", label: "What it means" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { term: "COD", meaning: "Cash on delivery — pay when the goods arrive" },
    { term: "Net 15", meaning: "Due 15 days after the invoice date" },
    { term: "Net 30", meaning: "Due 30 days after the invoice date" },
    { term: "Net 60", meaning: "Due 60 days after the invoice date" },
    { term: "2% 10 Net 30", meaning: "2% discount if paid within 10 days, otherwise due in 30" },
    { term: "Net EOM 10th", meaning: "Due the 10th of the month following the invoice" },
  ]}
/>

Acme buys from Ryerson on **Net 30** — the same terms that carry onto purchase order
`PO000057` in the hero thread.

</Step>

<Step>

### Link supplier part numbers

Your vendor almost never calls a part by *your* part number. Ryerson has its own catalog
number for the steel plate that Acme knows as `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`. A **supplier part**
links the two: it records the supplier's part number and their price for one of *your*
[items](/manual/overview/glossary), so the right number prints on the purchase order and the
price fills in automatically.

You can add supplier parts from either side:

- **From the supplier**, on the **Supplier Parts** tab — useful when onboarding a vendor's
  full catalog.
- **From the item**, on the item's **Suppliers** / **Purchasing** tab — useful when you want
  to know "who can sell me this, and for how much?"

For each link you record the supplier, their part number, the price (and optional
**price breaks** for larger quantities), and the supplier's unit of measure. With that in
place, picking Ryerson on a purchase order line for A36 plate auto-fills the `$0.25/LB`
price Acme negotiated.

</Step>

</Steps>

Once a supplier is this complete, raising a purchase order is mostly point-and-click: pick
Ryerson, pick the material, and Carbon already knows the contact to email, the address to
ship from, the terms to bill on, and the price to expect.

<Callout type="warn">
  Don't create a second supplier record for a vendor you already have — for example a
  separate "Ryerson Inc." alongside "Ryerson." Duplicates split your purchase history and
  spending across two records and make reporting unreliable. Search the supplier list first;
  if the vendor exists, reuse it.
</Callout>

## How a purchase order pulls it together

Every supplier field you fill in shows up downstream. When Acme raises `PO000057`, the
supplier record does the work:

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  S[Supplier Ryerson] --> C[Contact emailed]
  S --> L[Ship-from location]
  S --> T[Payment terms Net 30]
  S --> P[Supplier part price]
  C --> PO[Purchase Order PO000057]
  L --> PO
  T --> PO
  P --> PO`}
/>

That's the payoff for setting suppliers up carefully: the data you enter once flows into
every order, receipt, and payment that follows.
