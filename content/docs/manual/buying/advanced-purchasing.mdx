[Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) covered the everyday loop: raise a PO,
send it, receive the goods. This page covers the trickier buying jobs:

- Send a part out for a coating (**outside processing**).
- Shop several suppliers for the best price (**RFQs**).
- Ship straight to your customer (**drop-ship**).
- Let Carbon tell you what to buy (**planning**).

## Where this lives in Carbon

Everything here lives in the **Purchasing** module. Open it from the home screen, then use the
sub-nav for **Purchase Orders**, **Supplier Quotes**, and **Planning**.

## Outside processing

Some steps aren't done in-house. A part comes off your mill in great shape, but still needs
**anodizing** or **painting**. You send it to a specialist, they do the work, and they ship it
back. That round trip is **outside processing**.

In Carbon, it's just an **operation** in a part's [routing](/manual/overview/glossary) — like
Flame Cut or Inspection. The only difference: its type is **Outside**, not **Inside**.

- **Inside** — runs at your own [work center](/manual/overview/glossary) and costs you shop time.
- **Outside** — runs at a **supplier** and costs you what the supplier charges.

Acme sends parts to two outside suppliers, both set as **Subcontractors and Service Providers**:

### Set up and run an outside operation

<Steps>

<Step>
**Add the operation and set its type to Outside.** Open the part's **Manufacturing** tab, add
the operation where the service belongs, and choose **Outside** as the type.
</Step>

<Step>
**Point it at a supplier and a price.** Pick the supplier — **Allen's Anodizing** — and enter
what they charge. That price rolls up into the part's cost, just like in-house labor.
</Step>

<Step>
**Release the job.** The outside operation appears in the job's operation list with status
**Todo**, in sequence between the inside steps.
</Step>

<Step>
**Raise a PO for the service.** When production reaches the outside step, create a
[purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) to the supplier. Carbon links it back to
the job, so it knows which parts left the building and why.

</Step>

<Step>
**Ship out, then receive back.** Send the parts to the supplier. When they return finished,
record a [receipt](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving) against the PO. That marks the outside
operation **complete** and the job moves to the next step.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  **Outside parts are still your inventory.** While parts are at the anodizer, they're
  work-in-progress that's temporarily off-site. Don't close the job until you've received them
  back, or your job costs and on-hand counts will be wrong.
</Callout>

## Supplier quotes (RFQs)

Before you buy something expensive, you usually want more than one price. A **Request for Quote
(RFQ)** does that: describe what you want, send it to several suppliers, collect their
**supplier quotes**, compare, then convert the winner to a PO — no re-typing.

A supplier quote is the buying-side mirror of a [sales quote](/manual/selling/quoting). Carbon
makes one per supplier you ask.

Example: Acme needs a big lot of 1″ A36 steel plate. The RFQ goes to four suppliers at once —
**Ryerson**, **Olympic Steel**, **Reliance Steel**, and **MetalMart**.

### Send an RFQ and compare the replies

<Steps>

<Step>
**Create the RFQ and add what you want priced.** In the **Purchasing** module, create a new RFQ
and add a line per item — here, one line for **A36 Steel Plate 1″** (`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`).
</Step>

<Step>
**Choose the suppliers and send.** Add the four suppliers as recipients. Carbon makes a supplier
quote for each and sends it — by email or through the [supplier portal](#the-supplier-portal).
</Step>

<Step>
**Collect the replies.** Prices land back on each supplier quote. The list shows which are still
outstanding.

<Step>
**Compare side by side.** Open the **comparison** view. Carbon lines the suppliers up so the
cheapest price and best lead time jump out. The lowest number isn't always the right one — a
slightly higher price that arrives sooner can win when a job is waiting.

<Step>
**Convert the winner to a PO.** Pick the quote and convert it to a
[purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) in one click. Supplier, item, price, and
quantity carry over, and the PO stays linked to the quote. That's exactly how Acme's
`PO000057` to Ryerson came to be: A36 plate at **$0.25/LB**.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Keep the prices for next time.** A supplier quote can feed a supplier's **price list** for
  that item, so next time Carbon already knows the price — no RFQ needed unless you want to re-shop it.
</Callout>

## Drop-ship

Normally goods you buy come to *you* — you receive them, then ship from your own dock.
Sometimes the supplier should ship **straight to your customer** instead. That's a
**drop-shipment**: you still buy it and still bill your customer, but the goods never touch your
building.

In Carbon it's a flag that links a sales order and a purchase order. Acme's hero order
`SO000069` is normal — its `dropShipment` flag is **false** (Acme makes those hooks itself). A
drop-ship order sets that flag to **true**.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  C[Customer order] --> SO[Sales Order drop-ship]
  SO --> PO[Purchase Order to supplier]
  PO --> CUST[Supplier ships to customer]
  CUST --> INV[You invoice the customer]`}
/>

### Set up a drop-ship

<Steps>

<Step>
**Mark the sales order line as drop-ship.** On the [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders),
turn on **drop-shipment** for the line. Carbon now knows the part won't come from your inventory.
</Step>

<Step>
**Generate the linked PO.** Carbon creates a PO to your supplier with the **ship-to** set to
your *customer's* address, not your warehouse. Confirm supplier, price, and quantity.

<AppShot title="Purchase Order — drop-ship" url="app.carbon.ms/x/purchase-order/…">
  <RecordPanel
    title="Purchase Order — drop-ship"
    subtitle="Ships to the customer, not to Acme"
    status={<StatusBadge tone="blue">To Order</StatusBadge>}
    tabs={["Details", "Lines", "Delivery"]}
    activeTab="Delivery"
  >
    <FieldGrid cols={2}>
      <Field label="Drop-ship" value="Yes" />
      <Field label="Ship to" value="Radiant Aerospace (customer site)" />
      <Field label="Linked sales order" value="SO000069" mono />
      <Field label="Location" value="Headquarters" />
    </FieldGrid>
  </RecordPanel>
</AppShot>
</Step>

<Step>
**Send the PO; the supplier ships your customer.** Nothing arrives at your shop, so there's no
receipt and no inventory movement on your side.
</Step>

<Step>
**Invoice the customer as usual.** You still bill from the sales order and pay from the PO. Your
margin is the difference — Carbon tracks both sides.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  **Drop-ship skips your inventory on purpose.** The part never appears in your
  [on-hand stock](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock) — by design. If you want the goods
  first (to inspect or kit), buy it normally and ship it yourself.
</Callout>

## The supplier portal

How do suppliers actually reply to an RFQ or confirm a PO? Carbon gives each supplier a
**portal** — a secure web link where they respond online, with no Carbon login of their own.

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "doc", label: "Document" },
    { key: "can", label: "What the supplier can do" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { doc: <Tag>Supplier quote / RFQ</Tag>, can: "Enter unit prices, lead times, minimum quantities, and notes — then submit" },
    { doc: <Tag>Purchase order</Tag>, can: "Review and acknowledge the order, confirm dates, or flag a problem" },
  ]}
/>

Whatever the supplier types lands straight on the matching record in your Carbon — no copy-paste,
no typos. Each link is private, so a supplier only sees the documents you sent *them*.

<Callout type="tip">
  **No account, no friction.** The portal makes it easy for small shops — like an anodizer or
  painter — to reply fast. The faster they respond, the faster your RFQ comparison fills in.
</Callout>

## Purchasing planning

How do you know *what* to buy in the first place? **Purchasing planning** watches your demand
and your stock, works out where you'll run short, and hands you a list of **buy suggestions**
you can turn into POs.

Demand comes from two places:

- **Jobs.** When you release a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs), its
  [method](/manual/items/methods) lists the materials it needs. Not enough on hand = a shortage.
- **Reorder points.** For stocked items (see [Replenishment](/manual/items/replenishment)),
  Carbon flags anything that dips below its minimum.

This is how Acme's hero PO was born. Job `J000097` needed **40 LB** of A36 plate per piece —
**20,000 LB** for the batch of 500 — and Acme was short. Carbon flagged it, and that suggestion
became `PO000057` to Ryerson.

### Turn suggestions into purchase orders

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the planning view.** In the **Purchasing** module, open **Planning**. Carbon lists every
item about to run short, the quantity it suggests, and what's driving the need.
</Step>

<Step>
**Review and adjust.** Bump a quantity to hit a price break, change the supplier, or skip a line.
Carbon's number is a starting point, not a command.
</Step>

<Step>
**Generate the POs.** Select the lines and let Carbon create the POs. It groups them by supplier,
so one click can make several tidy orders — all the Ryerson items on one PO.
</Step>

<Step>
**Approve and send.** New POs land in **Draft** or **Needs Approval** (like `PO000057` did).
From there it's the normal [purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) loop.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="info">
  **Planning closes the loop.** A job creates demand, planning surfaces the shortage, a PO fills
  it, the receipt restocks the shelf, and the job pulls what it needs. Run planning regularly and
  shortages stop sneaking up on you.
</Callout>
