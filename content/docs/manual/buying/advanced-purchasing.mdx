[Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) covered the everyday loop: raise a
purchase order, send it, receive the goods. This page is for the trickier buying jobs that
real shops run into — sending a half-finished part out to a specialist for a coating,
getting competing prices before you commit, shipping straight from your supplier to your
customer, and letting Carbon tell you *what* to buy before a shortage stalls a job. None of
these are exotic; you'll likely use all of them within your first month.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Everything here sits inside the buying half of the flow —
  [Quote](/manual/selling/quoting) → [Sales Order](/manual/selling/sales-orders) →
  [Job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) → **Purchasing** → [Shipment](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving)
  → [Invoice](/manual/accounting/overview). Make sure you're comfortable with
  [Purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) and [Suppliers](/manual/buying/suppliers)
  first — this page builds on both.
</Callout>

## Outside processing

Sometimes a step in making a part isn't something you do in-house. A part comes off your
saw and CNC mill in great shape, but it still needs **anodizing** or **painting** — and you
don't own an anodizing line. So you send the parts to a specialist supplier, they perform
the service, and they ship the parts back to you to finish. That round trip is called
**outside processing** (also "outside service" or "subcontracting").

The key idea in Carbon: outside processing is just an **operation** in a part's
[routing](/manual/overview/glossary), exactly like Flame Cut or Inspection — except its type
is **Outside** instead of **Inside**. An *Inside* operation runs at one of your own
[work centers](/manual/overview/glossary) and costs you shop time. An *Outside* operation
runs at a **supplier**, and its cost is what the supplier charges you. When the job reaches
that operation, Carbon helps you raise a purchase order for the service and tracks the parts
while they're off-site.

Providers**:

[Counterweight Hook](/manual/walkthrough) — all three of its operations (Flame Cut,
Sandblasting, Inspection) are **Inside**, so the whole part is made under Acme's own roof.

### Set up and run an outside operation

<Steps>

<Step>
**Add the operation to the routing and set its type to Outside.** Open the part's
**Manufacturing** tab, add an operation where the service belongs in the sequence, and
choose **Outside** as the operation type. (You can do the same on a one-off
[job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) without changing the part — handy for a rush order.)
</Step>

<Step>
**Point it at a supplier and a service item.** Pick the supplier who performs the work —
**Allen's Anodizing** — and link the operation to a **Service** [item](/manual/overview/glossary)
if you track services as items. Enter what the supplier charges (per piece or per lot). That
price becomes part of the part's rolled-up cost, just like in-house labor does.
</Step>

<Step>
**Release the job.** When you release the job, the outside operation appears in its
operation list with status **Todo**, sitting in sequence between the inside steps before and
after it.
</Step>

<Step>
**Raise a purchase order for the service.** When production reaches the outside step, create
a [purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) to the supplier for the service. Because
the operation is linked to the job, the PO is tied back to it — Carbon knows these parts left
the building for anodizing, and which job they belong to.

</Step>

<Step>
**Ship the parts out, then receive them back.** Send the work-in-progress parts to the
supplier. When they come back finished, record a [receipt](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving)
against that PO. Receiving the service marks the outside operation **complete**, and the job
moves on to the next step — here, final Inspection.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **One supplier can do many things.** A subcontractor like Sendcutsend cuts *and* finishes
  parts, so it can appear on outside operations for several different services. Set the
  supplier's [supplier type](/manual/buying/suppliers) to **Subcontractors and Service
  Providers** so it's easy to find when you're building a routing.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  **Outside parts are still your inventory.** While parts are at the anodizer, they haven't
  been scrapped or shipped to a customer — they're work-in-progress that's temporarily off-site.
  Don't close the job or promise the parts as finished until you've received them back and the
  outside operation reads complete. Otherwise your job costs and on-hand counts will be wrong.
</Callout>

## Supplier quotes (RFQs)

Before you buy something expensive or new, you usually want more than one price. In Carbon
you do that with a **Request for Quote (RFQ)**: you describe what you want to buy, send it to
several suppliers, collect their **supplier quotes** back, and compare them. When you pick a
winner, you convert the chosen quote into a purchase order — no re-typing.

A **supplier quote** is the buying-side mirror of the [sales quote](/manual/selling/quoting)
you send customers. Carbon creates one for each supplier you ask, so a single RFQ can spin
off several supplier quotes you track side by side.

Say Acme needs a big lot of 1-inch A36 steel plate and wants to shop it around. Acme's metals
suppliers all carry it, so the RFQ goes to four of them at once:

### Send an RFQ and compare the replies

<Steps>

<Step>
**Create the RFQ and add what you want priced.** In the **Purchasing** module, create a new
RFQ and add a line for each item you want quoted — here, one line for **A36 Steel Plate 1″**
(`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`) at the quantity you're planning to buy. You can add notes,
target dates, and attachments (drawings, specs) per line.
</Step>

<Step>
**Choose the suppliers and send.** Add the four suppliers above as recipients. Carbon
generates a **supplier quote** for each one and sends a request — either by email or through
the [supplier portal](#the-supplier-portal), so suppliers can type their prices in directly.
</Step>

<Step>
**Collect the replies.** As suppliers respond, their prices land back on each supplier quote.
You'll see quotes come in at different prices, lead times, and minimum quantities. The list
view shows you which ones are still outstanding.

arrives a week sooner can be the right call when a job is waiting.

**Convert the winner to a purchase order.** Pick the quote you want and convert it to a

why you paid what you paid. That's exactly how Acme's `PO000057` to Ryerson came to be:
A36 plate at **$0.25/LB**.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Keep the prices for next time.** A supplier quote can feed a supplier's **price list** for
  that item, so the next time you buy it Carbon already knows the price and lead time — no RFQ
  needed unless you want to re-shop it.
</Callout>

## Drop-ship

Normally goods you buy come to *you* — you receive them, then ship from your own dock. But
sometimes it makes more sense for the supplier to ship **straight to your customer**. Maybe
you're reselling a finished item, or the part is bulky and your shop is just a middleman. That
shortcut is a **drop-shipment**: you still buy it and you still bill your customer, but the
goods never touch your building.

In Carbon, drop-ship is a flag that links a sales order and a purchase order together.
Acme's hero order `SO000069` is a normal (non-drop-ship) order — its `dropShipment` flag is
**false**, because Acme makes those hooks in-house and ships them itself. A drop-ship order
sets that flag to **true** instead.

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  C[Customer order] --> SO[Sales Order drop-ship]
  SO --> PO[Purchase Order to supplier]
  PO --> CUST[Supplier ships to customer]
  CUST --> INV[You invoice the customer]`}
/>

### Set up a drop-ship

<Steps>

<Step>
**Mark the sales order line as drop-ship.** On the [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders),
turn on **drop-shipment** for the line. This tells Carbon not to expect the part to come out
of your own inventory.
</Step>

<Step>
**Generate the linked purchase order.** Carbon creates a purchase order to your chosen
supplier for that line, with the **ship-to** set to your *customer's* address rather than
your own warehouse. Confirm the supplier, price, and quantity.

<AppShot title="Purchase Order — drop-ship" url="app.carbon.ms/x/purchase-order/…">
  <RecordPanel
    title="Purchase Order — drop-ship"
    subtitle="Ships to the customer, not to Acme"
    status={<StatusBadge tone="blue">To Order</StatusBadge>}
    tabs={["Details", "Lines", "Delivery"]}
    activeTab="Delivery"
  >
    <FieldGrid cols={2}>
      <Field label="Drop-ship" value="Yes" />
      <Field label="Ship to" value="Radiant Aerospace (customer site)" />
      <Field label="Linked sales order" value="SO000069" mono />
      <Field label="Location" value="Headquarters" />
    </FieldGrid>
  </RecordPanel>
</AppShot>
</Step>

<Step>
**Send the PO; the supplier ships your customer.** The supplier fulfills the order directly
to the customer's door. Nothing arrives at your shop, so there's no receipt to do and no
inventory movement on your side.
</Step>

<Step>
**Invoice the customer as usual.** You still bill your customer from the sales order, and you
still pay the supplier from the purchase order. Your margin is the difference — Carbon tracks
both sides even though the goods never passed through your hands.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  **Drop-ship skips your inventory on purpose.** Because the part never comes through your
  dock, it never appears in your [on-hand stock](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock) — by
  design. Only use drop-ship for lines you genuinely want fulfilled supplier-to-customer. If
  you want the goods first (to inspect, kit, or combine with other items), buy it normally and
  ship it yourself.
</Callout>

## The supplier portal

How do suppliers actually *reply* to an RFQ or confirm a PO? You don't want to chase prices
over email and re-key them by hand. Carbon gives each supplier a **portal** — a secure web
link where they can see what you've sent them and respond online, without needing a Carbon
login of their own.

When you send a supplier quote or purchase order through the portal, Carbon includes a
private link. The supplier clicks it and can:

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "doc", label: "Document" },
    { key: "can", label: "What the supplier can do" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { doc: <Tag>Supplier quote / RFQ</Tag>, can: "Enter unit prices, lead times, minimum quantities, and notes — then submit" },
    { doc: <Tag>Purchase order</Tag>, can: "Review and acknowledge the order, confirm dates, or flag a problem" },
  ]}
/>

Whatever the supplier types lands straight on the matching record in your Carbon — no copy-paste,
no transcription mistakes. Each request carries its own link, so a supplier only ever sees the
documents you sent *them*.

<Callout type="tip">
  **No account, no friction.** The whole point of the portal is to make it easy for suppliers
  to respond — especially small shops like an anodizer or painter who'll never log in to a full
  ERP. The link does the work. The faster they can reply, the faster your RFQ comparison fills in.
</Callout>

## Purchasing planning

So far you've been creating purchase orders by hand. But how do you know *what* to buy in the
first place? On a busy shop floor, the answer is **purchasing planning** — Carbon watches your
demand and your stock, works out where you're going to run short, and hands you a list of
**buy suggestions** you can turn into purchase orders.

Demand comes from two places:

- **Jobs.** When you release a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs), its
  [method](/manual/items/methods) lists the materials it needs. If you don't have enough on
  hand, that's a shortage.
- **MRP / replenishment.** For stocked items with reorder points (see
  [Replenishment](/manual/items/replenishment)), Carbon flags anything that's dipped below
  its minimum.

This is exactly how Acme's hero purchase order was born. Job `J000097` needed **40 LB** of
A36 steel plate per piece — **20,000 LB** for the batch of 500 — and Acme didn't have that
much in stock. Carbon flagged the shortage, and that suggestion became purchase order
`PO000057` to Ryerson.

### Turn suggestions into purchase orders

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the planning view.** In the **Purchasing** module, open purchasing planning. Carbon
lists every item where projected demand is about to outrun supply, the quantity it suggests
buying, and what's driving the need (a specific job, or a reorder point).
</Step>

<Step>
**Review and adjust.** You're in control — bump a quantity to hit a price break, change the
suggested supplier, or skip a line you'll handle another way. Carbon's number is a starting
point, not a command.
</Step>

<Step>
**Generate the purchase orders.** Select the lines you want and let Carbon create the POs.
It groups lines by supplier, so one click can produce several tidy orders — all the Ryerson
items on one PO, all the Fastenal items on another.
</Step>

<Step>
**Approve and send.** The new POs land in **Draft** or **Needs Approval** (just like
`PO000057` did). From there it's the normal [purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics)
loop — approve, send, receive.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="info">
  **Planning closes the loop.** This is the moment the buying side connects back to
  production: a job creates demand, planning surfaces the shortage, a PO fills it, the
  receipt restocks the shelf, and the job pulls the material it needs. Run planning
  regularly and shortages stop sneaking up on you.
</Callout>
