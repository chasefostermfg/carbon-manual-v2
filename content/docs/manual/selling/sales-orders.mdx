A **sales order** (SO) is your customer's commitment to buy: who's ordering, what,
how many, when it's due, and at what price. It's the single most important record in
fulfillment because everything downstream hangs off it — the [job](/manual/overview/glossary)
that makes the part, the [purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) that buys the
material, the [shipment](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving) that sends it, and the
[invoice](/manual/accounting/overview) that bills for it all point back to this one document.
Enter the order once and Carbon carries that information everywhere the work goes.

This page follows Acme's real order **SO000069** — 500 Counterweight Hooks for Radiant
Aerospace, $36,820, *Make to Order* — from creation to completion.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** A sales order usually starts life as a [quote](/manual/selling/quoting)
  the customer accepts. From here, Carbon spawns the [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) that
  makes the part, then the order flows on to [shipping and the invoice](/manual/selling/sales-to-shipment).
  Quote → **Sales Order** → Job → Shipment → Invoice.
</Callout>

## What a sales order is made of

Every sales order has two parts:

- A **header** — the facts that apply to the whole order: the customer, the order date,
  the [payment terms](/manual/overview/glossary), the shipping location, and the customer's
  own purchase-order number (their "PO reference").
- One or more **lines** — each line is one thing the customer is buying: an item, a
  quantity, a unit price, a promised date, and a **fulfillment method** that tells Carbon
  *how* you'll deliver it. One order can mix made-to-order parts, stock items, and services
  on separate lines.

## Create a sales order

There are two ways an order is born: you **convert a quote** the customer accepted, or you
**start a fresh order** by hand. Converting is by far the most common — it's how SO000069
was created — so start there.

<Tabs items={["Convert a quote", "Start from scratch"]}>
<Tab value="Convert a quote">

When a customer accepts a [quote](/manual/selling/quoting), you don't re-type anything.
Carbon copies the lines, quantities, prices, and the linked manufacturing
[method](/manual/items/methods) straight across, and keeps the two records linked so you
can always trace an order back to the price you promised.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the accepted quote.** Go to **Sales → Quotes** and open the quote — for Acme that's
`Q000061`. Confirm the line, quantity, and price are what the customer agreed to.
</Step>

<Step>
**Convert to Sales Order.** Click **Convert to Order** (or **Finalize**) in the top-right
of the quote. Carbon creates a new sales order — `SO000069` — and copies every line over,
including the *Make to Order* fulfillment method on the Counterweight Hook line.
</Step>

<Step>
**Review the header.** The customer, currency, and payment terms come from the quote and the
customer record. Add or confirm the **customer PO reference** (`55557777`) and the **ship-from
location** (Headquarters). Set the **order date** if it isn't today.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Because the quote line and the sales-order line share the same internal ID, Carbon links
  them directly. Open `SO000069` and you can jump straight back to `Q000061` — handy when a
  customer asks "is this the price you quoted me?"
</Callout>

</Tab>
<Tab value="Start from scratch">

Not every order comes from a quote — a repeat customer might just email a PO. In that case
you create the order directly.

<Steps>

<Step>
**New sales order.** Go to **Sales → Sales Orders** and click **New**. Pick the
[customer](/manual/selling/customers) (Radiant Aerospace). Carbon fills in their default
payment terms, currency, and ship-to address automatically.
</Step>

<Step>
**Fill in the header.** Set the **order date**, the **ship-from location**, and type in the
customer's **PO reference**. Save the header to get an order number.
</Step>

<Step>
**Add the lines.** Add one line per item the customer is buying — covered next.
</Step>

</Steps>

</Tab>
</Tabs>

## Choose a fulfillment method per line

The **fulfillment method** is the most important choice on a line. It tells Carbon how this
particular item gets delivered — and that decision drives everything that happens next. You
set it **per line**, so one order can fulfill some items from stock and make others to order.

<DataTable
  columns={[
    { key: "method", label: "Method" },
    { key: "when", label: "Use it when…" },
    { key: "does", label: "What Carbon does" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { method: <Tag>Make to Order</Tag>, when: "The item is built specifically for this order.", does: "Spawns a job to manufacture it (see below)." },
    { method: <Tag>Pull from Stock</Tag>, when: "You already keep the finished item on a shelf.", does: "Reserves on-hand inventory; no job is created." },
    { method: <Tag>Drop Ship</Tag>, when: "A supplier ships straight to your customer.", does: "Links a purchase order that delivers to the customer's address." },
  ]}
/>

The Counterweight Hook line on SO000069 is **Make to Order** — Acme doesn't keep 500 finished
hooks on a shelf, so each order is built fresh. That one setting is why a job appears next.

<Callout type="info">
  An item's default fulfillment method comes from its
  [replenishment setting](/manual/items/replenishment) — a *Make* part defaults to *Make to
  Order*, a *Buy* part to *Pull from Stock*. You can override the default on any individual
  line.
</Callout>

## How a line spawns a job

When you confirm an order with a **Make to Order** line, Carbon creates a **job** — a
production order that carries its own copy of the item's [method](/manual/items/methods)
(its bill of materials and routing). The job is the bridge from "the customer ordered it" to
"the shop floor makes it."

<Callout type="tip">
  Carbon won't usually create the job until the order is **confirmed**. Confirming is your
  signal that the order is real and the shop should commit material and machine time to it —
  so review your lines before you confirm.
</Callout>

## Promised dates and the due date

The **promised date** on a line is the date you've told the customer they'll have the goods.
Carbon uses it to set the job's **due date** and to schedule the work backwards from there —
so the shop knows when each operation has to start. On SO000069 the promised date is
**2026-06-26**, which became the due date on job J000097.

<Callout type="warn">
  Promising a date Carbon can't hit is the most common scheduling mistake. If the routing
  needs more shop time than the days between today and the promised date, the job will be
  late before it starts. Check the job's schedule after you confirm — and if material has to
  be [purchased first](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) (Acme bought A36 steel on PO000057),
  build that lead time into the promise.
</Callout>

## Common gotchas

<Accordions type="single">
<Accordion title="I confirmed the order but no job appeared.">
Check the line's **fulfillment method**. A job is only created for **Make to Order** lines.
If the line is set to *Pull from Stock*, Carbon reserves inventory instead — no job. Change
the method on the line if the item really does need to be made.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="The price on the line isn't what I quoted.">
Lines copied from a quote carry the quoted price. A line you add by hand uses the item's
default sell price instead, which may differ. When in doubt, convert from the quote rather
than re-keying — that's the whole point of the quote-to-order link.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Can one order ship in more than one batch?">
Yes. A line can be shipped partially — send what's ready now and the rest later. Each
shipment shows on the **Shipments** tab, and the line stays open (status *To Ship*) until the
full quantity has gone out. The order only reaches *Completed* when every line is fully
shipped and invoiced.
</Accordion>
</Accordions>
