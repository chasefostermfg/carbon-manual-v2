A **customer** is anyone you sell to. In Carbon, a customer record is the home base for one
company you do business with — it holds their people (contacts), their addresses (ship-to and
bill-to locations), how and when they pay you (payment terms), and even the part numbers *they*
use for *your* parts. Once a customer exists, you can quote them, take their orders, ship to them,
and invoice them — all without re-typing their details each time.

This page walks you through creating a customer, filling in everything Carbon needs, and reading
a customer record. We'll use Acme's real customers throughout, and build out **Radiant Aerospace**
in full — the customer behind the Counterweight Hook order you may have seen in
[Carbon in 15 minutes](/manual/walkthrough).

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Customers are the *who* of selling — the starting point for everything on
  the sales side. Once a customer exists, you can write them a [quote](/manual/selling/quoting),
  turn that into a [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders), and eventually bill them. This is
  the first stop in the Quote → Sales Order → Job → Shipment → Invoice flow.
</Callout>

### Type: how you group customers

A **customer type** is a label you use to slice your customers into segments — handy for filtering
lists and reporting. Acme groups its customers by industry: *Aerospace & Defense*, *Construction*,
*Dealers*, *Energy & Power*, and *Mining*. Radiant Aerospace is **Aerospace & Defense**. Types are
optional but make a long customer list far easier to navigate.

<Callout type="tip">
  Both statuses and types are fully editable lists you set up once for your company, under
  **Settings**. Define the segments that match *your* business before you start adding customers in
  bulk, so everything lands in the right bucket from day one. See
  [Set up your company](/manual/getting-started/setup-company).
</Callout>

## Create a customer

Let's create Radiant Aerospace from scratch. The only thing Carbon truly requires is a name — but
filling in the rest now saves re-keying later.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Open the new-customer form

From the **Customers** list, click **New Customer** (top right). Carbon opens a blank customer
form. Give it a **name** — this is the one required field — then set the optional details that make
the customer usable everywhere else.

<AppShot title="New Customer" url="app.carbon.ms/x/sales/customers/new" caption="Creating Radiant Aerospace.">
  <FieldGrid cols={2}>
    <Field label="Name" value="Radiant Aerospace" />
    <Field label="Customer type" value="Aerospace & Defense" />
    <Field label="Status" value="Active" />
    <Field label="Currency" value="USD" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>

</Step>

<Step>

### Set the customer ID (or let Carbon do it)

Carbon assigns a readable **customer ID** automatically using your company's numbering sequence —
Radiant's is `CUS000003`, and you rarely type this yourself. Behind the scenes every customer also
gets a permanent internal ID (Radiant's is `cust_LxoH7AbowNVjTwGSoqkW1Q`); you'll see that form of
ID in the URL and in the [API](/manual/developing/using-the-api), but day to day you just use the
name.

</Step>

<Step>

### Save

Click **Save**. The customer now exists and appears on the Customers list. You can stop here and
add the rest later — but the next sections (contacts, locations, payment terms) are what make a
customer ready to quote and ship.

</Step>

</Steps>

## Add contacts

A **contact** is a person at the customer — a buyer, an engineer, an AP clerk. You attach contacts
to a customer so that when you write a quote or a sales order, you can pick *who* it's for and
*who* to email. A customer can have as many contacts as you need; one is usually marked as the
primary point of contact.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Open the Contacts tab

On the customer record, switch to the **Contacts** tab and click **New Contact**.

</Step>

<Step>

### Enter the person's details

Fill in name, email, phone and (optionally) job title. Email matters most — it's what Carbon uses
when you send a quote or order PDF, and it's how the customer can be invited to the
[customer portal](/manual/selling/sales-orders) to see their orders.

<AppShot title="New Contact · Radiant Aerospace" url="app.carbon.ms/x/sales/customers/…/contacts" caption="Adding Radiant's primary contact.">
  <FieldGrid cols={2}>
    <Field label="First name" value="Randy" />
    <Field label="Last name" value="Raymer" />
    <Field label="Title" value="CEO" />
    <Field label="Email" value="On file" />
    <Field label="Primary contact" value="Yes" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>

</Step>

<Step>

### Save and repeat

Save the contact. Add more for anyone else you deal with — for example a separate accounts-payable
contact who should receive invoices. When you later create a quote or order for this customer,
these contacts appear in a dropdown so you can pick the right recipient.

</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  The buyer's name and email aren't just decoration. On Radiant's real order **SO000069**, the
  selected contact is the person Carbon addressed the order confirmation to — and the same contact
  flowed through to the invoice **AR000025**. Set contacts up once and they follow the paperwork.
</Callout>

## Add ship-to and bill-to locations

A customer **location** is an address. Carbon treats two address *roles* specially:

- **Ship-to** — where finished goods are delivered. This drives [shipments](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving).
- **Bill-to** — where invoices are sent. This drives [accounting](/manual/accounting/overview).

Most customers have one of each (often the same address), but a large customer might receive at
several plants while all the bills go to one accounts-payable office. You add as many addresses as
you need and tag each one with its role.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Open the Locations tab

On the customer record, go to the **Locations** tab and click **New Location**.

</Step>

<Step>

### Enter the address and its role

Type the street address, city, state, postal code and country. Mark whether this address is a
**ship-to**, a **bill-to**, or both, and set one as the **default** so Carbon pre-fills it on new
orders. Radiant uses a single headquarters address for both roles, which is the most common setup.

<AppShot title="Customer Locations · Radiant Aerospace" url="app.carbon.ms/x/sales/customers/…/locations" caption="Radiant's headquarters — used as both ship-to and bill-to." flush>
  <DataTable
    columns={[
      { key: "name", label: "Location" },
      { key: "addr", label: "Address" },
      { key: "role", label: "Role" },
      { key: "default", label: "Default", align: "right" },
    ]}
    rows={[
      { name: "Radiant Aerospace HQ", addr: "4801 Shaw Lane, Austin, TX 78744", role: <Tag>Ship-to &amp; Bill-to</Tag>, default: "Yes" },

  />
</AppShot>

</Step>

<Step>

### Save

Save each address. On every new quote, order and invoice for this customer, Carbon proposes the
default ship-to and bill-to — but you can switch to any other address on the customer in one click.

</Step>

</Steps>

## Set payment terms

**Payment terms** define *when* a customer is expected to pay an invoice — for example **Net 30**
means "due 30 days after the invoice date." Setting terms on the customer means every invoice you
raise for them inherits the right due date automatically, so your accounting stays consistent
without anyone remembering the rule.

<Steps>

<Step>

### Open the Payment tab

On the customer record, go to the **Payment** tab. Pick the customer's default **payment terms**
from the dropdown (these terms are a reusable list set up in **Settings**).

</Step>

<Step>

### Choose the terms and currency

Radiant Aerospace is set to **Net 30**, billed in **USD**. You can also set a default tax setting
here if the customer is tax-exempt or charged at a specific rate.

<AppShot title="Payment · Radiant Aerospace" url="app.carbon.ms/x/sales/customers/…/payment" caption="Radiant's default billing terms.">
  <FieldGrid cols={3}>
    <Field label="Payment terms" value="Net 30" />
    <Field label="Currency" value="USD" />
    <Field label="Tax" value="None (0%)" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>

</Step>

<Step>

### Save

Save. From now on, any invoice for this customer defaults to Net 30 — exactly what you'll see on
Radiant's real invoice **AR000025** (issued and due against the same order).

</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Terms set on the customer are *defaults*, not handcuffs. You can override the payment terms on a
  single order or invoice when a deal calls for it — the customer default just spares you from
  setting them every time.
</Callout>

## Customer-specific part numbers

Here's a small feature that prevents big headaches. Your customer often calls your part by *their*
internal number, not yours. Acme's part is `CWH-A36-15` (the Counterweight Hook), but Radiant
Aerospace orders it as **730562**. Carbon lets you record that mapping once, on the customer, so:

- When Radiant sends a PO referencing **730562**, you instantly know it's `CWH-A36-15`.
- When you send Radiant a quote, order or packing slip, Carbon can print *their* number alongside
  yours — so the paperwork matches what the customer expects.

This is stored as the **customer part number** (Carbon's field is `customerPartId`), and it lives
on the link between a customer and one of your [items](/manual/items/defining-an-item).

<AppShot title="Customer Parts · Radiant Aerospace" url="app.carbon.ms/x/sales/customers/…/parts" caption="Mapping Acme's part numbers to Radiant's." flush>

    ]}
    rows={[
      { ours: "CWH-A36-15", desc: "Counterweight Hook", theirs: "730562", rev: "—" },

<Callout type="info">
  You'll see **730562** again on Radiant's real sales order line in
  [Sales orders](/manual/selling/sales-orders) — Carbon carried the customer part number straight
  from the customer record onto the order, with no extra typing.
</Callout>

enter the relationship once and it follows every transaction.

<Callout type="warn">
  Avoid creating the same company twice. Duplicate customers split a company's history across two
  records, so quotes, orders and invoices no longer add up in one place. Before adding a customer,
  search the list — if they're already there (even as a *Lead*), edit that record rather than making
  a new one.
</Callout>
