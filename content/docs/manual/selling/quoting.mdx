A **quote** is your answer to a customer's "what would this cost?" In Carbon you build a
quote by adding a line for the part and a quantity, and Carbon adds up the cost of the
materials and shop time behind it so you can set a price you're confident in. When the
customer says yes, one click turns the quote into a [**sales order**](/manual/overview/glossary)
— no re-typing, and the two stay linked forever. This page walks through that whole arc using
Acme's real quote **Q000061**: 500 Counterweight Hooks for **Radiant Aerospace** at
**$73.14 each**.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** The quote is the *first* stop in Carbon's sales flow:
  **Quote → [Sales Order](/manual/selling/sales-orders) → [Job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) → Shipment → [Invoice](/manual/accounting/overview)**.
  Before you quote, you need a [customer](/manual/selling/customers) to quote *to* and an
  [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) to quote — so if you haven't set those up yet,
  start there. To see the whole chain end to end, read the [15-minute walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough).
</Callout>

## What a quote is (and isn't)

A quote is a **non-binding price offer**. Nothing gets made, bought, or billed because a
quote exists — it's a proposal. That makes it the safe place to experiment: try different
quantities, see how the price-per-piece changes, adjust your margin, and send a polished
PDF to the customer. Only when they accept do you convert it into a sales order, which *is*
a commitment.

A quote in Carbon has three layers, from the outside in:

- **The quote** — the document itself, addressed to one customer (Radiant Aerospace), with a
  number Carbon assigns automatically (`Q000061`).
- **Lines** — one per thing you're pricing. Each line points at an [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item)
  and carries a quantity.
- **Price breaks** — one or more quantities you price *within* a line. Quoting 100 vs. 500 vs.
  1,000 of the same part usually gives different per-piece prices, and Carbon lets you show
  all three on one line.

## Create the quote

Quotes live in the **Sales** module. You start a quote by picking the customer, then Carbon
opens an empty quote with a fresh number for you to fill in.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open Sales → Quotes and select New.** Carbon shows the list of every quote in the system.
Acme has 44 of them. The list is your home base — you'll come back here to find, sort and
filter quotes by customer or status.

    ]}
    rows={[
      { id: "Q000061", customer: "Radiant Aerospace", lines: "1", status: <StatusBadge tone="green">Ordered</StatusBadge> },
      { id: "Q000060", customer: "Deep Rock Mining", lines: "1", status: <StatusBadge tone="neutral">Draft</StatusBadge> },
      { id: "Q000059", customer: "Nexus Power", lines: "1", status: <StatusBadge tone="blue">Sent</StatusBadge> },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>
</Step>

<Step>
**Choose the customer.** Pick **Radiant Aerospace** from the customer field. Carbon pulls in
everything it already knows about them — billing address, ship-to location, the contact who
asked for the quote, currency (USD) and payment terms (**Net 30**) — so you don't re-key any
of it. This is why [setting up your customer](/manual/selling/customers) first pays off.
</Step>

<Step>
**Save the header.** Carbon assigns the next quote number (`Q000061`) and a *revision* of `0`.
The quote opens in **Draft** status. Draft means it's yours to edit freely; the customer hasn't
seen it yet.

<Callout type="tip">
  Revisions let you re-quote without losing history. If Radiant comes back asking for a
  different quantity, you bump the quote to revision `1` instead of starting over — the
  original price you offered stays on the record.
</Callout>
</Step>

</Steps>

## Add a line

A blank quote is just a header. The work happens on the **lines** — one per part you're
pricing. Each line ties the quote to a real [item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) so Carbon
can cost it accurately.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Add a line and pick the part.** On the quote, choose **Add Line**, set the line type to
**Part**, and select **Counterweight Hook** (`CWH-A36-15`). Carbon fills the description and
the unit of measure (**EA** — Each) from the item. You can also capture the customer's own
part number here — Radiant calls this hook `730562`, so it prints on the quote and they
recognize it instantly.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the quantity.** Type **500**. This is the price break you're quoting. If you wanted to
show Radiant a price ladder, you'd add more quantities to the same line — say 100, 500 and
1,000 — and Carbon prices each one.
</Step>

</Steps>

  makes the next step possible.
</Callout>

## How Carbon prices the line

This is the part that makes Carbon a quoting tool, not just a form. For a **Make to Order**
part, Carbon already knows the recipe — what the part is made *of* and the steps to make it —
so it can roll up a real cost instead of asking you to guess.

For the Counterweight Hook, the recipe is short. Each hook is flame-cut from **40 lb of
1-inch A36 steel plate**, then runs through three shop operations:

<AppShot title="Method · CWH-A36-15" url="app.carbon.ms/x/part/item_2ejw9…/manufacturing" caption="Carbon costs the quote from this method — one material, three operations." flush>
  <MethodTree
    nodes={[
      {
        kind: "assembly",
        label: "CWH-A36-15",
        detail: "Counterweight Hook",
        qty: "1 EA",
        children: [
          { kind: "material", label: "A36-STEEL-PLT-1\"", detail: "A36 Steel Plate 1″ — pulled from stock", qty: "40 LB" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "10 · Flame Cut", detail: "Burn Table — PA2 · $50/hr labor", qty: "45 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "20 · Sandblasting", detail: "Sand Blasting Room · $50/hr labor", qty: "2 min/pc" },
          { kind: "operation", label: "30 · Inspection", detail: "Inspection Station · $50/hr labor", qty: "10 min/pc" },
        ],
      },
    ]}
  />
</AppShot>

Carbon multiplies each ingredient by its cost and each operation's time by its
[work center](/manual/overview/glossary) rate, then adds it all up into a **rolled-up cost
per piece**. You don't do the arithmetic — Carbon does, and it re-does it the moment any
input changes (a new steel price, a faster operation, a bigger batch).

You set the **price**; Carbon shows you the **margin**. Margin is the gap between what the
work costs you and what you charge — the cushion that has to cover overhead and profit.
Type a unit price and Carbon shows the margin live; type a target margin and Carbon
back-solves the price. Acme set **$73.14 each** on this line:

<StatGrid cols={3}>
  <Stat label="Quoted price" value="$73.14" hint="per hook, what Radiant pays" />
  <Stat label="Line total" value="$36,570.00" hint="500 EA at $73.14" />
  <Stat label="Quantity" value="500 EA" hint="this price break" />
</StatGrid>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Cost is not price.** Carbon tracks the internal *cost* to make a part separately from the
  *price* you quote — on this line the recorded unit cost rounds to fractions of a cent
  against a $73.14 price, because most of the value is in the labor and material Carbon rolls
  up behind the scenes. You always control the price; Carbon just keeps the cost honest so
  your margin is real.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  Carbon's cost rollup is only as good as the part's [method](/manual/items/methods). If a
  part has no bill of materials or routing yet, Carbon can't estimate its cost, and you'll be
  pricing blind. Build the method first — or quote a known price and refine it later.
</Callout>

## Configurators: pricing made-to-order parts

Some parts aren't one fixed thing — they're a *family*. A flame-cut plate part might come in
any thickness, size or grade the customer asks for, and each combination has a different
amount of steel and cutting time, so a different cost. Re-typing a method for every variation
would be miserable.

A **configurator** solves this. It's a single item that exposes a few choices — Acme has one
called the **Flame Cut Part Configurator** (`FLAME CONFIG`) — and those choices drive the
method and the cost automatically. You quote the *configurator*, answer its questions, and
Carbon assembles the right bill of materials, routing and rolled-up cost on the fly.

<Callout type="tip">
  Use a configurator when you sell the same *kind* of part in many shapes — cut plate,
  machined bar stock, bent sheet. Use a plain part with a fixed method (like `CWH-A36-15`)
  when the design is settled and you make it the same way every time. Both price the same way
  once the method exists. Learn more in [Methods: BOM & routing](/manual/items/methods).
</Callout>

## Send the quote

When the lines and prices look right, you send the quote to the customer. Carbon generates a
clean PDF and can email it to the contact on the quote — no copying numbers into a separate
document.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Review the totals.** Check the line prices and the quote total one more time. On Q000061
that's a single line: 500 EA × $73.14 = **$36,570.00**.
</Step>

<Step>
**Select Send.** Carbon builds the quote PDF, addresses it to Radiant's contact, and records
that the quote was sent. The status moves from **Draft** to **Sent** — your signal that the
ball is now in the customer's court.

<Callout type="tip">
  Sending also timestamps the quote. If you've set an expiration window, that clock starts
  here, so the customer knows the price isn't open-ended.
</Callout>
</Step>

</Steps>

## Convert a won quote to a sales order

When Radiant says yes, you don't rebuild anything. From the quote you select **Convert to
Order** and Carbon copies the customer, lines, quantities and prices straight onto a brand-new
[sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders) — here, **SO000069**.

The two records stay **linked**. Open J000097 later and you can trace it back through
SO000069 all the way to the quote you sent — so when someone asks "what price did we promise
on this?" the answer is one click away. That traceability is the whole point of entering the
information once.

<Callout type="info">
  The order total ($36,820.00) is a little higher than the quote line total ($36,570.00)
  because $250 of shipping was added on the order. Quotes price the *work*; the order is where
  freight, taxes and other add-ons get finalized.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  Converting doesn't close the quote behind your back — it marks it **Ordered** so you can
  still see exactly what you offered. A quote that the customer passes on can be marked
  **Lost** instead, which keeps your win/loss history honest for later analysis.
</Callout>
