You've taken the order — now you have to deliver it. This page follows the **back half** of
the sale: how a confirmed sales order turns into a finished, shipped, and invoiced job. It's
a close-up companion to the [15-minute walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough), so we'll reuse the
same real example — the 500 **Counterweight Hooks** Acme made for **Radiant Aerospace** on
[sales order](/manual/overview/glossary) **SO000069** — and zoom in on the steps the
walkthrough skipped past: the [job](/manual/overview/glossary) the order created, tracking
fulfillment, building the [shipment](/manual/overview/glossary), and raising the invoice.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** This is the last leg of the selling flow:
  Quote → Sales Order → **(make it) → ship it → invoice it**.
  It picks up right after [Sales orders](/manual/selling/sales-orders), where you confirm
  the order, and hands off to [Accounting](/manual/accounting/overview), where the invoice
  becomes revenue. If a line is *Make to Order*, the manufacturing chapter
  ([Jobs](/manual/manufacturing/jobs)) covers actually building it; here we stay on the
  sales side and watch the order get fulfilled.
</Callout>

## The fulfillment flow at a glance

Once a sales order is confirmed, every line has to be **fulfilled** — Carbon's word for
"get the goods to the customer." How that happens depends on one setting on each line: the
**fulfillment method** (Carbon also calls this the *method type*).

- **Make to Order** — the part has to be built, so confirming the order creates a **job**.
  Nothing ships until that job produces finished pieces.
- **Make to Stock** / **Pull from Inventory** — the part is already on a shelf, so you skip
  straight to picking and shipping.

Our hero line is *Make to Order*, which is the longer, more interesting path. Here's the
whole chain you're about to walk through:

## Step 1 — A Make-to-Order line creates a job

When you confirm a sales order with a *Make to Order* line, Carbon doesn't expect the part
to be sitting in inventory. Instead it spins up a **job** — a production order that knows
exactly which order line it's serving — and links the two together. You don't re-type
anything; the part, the quantity, and the due date all flow from the line into the job.

On SO000069, line 1 is 500 Counterweight Hooks set to *Make to Order*. Confirming the order
created job **J000097** for exactly 500 pieces, due 2026-06-26.

<Callout type="tip">
  You don't have to leave the sales order to check on production. The **Jobs** tab on the SO
  lists every linked job and its live status — for SO000069 it shows J000097 at 500 of 500
  complete. From the [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders) you always know whether the
  thing you sold actually got made.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  A *Make to Order* line **can't ship until its job produces finished pieces**. If the job is
  still In Progress, the order line stays open — Carbon won't let you ship parts you haven't
  made yet. Building that job is the [Manufacturing](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) chapter's
  job (pun intended); the rest of this page assumes the pieces are done and in stock.
</Callout>

## Step 2 — Track fulfillment on the sales order

While the job runs, the sales order is your dashboard for "how close are we to delivering?"
Every line carries three running tallies that Carbon keeps up to date automatically:

- **Quantity** — how many the customer ordered.
- **Quantity shipped** — how many have left the building so far.
- **Quantity invoiced** — how many you've billed for.

These three numbers tell the whole fulfillment story at a glance. When *shipped* catches up
to *ordered*, the line is fully delivered; when *invoiced* catches up too, the line is fully
billed. Carbon also flips two flags — `sentComplete` and `invoicedComplete` — to **true**
once a line is fully shipped and fully invoiced, which is how the order knows it's finished.

<Callout type="info">
  These tallies are **per line**, not just per order. A single sales order can have many
  lines at different stages — one fully shipped, one half shipped, one not started — and
  Carbon tracks each independently. The order as a whole only counts as *Completed* when
  every line is fully shipped and invoiced, which is exactly the state SO000069 is in.
</Callout>

## Step 3 — Create the shipment

A **shipment** is the record of goods physically leaving your dock. It's where you say "this
much, of this part, going to this customer, by this carrier." Creating one is how you turn
finished pieces into a delivery — and it's the event that updates the *shipped* tally you
just saw.

You can start a shipment straight from the sales order. Carbon pre-fills it from the order
so you're confirming, not re-typing.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the order and start a shipment.** From SO000069, use **Ship** (or the **Shipments**
tab → **New Shipment**). Carbon creates a shipment pre-filled with the customer (Radiant
Aerospace), the ship-to address, and the open lines from the order.
</Step>

<Step>
**Pick the finished goods.** Set how many of each line you're sending. The Counterweight
Hooks were received into inventory from the completed job, so all 500 are available to pick.
Because A36 steel is **batch-tracked**, Carbon records *which batch* of finished hooks you're
shipping — that's the genealogy link that powers [traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality)
later. Enter **500 EA** to ship the line in full.
</Step>

<Step>
**Choose a shipping method.** Pick the carrier and service. Acme has **10** shipping methods
configured, from in-house delivery to overnight air (see the table below). Choose the one
that matches what you quoted — for a heavy 500-piece steel order, that's typically a freight
method like **LTL (Less Than Truckload)** rather than a parcel service.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post the shipment.** Posting commits it: Carbon decrements the finished hooks from
inventory and adds **500** to the line's *quantity shipped*. A shipment record is created
(internally `sh_7injMjdu1xyZqTVFFrjauv`) and linked back to the order.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Shipping cost vs. shipping method are two different things.** The *method* is the carrier
  service you pick. The *cost* is what you bill the customer for freight — on SO000069 that's
  **$250**, set on the order line. The cost rides through to the invoice automatically; you
  don't add it twice.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  You can ship a line in **more than one go**. If you only finish 300 of 500 hooks, ship 300
  now — Carbon records a partial shipment, sets *quantity shipped* to 300, and leaves the
  line open for the remaining 200. The line only reads *Shipped* once the shipped quantity
  reaches the ordered quantity.
</Callout>

## Step 4 — The line is marked Shipped

Posting the shipment is what moves the needle on the order. Carbon adds the shipped quantity
to the line, and the moment *shipped* equals *ordered*, the line's `sentComplete` flag flips
to **true** and the status reads **Shipped**. For SO000069, posting all 500 finished the
line in a single shipment.

## Step 5 — Invoice the order

With the goods shipped, you can bill for them. A **sales invoice** is your request for
payment. The best part: Carbon builds it from what actually shipped, so the invoice can't
drift away from reality. You don't re-key the line — Carbon pulls the part, the quantity,
and the price straight off the shipped order line.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Create the invoice from the order.** From SO000069, use **Invoice** (or the **Invoices**
tab). Carbon drafts a sales invoice — **AR000025** — pulling line 1 in at the shipped
quantity (500) and the order's unit price ($73.14).
</Step>

<Step>
**Check the totals.** The line subtotal is 500 × $73.14 = **$36,570**. The **$250** shipping
cost from the order line carries over, making the invoice total **$36,820**. Payment terms
(Net 30) come from the customer record — no need to set them again.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post and send it.** Posting the invoice books the revenue and sets the line's
`invoicedComplete` flag to **true**. Send it to Radiant, and when they pay, mark it **Paid**
— which is exactly the state AR000025 ended in, paid the same day it was issued.
</Step>

</Steps>

  **Subtotal vs. invoice total.** The *subtotal* ($36,570) is the goods alone. The *invoice
  total* ($36,820) adds the $250 freight. That's why the order total and the invoice total
  match but the line subtotal is lower — the difference is shipping, every time.
</Callout>

Once posted, the invoice is an accounting document, not a sales one — it flows into your
books as **accounts receivable** and, when paid, recognizes revenue. How that posting works
is covered in [Accounting](/manual/accounting/overview).
