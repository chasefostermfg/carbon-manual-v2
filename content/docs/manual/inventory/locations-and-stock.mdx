Carbon always knows two things about your physical stuff: **where it is** and **how much
you have**. This page explains how Carbon models your warehouse — locations, then shelves
and bins inside them — how it tracks the on-hand quantity of every [item](/manual/overview/glossary),
and how it keeps a complete history of every movement in the **item ledger**. You'll also
learn to correct a count that's wrong (an *inventory adjustment*) and to verify stock with a
*cycle count*.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Stock is the thing every other part of Carbon pushes and pulls.
  [Receiving a purchase order](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving) *adds* stock; a
  [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) *consumes* raw material and *produces* finished parts;
  a [shipment](/manual/inventory/shipping-receiving) *removes* stock when it goes out the
  door. This page is the foundation underneath all of that — it's how Carbon answers
  "do we have it, and where?"
</Callout>

## The two questions inventory answers

Throughout this page we'll follow Acme's stock of **A36 Steel Plate 1″** (part number
`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`) — the raw material that becomes the
[Counterweight Hook](/manual/walkthrough) on job `J000097`. Acme buys it by the pound, stores
it on a rack, and pulls 40 lb into every hook it makes.

Two simple numbers — *17,507 lb* and *bin R02-B01-01* — but Carbon tracks them for every
item, in every location, and remembers every transaction that ever changed them. Let's build
that up one layer at a time.

## Layer 1 — Locations

A **location** is a physical site where you hold stock: a warehouse, a plant, a building.
Most small shops run a single location; larger ones run several. Acme has **two**:

Locations matter because **on-hand quantity is always *per location***. The same item can
have 17,507 lb at Headquarters and a different amount in Texas, and Carbon never mixes them
up. When you create a [sales order](/manual/selling/sales-orders),
[purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) or [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs), you
pick the location it draws from or delivers to — every hero-thread document for the
Counterweight Hook uses **Headquarters** (`loc_CZUGEFr…`).

<Callout type="tip">
  Moving stock between locations is a **transfer**, not an adjustment — Carbon has dedicated
  *stock transfer* and *warehouse transfer* records so the quantity that leaves one site is
  the exact quantity that arrives at the other. Reach for those when relocating physical
  stock; reserve adjustments (below) for *correcting* a count, not moving it.
</Callout>

## Layer 2 — Shelves & bins (storage units)

Inside a location, Carbon lets you pinpoint exactly where something sits using **storage
units** — the general term for any shelf, rack, row or bin. They can nest: a *warehouse*
contains *rows*, a row contains *bins*. Acme's Headquarters mixes both styles:

- **Flat rack bins** named by position — for example `R02-B01-01` means **R**ack 02, Row **B**,
  Level **01**, Position **01**. Acme has many of these spread across racks `R01`–`R05`.
- **Nested storage** — a broad "A Warehouse" zone that contains "Row H", which in turn
  contains a specific bin "H-12".

A storage unit is just a label for a place — naming it well (a consistent
`Rack-Row-Level-Position` scheme like Acme's) is what makes a part findable on a busy floor.
Each item can have a **default storage unit**, so Carbon suggests the right bin automatically
when you receive or pick it. The A36 Steel Plate's default is `R02-B01-01`
(`KgTNPORwzkxDG2z_wGLcI`).

<Callout type="info">
  **Item vs. storage unit.** An *item* is *what* the thing is (A36 Steel Plate 1″). A
  *storage unit* is *where* a quantity of it sits (bin `R02-B01-01`). One item can live in
  several bins; one bin can hold several items. The combination — item × location × storage
  unit — is what Carbon counts.
</Callout>

  **Available** is the number that actually matters for promising work: roughly
  *on hand minus what's committed, plus what's confidently inbound*. With 100 hooks on hand

  ahead. For a *buy-to-stock* material like A36 plate, a low available number is the trigger
  to reorder.
</Callout>

## The item ledger — every movement, forever

Behind those live quantities sits the **item ledger**: an append-only log of every single
transaction that has ever changed an item's stock. Receipts, job consumption, job output,
shipments, adjustments — each writes one entry. The on-hand quantity isn't stored as a
guess; it's the **running sum of the ledger**. That's why Carbon's numbers reconcile: you can
always trace a balance back to the events that produced it.

  The rows above illustrate the *shape* of a ledger — a positive receipt, the 20,000 lb that
  job `J000097` consumed to make 500 hooks (40 lb × 500), then a count that confirmed the
  balance. Every entry names the **document that caused it**, so the ledger doubles as an
  audit trail: "who moved this, when, and why" is never a mystery.
</Callout>

## Batch & lot tracking (a quick note)

Some items demand more than a quantity — they need **traceability** down to the specific
batch you received. A36 Steel Plate is one: it has `requiresBatchTracking` turned on. That
means when you receive it, Carbon records a **batch number** (often the supplier's heat or
lot number), and when a job consumes it, Carbon remembers *which batch* went into *which
finished parts*.

- A **batch** (or **lot**) is a quantity received together that shares a property — a steel
  *heat*, a chemical *mix date*, a casting *pour*.
- **Serial tracking** goes one step further: every individual unit gets its own unique
  serial number (think a numbered turbine blade), rather than a shared batch.

The payoff is **genealogy**: months later you can answer "which heat of steel went into
Radiant Aerospace's order?" — or, if a supplier recalls a heat, "which of our finished parts
are affected?" — in seconds. Full detail lives in
[Tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).

## Fixing the numbers: inventory adjustments

Sometimes the physical count and Carbon's number disagree — a drop, a miscount, scrap that
was never recorded. An **inventory adjustment** is how you make Carbon match reality. It
writes one ledger entry (so the change is traceable) and posts the value difference to the
**Inventory Adjustment** account (`5310`) in the books, keeping accounting honest too.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the item at the right location.** Find the item (for example A36 Steel Plate 1″) and
make sure you're looking at the correct location — adjustments are always per location.
Adjusting at Headquarters never touches Texas.
</Step>

<Step>
**Start a new adjustment.** Choose *Adjust quantity* (sometimes labelled *Positive
adjustment* / *Negative adjustment*). Pick the **storage unit** the change happened in — say
`R02-B01-01`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the corrected quantity, not the delta.** Tell Carbon what's *actually* there. If the
system says 17,507 lb but you physically counted 17,450 lb, enter the new on-hand and Carbon
computes the −57 lb movement for you. If the item is batch-tracked, pick **which batch** you're
adjusting.
</Step>

<Step>
**Record a reason and post.** Pick a reason (damaged, miscount, found stock) and a note so
the entry explains itself later. Posting writes the ledger entry, updates the on-hand
quantity, and books the value change.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  Adjustments are for **corrections**, not routine flow. Don't adjust stock down to "use it
  up" — that's what consuming material on a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) is for — and
  don't adjust it to move stock between sites — that's a transfer. Misusing adjustments hides
  the real reason stock changed and muddies your costs. Each adjustment should answer a clear
  "why was the count wrong?"
</Callout>

## Verifying the numbers: cycle counts

A **cycle count** is a scheduled physical count of part of your inventory — a few bins at a
time, rotating through everything over weeks, rather than shutting the whole shop for one big
annual count. The goal is to catch drift early and keep the ledger trustworthy without
stopping production.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Decide what to count.** Pick a slice — one rack, one item class, or your highest-value
materials. Acme might count the steel racks (`R02-B01-*`) one week and the stainless racks
the next. Counting your costliest stock most often (an "ABC" approach) catches the errors
that matter most.
</Step>

<Step>
**Print or pull the count sheet.** Carbon lists what it *thinks* is in each bin. A counter
walks the floor and writes down what's *actually* there — ideally without seeing the expected
number, so the count stays honest.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the counted quantities.** Type each counted figure back in. Carbon compares it to the
expected on-hand and flags any **variance**. A bin with no variance needs nothing further.
</Step>

<Step>
**Reconcile variances into adjustments.** For each bin that differs, Carbon posts an
inventory adjustment (the same mechanism as above) so the ledger, the on-hand quantity, and
the books all move together. After posting, Carbon's number once again matches the shelf.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Treat repeat variances as a signal, not just a number to fix. If the same bin is always
  short, the cause is usually upstream — material consumed but never recorded on a job, scrap
  that skipped the system, or a picking step done by hand. Counting tells you *that* you're
  off; the [item ledger](#the-item-ledger--every-movement-forever) tells you *why*.
</Callout>
