Carbon always knows two things about your stuff: **where it is** and **how much you have**.
This page shows how the Inventory module tracks both.

## Where this lives in Carbon

This all lives in the **Inventory** module. Open it, then go to **Quantities** to see
on-hand stock for every [item](/manual/overview/glossary), with its reorder policy.

## Locations

A **location** is a physical site where you hold stock. Small shops run one; bigger ones run
several. Acme has **two**:

      { name: "Texas", id: "loc_DWGsF8R…", role: "Secondary site — laser & burn tables" },

On-hand is **always per location**. The same item can have one amount at Headquarters and
another in Texas. Carbon never mixes them. Acme builds the Counterweight Hook at **Headquarters**.

<Callout type="tip">
  Moving stock between sites is a **transfer**, not an adjustment. Use a stock transfer so the
  amount that leaves one site is exactly the amount that arrives at the other.
</Callout>

## Storage units (shelves & bins)

Inside a location, a **storage unit** pins down exactly where something sits — a shelf, rack,
row, or bin. They can nest (a warehouse holds rows, a row holds bins).

Acme names bins by position. `R02-B01-01` means **R**ack 02, Row **B**, Level **01**,
Position **01**. The A36 Steel Plate lives in `R02-B01-01`. That bin is its **default
storage unit**, so Carbon suggests it automatically when you receive or pick the plate.

<Callout type="info">
  **Item vs. storage unit.** An *item* is *what* the thing is (A36 Steel Plate). A *storage
  unit* is *where* a quantity of it sits (bin `R02-B01-01`). One item can live in several
  bins; one bin can hold several items.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Available** is the number to watch when promising work: roughly *on hand minus committed,
  plus confidently inbound*. For a buy-to-stock material like A36 plate, a low available number
  is your cue to reorder. The hook is **Make-to-Order**, so Carbon builds each batch on a job
  instead of stocking ahead.
</Callout>

## The item ledger

Behind those live numbers sits the **item ledger** — a log of every stock move, ever.
Receipts, job consumption, shipments, and adjustments each write one row. On-hand is just the
**running total of the ledger**, so every number traces back to the move that caused it.

Every row names the document that caused it, so the ledger doubles as an audit trail: who
moved this, when, and why.

<Callout type="info">
  **Batch tracking.** A36 Steel Plate has batch tracking on, so Carbon records a **batch
  number** (often the supplier's heat number) on receipt and remembers which batch went into
  which finished parts. That's **genealogy** — full detail in
  [Tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).
</Callout>

## Fix a count: inventory adjustment

When the shelf and Carbon disagree, an **inventory adjustment** makes Carbon match reality.
It writes one ledger entry and posts the value change to the books.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Open the item at the right location.** Adjustments are per location — adjusting at
Headquarters never touches Texas.
</Step>

<Step>
**Start the adjustment.** Choose *Adjust quantity* and pick the **storage unit**, e.g.
`R02-B01-01`.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the corrected quantity, not the difference.** If Carbon says 17,507 LB but you
counted 17,450, type the new on-hand — Carbon works out the −57 LB. If the item is
batch-tracked, pick which batch.
</Step>

<Step>
**Add a reason and post.** Pick a reason (damaged, miscount, found stock). Posting updates
on-hand, writes the ledger entry, and books the value change.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="warn">
  Adjustments are for **corrections** only. Don't adjust stock down to "use it up" (that's
  consuming material on a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs)) or to move it between sites
  (that's a transfer).
</Callout>

## Verify counts: cycle count

A **cycle count** is a scheduled count of part of your inventory — a few bins at a time,
rotating through everything — so you catch drift without stopping production.

<Steps>

<Step>
**Pick what to count.** A rack, an item class, or your highest-value stock. Counting the
costliest items most often (an "ABC" approach) catches the errors that matter most.
</Step>

<Step>
**Pull the count sheet and walk the floor.** A counter writes down what's actually in each
bin — ideally without seeing the expected number, so the count stays honest.
</Step>

<Step>
**Enter the counted quantities.** Carbon compares each to the expected on-hand and flags any
**variance**.
</Step>

<Step>
**Post the variances.** For each bin that differs, Carbon posts an adjustment so the ledger,
on-hand, and books all move together.
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  Treat a bin that's always short as a signal, not just a number to fix — the cause is usually
  upstream (material used but never recorded on a job, or scrap that skipped the system).
</Callout>
