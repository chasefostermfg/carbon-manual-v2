A **material** is the raw stock you buy and then cut, machine or form into parts — a steel
plate, an aluminum billet, a length of brass bar. In Carbon a material is a special kind of
[**item**](/manual/overview/glossary), built to describe physical stock the way a metals
distributor sells it: a *substance* (what alloy), a *form* (plate, bar, tube…), a *grade*,
and *dimensions*. This page explains what makes a material different from a part, how Carbon
names and organizes them, and how to create one — using Acme's real steel, stainless,
aluminum and Inconel stock as the example throughout.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Materials are the raw stock that feeds your
  [methods](/manual/items/methods) and [jobs](/manual/manufacturing/jobs). On the hero
  thread, the Counterweight Hook (`CWH-A36-15`) is a *Part* made from one material —
  **A36 Steel Plate 1″** — which Acme buys from [Ryerson](/manual/buying/suppliers) on
  purchase order [PO000057](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics). Define the material here,
  and it's ready to drop into a bill of materials.
</Callout>

## Material vs. Part: what's the difference?

Both a **part** and a **material** are *items*, so they share the same plumbing — a unique
ID, a unit of measure, costs, inventory tracking, suppliers. The difference is what they
*describe* and how you usually replenish them.

The line gets blurry on purpose — a material *can* be made (some shops melt and pour their
own), and a part *can* be bought. What you pick mostly drives the defaults Carbon shows you.
The practical reason to model raw stock as a **material** rather than a generic part is that
Carbon then understands its anatomy: it knows "A36 plate, 1 inch thick, 96×240 sheet" is a
single substance in three descriptive pieces, so it can group, search and reorder stock the
way your buyers actually think.

<Callout type="tip">
  If you're unsure whether something is a part, a material, a tool, a consumable or a
  service, see the [item type overview](/manual/items/defining-an-item). Materials are the
  right choice for **anything you cut, machine, weld or form from raw stock**.
</Callout>

Every Carbon material is described by four attributes. Acme's catalog uses a tidy naming

### Substances

A **substance** is the underlying material — the alloy or compound. Carbon ships with 17
system-defined substances, and you can add your own. Acme's catalog draws on **six** of
them — steel, stainless steel, aluminum, brass, Inconel and titanium. A few from Acme's
shelves:

sold by area or weight, bar by length, tube by length and wall thickness.

### Grades and dimensions

**Grade** captures the temper or spec — `T6` on aluminum, the alloy designation baked into
the name (304 vs 316 stainless, A36 vs 4140 steel). **Dimensions** capture the physical size:
plate thickness and sheet size (96×240 inches), bar diameter, tube wall. In Acme's catalog,
grade and dimension live inside the readable ID rather than as separate lookup fields, which
keeps the part list scannable.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Reading an Acme material ID.** `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240` decodes as: **A36** (grade) ·
  **STEEL** (substance) · **PLT** (form = plate) · **1″** (thickness) · **96×240** (sheet
  size in inches). Once you can read the pattern, the whole catalog becomes self-describing.
</Callout>

## Cut-to-size stock

Most raw stock isn't consumed whole — you cut a piece off a larger plate or bar. Carbon models
this naturally: the material is the *full* stock item (a 96×240 sheet of 1-inch A36), and each
job consumes only the **quantity it needs**, expressed in the material's unit of measure.

On the hero thread, the Counterweight Hook's [method](/manual/items/methods) pulls **40 LB** of
A36 plate per hook. A batch of 500 hooks therefore needs **20,000 LB** — not 500 whole sheets.
Carbon tracks the running pounds on hand and consumes against it, so a single sheet feeds many
parts and you always know how much usable plate is left.

<Callout type="tip">
  Buying stock in one unit and consuming it in another is common — you might purchase plate by
  the *sheet* but consume it by the *pound*. Carbon supports a purchase unit and an inventory
  unit with a conversion between them. Acme keeps it simple and uses **LB** for both on its
  steel. See [purchasing basics](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) for the buy side.
</Callout>

## Batch and lot tracking

For materials, **where the metal came from** often matters as much as how much you have. A
**batch** (or *lot*, or *heat* for steel) is a group of stock that arrived together from one
supplier shipment. Turning on batch tracking tells Carbon to record a batch number every time
the material is received, and to carry that number forward into every part made from it.

Acme's A36 plate has `requiresBatchTracking` switched on. That single flag means:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **At receipt**, Carbon asks for (or generates) a batch number when the steel arrives from
    Ryerson on [PO000057](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics). The pounds are added to inventory
    *tagged* with that batch.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **During the job**, when 20,000 LB of plate is issued to job J000097, Carbon records which
    batch (or batches) was consumed.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Forever after**, the finished Counterweight Hooks carry a link back to that exact heat of
    steel. If Ryerson later reports a problem with that heat, Acme can answer "which orders did
    that steel ship in?" in seconds.
  </Step>
</Steps>

This chain — supplier shipment → material batch → job → finished part → customer — is what
manufacturers call **traceability** (or *genealogy*). It's a requirement in aerospace, defense
and medical work, and it's exactly why Radiant Aerospace cares that Acme runs batch-tracked
steel. Read more in [Tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).

<Callout type="warn">
  Decide on batch (or [serial](/manual/overview/glossary)) tracking **before** you start
  receiving a material. Switching it on after stock already exists in inventory is messy,
  because the existing quantity has no batch number attached. For regulated work, turn it on
  when you first create the material.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
  **Batch vs. serial.** *Batch* tracking groups many units under one number — right for bulk
  stock like plate and bar where individual pounds are interchangeable. *Serial* tracking gives
  every single unit its own number — right for high-value finished parts you must track
  one-by-one. Materials almost always use batch tracking.
</Callout>

## Create a material

Here's how Acme added the 1-inch A36 plate. You'll create materials the same way for steel,
stainless, aluminum, brass — anything you cut from raw stock.

<Steps>
  <Step>
    **Open Items and start a new material.** In the **Items** module, choose **New → Material**.
    Picking *Material* (rather than Part) gives you the substance/form/grade/dimension fields
    instead of a blank part record.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Pick the substance, form and grade.** Choose **Steel** as the substance and **Plate (PLT)**
    as the form from Carbon's built-in lists. Enter the grade — **A36** — and the dimensions:
    **1″** thickness, **96 × 240** inch sheet. Carbon assembles these into a suggested readable
    ID following your naming pattern (here, `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`).

    <AppShot title="New Material" url="app.carbon.ms/x/items/material/new" caption="Substance, form, grade and dimensions build the material.">
      <FieldGrid cols={2}>
        <Field label="Substance" value="Steel" />
        <Field label="Form" value="Plate (PLT)" />
        <Field label="Grade" value="A36" />
        <Field label="Thickness" value={"1\""} />
        <Field label="Dimensions" value={"96\" × 240\""} />
        <Field label="Readable ID" value={"A36-STEEL-PLT-1\".96x240"} mono />
      </FieldGrid>
    </AppShot>
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Set the unit of measure.** Choose **LB (Pound)** — the unit you'll buy and consume the
    plate in. This is the unit every quantity for this material is expressed in, from purchase
    orders to bills of materials to on-hand stock.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Set replenishment to Buy.** Because Acme buys plate rather than making it, set the
    [replenishment system](/manual/items/replenishment) to **Buy**. This unlocks the supplier
    and reorder settings and tells Carbon to suggest a purchase order when stock runs low.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Turn on batch tracking (if needed).** For traceable stock like aerospace steel, switch on
    **Requires batch tracking** now — before any inventory exists. Acme leaves this on for all
    its structural metals.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Add the default supplier.** On the **Suppliers** tab, add **Ryerson** as the source, with
    the supplier's price (Acme pays **$0.25/LB**). Now the material is ready to be reordered and
    to appear on a [purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) like PO000057.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    **Save.** The material is now a first-class item: it can be dropped onto any
    [method](/manual/items/methods), stocked in any [location](/manual/inventory/locations-and-stock),
    and bought from any supplier. The hero thread's hook is now buildable.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Reuse, don't duplicate.** Before creating a material, search the catalog — Acme already has
  A36 plate in 1″, 2″, 3″ and 4″ thicknesses. If the exact substance + form + size already
  exists, use it. Only create a new material when the physical stock is genuinely different.
</Callout>
