Almost everything in Carbon starts with an **item**. An item is a single, named thing you
make, buy, sell, or use in your shop — a finished part, a slab of steel, a drill bit, a
service you charge for. Every quote line, sales order, job, purchase order and invoice
ultimately points back to an item, so getting items right is the foundation of everything
else. This page shows you what an item *is*, the six kinds Carbon recognizes, and exactly
how to create one — using real parts from Acme Inc.'s shop.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** Items are the very first thing you set up in Carbon, before you can
  quote, sell, make, or buy anything — they're one of the
  [core concepts](/manual/overview/core-concepts) the whole system is built on. Once an item
  exists, you give it a [**method**](/manual/items/methods) (how it's made) and a
  [**replenishment**](/manual/items/replenishment) rule (how you keep it in stock). After
  this page, those two are your natural next stops.
</Callout>

## What an item is

Think of an item as a record card for one thing. It carries a unique
[**part number**](/manual/overview/glossary), a description, and a handful of settings that
tell Carbon how to treat it everywhere it shows up — what unit to count it in, whether to
track stock of it, what it costs, what you sell it for, and whether you **make** it or
**buy** it.

You only ever describe an item *once*. From then on, the Counterweight Hook is "the
Counterweight Hook" on every quote, job and invoice — Carbon reuses the same record, so a
price change or a spelling fix happens in exactly one place.

## The six item types

When you create an item, the first thing you choose is its **type**. The type tells Carbon
what role the item plays in your business. Acme's shop uses all six.

    { key: "example", label: "Acme example", mono: true },

    { type: <Tag>Material</Tag>, what: "Raw stock you buy and consume to make parts", example: "A36-STEEL-PLT-1\".96x240" },
    { type: <Tag>Tool</Tag>, what: "Durable shop equipment used across many jobs", example: "TOL-CAL (Digital Calipers)" },
    { type: <Tag>Consumable</Tag>, what: "Low-cost supplies used up during work", example: "GRD-WHL (Grinding Wheels)" },
    { type: <Tag>Service</Tag>, what: "Work performed (yours or a supplier's), not a physical good", example: "Outside plating / anodizing" },
    { type: <Tag>Fixture</Tag>, what: "A jig or holding device that positions a part during an operation", example: "Flame-cut weldment fixture" },
  ]}
/>

The two types you'll use most are **Part** and **Material**, and they sit at opposite ends
of the same job:

- A **Part** is the thing you deliver. Acme's hero part, the **Counterweight Hook**
  (`CWH-A36-15`), is a Part — a finished steel hook it makes for Radiant Aerospace.
- A **Material** is the raw stock you consume to make a Part. The hook is flame-cut from
  **A36 Steel Plate 1″** (`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`), which is a Material.

<Callout type="tip">
  **Tool vs Consumable** is just durability and value. A **Tool** (a torque wrench, a
  micrometer) is reused for years and worth tracking individually. A **Consumable** (sandpaper,
  shop rags, welding wire) is cheap and used up — you track it by quantity, not by
  serial number. When in doubt, ask "would I notice if one walked off?" — if yes, it's
  probably a Tool.
</Callout>

## Anatomy of an item: the key fields

Every item type shares the same core fields. Here's what each one means and why it matters.

### Part number (readableId)

The **part number** — Carbon's field name is `readableId` — is the short, human-friendly
code you and your customers use to refer to the item. Acme's are descriptive:
`CWH-A36-15` reads as *Counterweight Hook, A36 steel*. Materials follow a pattern of
`{grade}-{form}-{thickness}".{sheet size}`, so `A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240` is *A36 steel
plate, 1 inch thick, 96″×240″ sheet*.

Pick a convention and stick to it — a readable, consistent scheme pays off the moment your
item list grows past a few dozen. The part number must be unique within your company.

<Callout type="info">
  An item can also carry a **revision** (the Counterweight Hook is revision `15`). Carbon
  shows the combined value as `readableIdWithRevision`. Revisions let you change a design
  over time while keeping the history of older versions intact.
</Callout>

### Description

A plain-language name — "Counterweight Hook", "A36 Steel Plate 1″". This is what prints on
quotes, orders and invoices, so write it for the reader, not the engineer.

### Unit of measure (UoM)

How you **count** the item. The Counterweight Hook is counted in `EA` (Each); the steel
plate is bought and consumed in `LB` (Pounds). The unit of measure flows everywhere: when
the hook's method says it needs "40 LB" of steel per piece, Carbon knows that "40" means
pounds because the steel's UoM is `LB`.

### Item tracking type

This decides *how much detail* Carbon keeps about your stock of the item. There are four
modes, from least to most granular:

  columns={[
    { key: "mode", label: "Tracking type" },
    { key: "means", label: "What Carbon tracks" },
    { key: "use", label: "Use it for" },
  ]}
  rows={[
    { mode: <Tag>Inventory</Tag>, means: "A running on-hand quantity by location", use: "Most stocked parts and materials" },

/>

The Counterweight Hook is **Inventory**-tracked: Carbon keeps a running count of how many
finished hooks are on hand. The A36 steel plate is **Batch**-tracked
(`requiresBatchTracking: true`) — because when a quality question comes up later, Acme needs
to know *which heat of steel* went into Radiant's order. That batch link is the start of
full [traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).

<Callout type="warn">
  Tracking type is hard to change once an item has transaction history, so choose it when you
  create the item. If you're unsure, **Inventory** is the safe default for physical goods and
  **Non-Inventory** for services.
</Callout>

### Unit cost and sale price

- **Unit cost** is what the item costs *you* — for a Material, the price you pay your
  supplier; for a Part you make, the cost Carbon rolls up from its method (materials plus
  shop time). Acme buys A36 plate from Ryerson at **$0.25/LB**.
- **Sale price** is what you charge the customer. The Counterweight Hook sells to Radiant
  Aerospace at **$73.14 EA**.

These are *defaults*. A specific quote or order can override the price, but the item-level
figures give Carbon something to work with the moment you add the item to a document.

### Make vs Buy (replenishment)

The **replenishment system** answers one question: when you need more of this item, do you
**make** it or **buy** it?

<Mermaid
  chart={`flowchart LR
  I[Need more of an item] --> Q{Make or Buy?}
  Q -->|Make| J[Carbon raises a Job]
  Q -->|Buy| P[Carbon raises a Purchase Order]`}
/>

- **Make** items get a [**method**](/manual/items/methods) (a recipe) and are produced on a
  [**job**](/manual/manufacturing/jobs). The Counterweight Hook is a *Make* item.
- **Buy** items get suppliers and prices and are replenished with a
  [**purchase order**](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics). The A36 steel plate is a *Buy*
  item — Acme orders it from Ryerson.

This single setting is why the hero thread works: because the hook is *Make*, an order for
it creates a job; because the steel is *Buy*, that job's shortage creates a purchase order.
You'll tune the finer details (reorder points, lead times) in
[Replenishment & planning](/manual/items/replenishment).

## Create a Part, step by step

Let's create the Counterweight Hook the way Acme did. The flow is the same for any item
type — you just pick a different **Type** in step 2.

<Steps>

<Step>
### Open the new-item form

In the left rail, open the **Items** module and choose **Parts**, then click **New**. (Each
type has its own list — Parts, Materials, Tools, and so on — but they all open the same
create form.)
</Step>

<Step>
### Choose the type and enter the basics

Set **Type** to *Part*. Then fill in the part number, a description, and the unit of
measure. Acme enters `CWH-A36-15`, "Counterweight Hook", and `EA`.

<AppShot title="New Part" url="app.carbon.ms/x/part/new" caption="Creating the Counterweight Hook — the four fields every item needs.">
  <FieldGrid cols={2}>
    <Field label="Type" value="Part" />
    <Field label="Part number (readableId)" value="CWH-A36-15" mono />
    <Field label="Description" value="Counterweight Hook" />
    <Field label="Unit of measure" value="EA (Each)" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>
</Step>

<Step>
### Set tracking and replenishment

Choose how Carbon should track stock and whether you make or buy the item. For the hook,
that's **Inventory** tracking and **Make** replenishment.

<AppShot title="New Part — tracking & replenishment" url="app.carbon.ms/x/part/new">
  <FieldGrid cols={2}>
    <Field label="Item tracking type" value="Inventory" />
    <Field label="Replenishment system" value="Make" />
  </FieldGrid>
</AppShot>

<Callout type="tip">
  Because you chose **Make**, Carbon will expect a **method** for this part next — the recipe
  of materials and operations. You'll build it in [Methods: BOM & routing](/manual/items/methods).
  A **Buy** part would instead prompt you for suppliers and purchase prices.
</Callout>
</Step>

<Step>
### Save — and you're done

Click **Save**. Carbon creates the item, marks it **Active**, and opens its detail page.
That's the whole foundation: from here you can add the method, set prices, and start
quoting. The hook now exists as a single record that every future document will reuse.
</Step>

</Steps>
