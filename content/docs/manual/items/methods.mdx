A **method** is Carbon's word for the recipe that makes a part. Once you've created an
[item](/manual/items/defining-an-item) that you *make* (rather than buy), the method is how
you teach Carbon what that part is made *of* and what steps turn raw stock into a finished
piece. Get the method right once, and every future quote, job and cost estimate for that
part is built from it automatically — no re-typing, no guesswork.

This page builds the method for Acme's hero part, the **Counterweight Hook**
(`CWH-A36-15`), the same heavy steel hook that runs all the way through the
[15-minute walkthrough](/manual/walkthrough). By the end you'll know how to add materials,
lay out a sequence of operations, point each step at the right machine, and keep older
versions of a method around as the part evolves.

<Callout type="info">
  **Where this fits.** A method lives on an item. So the order is: first
  [define the item](/manual/items/defining-an-item), then give it a method (this page), then
  Carbon can [quote it](/manual/selling/quoting) and turn an order into a
  [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs) on the shop floor. A method is the bridge between *"we
  sell this"* and *"here's exactly how we build it."*
</Callout>

## The two halves of a method

Every method is made of two parts that work together. Keep them straight and the rest of
this page is easy:

- The **bill of materials (BOM)** — *what the part is made of.* A list of the materials,
  sub-parts and sub-assemblies that go into one finished piece, each with a quantity. For
  the hook, that's a single line: 40 lb of A36 steel plate.
- The **routing** — *how the part is made.* An ordered list of [operations](/manual/overview/glossary)
  (shop-floor steps), each run at a [work center](/manual/overview/glossary) and each with a
  time. For the hook: flame cut, then sandblast, then inspect.

## Build the bill of materials

The BOM answers one question: *to make one of these, what do I consume?* You add a line for
every material or sub-part, with the quantity needed for a **single** finished piece. Carbon
does the multiplication later — when a job is for 500 hooks, it scales every BOM line by 500.

For each material line you also choose how Carbon should *get* that material when a job
runs. This is the line's **method type**, and the two you'll use most are:

- **Pull from Inventory** — the material is already stocked (or will be); the job pulls it
  off the shelf. This is what the hook uses for its steel plate.
- **Make** — the material is itself something you manufacture, so it has its own method.
  That's a **sub-assembly** (more on that below).

<Steps>

<Step>
### Open the part's Manufacturing tab

Go to **Items**, open the Counterweight Hook (`CWH-A36-15`), and select the
**Manufacturing** tab. A brand-new make part starts with an empty method — just the root
part and nowhere for materials or operations yet.
</Step>

<Step>
### Add a material line

Under **Bill of Materials**, click **Add Material**. Search for and pick the raw material —
here, **A36 Steel Plate 1″** (`A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240`). Carbon pulls in its description and
unit of measure automatically.
</Step>

<Step>
### Set the quantity per piece

Enter how much one finished hook consumes: **40** in the unit **LB** (pounds). This is the
*per-each* figure. Don't multiply by your batch size — Carbon scales it when it creates a
job.
</Step>

<Step>
### Choose the method type

Set the line to **Pull from Inventory**. That tells Carbon the steel comes off the shelf
rather than being made. (If stock runs short when a job is planned, Carbon flags the
shortage so you can raise a [purchase order](/manual/buying/purchasing-basics) — exactly what
happened on `PO000057` to Ryerson in the walkthrough.)
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **40 lb per piece, 20,000 lb per job.** Because you entered the quantity *per piece*, a job
  for 500 hooks automatically needs 40 × 500 = **20,000 lb** of plate. Enter the small number
  once and let Carbon scale it — that's the whole point of a per-each BOM.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn">
  A36 steel plate is **batch-tracked** at Acme. That means each receipt of plate gets a batch
  number, and when a job consumes it, Carbon records exactly which batch went into which
  hooks. You don't set that up on the method — it's a property of the *material item* — but
  it's why the hook earns full [traceability](/manual/quality/tracking-quality) downstream.
</Callout>

## Build the routing

The routing is the ordered list of shop-floor steps. Each step is an **operation**, and an
operation answers four things: *what* you do, *where* you do it, *how long* it takes, and
*when* it can start relative to the other steps.

A couple of terms before we add the steps:

- A [**work center**](/manual/overview/glossary) is a specific machine or station — for
  example *Burn Table — PA2* or *Inspection Station*. It carries its own labor and machine
  rates, which Carbon uses to cost the operation.
- A [**process**](/manual/overview/glossary) is the *kind* of work — *Flame Cut*,
  *Sandblasting*, *Quality Inspection*. A work center can perform one or more processes.
  When you add an operation you pick the process, and Carbon offers the work centers that can
  run it.

Here's how to add each one.

<Steps>

<Step>
### Add the first operation

Under **Routing**, click **Add Operation**. Pick the process **Flame Cut**, then choose the
work center **Burn Table — PA2**. Carbon assigns it sequence number **10** — routings are
numbered in tens (10, 20, 30…) so you have room to slot a step in between later without
renumbering everything.
</Step>

<Step>
### Enter setup and run time

Every operation can carry two kinds of time:

- **Setup time** — a one-time amount per job, no matter the quantity (e.g. fixturing a part,
  loading a program). The hook's flame cut has **0** setup.
- **Run time** (labor time) — the per-piece time to actually do the work. For flame cut,
  that's **45 Minutes/Piece**.

The *unit* matters as much as the number. `Minutes/Piece` multiplies by the job quantity;
`Total Minutes` does not. So 45 Minutes/Piece on a 500-piece job is 375 hours of cutting,
while a setup entered as Total Minutes stays fixed. Carbon uses these times plus the work
center's rates to estimate the job's cost and schedule.
</Step>

<Step>
### Set the operation order

Leave the order as **After Previous**. That tells Carbon this step can't start until the one
before it finishes — the natural sequence for the hook (you can't sandblast before you've
cut). The alternative, *With Previous*, lets two steps run in parallel; you'd use it when,
say, two stations work the same part at once.
</Step>

<Step>
### Add the remaining operations

Repeat for the other two steps, in order:

- **20 · Sandblasting** — process *Sandblasting*, work center *Sand Blasting Room*,
  **2 Minutes/Piece**, *After Previous*.
- **30 · Inspection** — process *Quality Inspection*, work center *Inspection Station*,
  **10 Minutes/Piece**, *After Previous*.

The last step being an inspection is deliberate: it's the quality gate before the hooks can
ship. More on that in [Tracking quality](/manual/quality/tracking-quality).
</Step>

</Steps>

<Callout type="tip">
  **Inside vs. outside operations.** All three hook operations are *Inside* — Acme does them
  in its own shop. If a step is sent to a vendor (anodizing, heat treat, plating), you'd mark
  it an **Outside** operation, and Carbon can raise a purchase order to the subcontractor for
  that service. See [Advanced purchasing](/manual/buying/advanced-purchasing).
</Callout>

Because setup and run time behave differently, it's worth seeing what they do to a real
batch. For the hook's flame-cut step at a 500-piece job:

The takeaway: **run time scales with quantity, setup doesn't.** Spreading a fixed setup over
more pieces is why larger batches usually cost less per piece — and Carbon does that math for
you the moment you set a job quantity.

## Multi-level methods (sub-assemblies)

The hook is a *single-level* method — raw material straight to finished part. But many
products are built from sub-parts that are themselves manufactured. Carbon handles this with
**multi-level methods**: a BOM line can point at another *make* part, which brings its own
BOM and routing along.

The mechanism is the material line's **method type** again. Set a BOM line to **Make** (instead
of *Pull from Inventory*) and that line becomes a **sub-assembly** — Carbon nests the child
part's whole method underneath it in the tree. When a job runs, Carbon can create
sub-jobs to build those sub-assemblies first.

Here's the *idea*, using a made-up two-level part so you can see the shape (the hook itself
stays single-level):

  You don't redefine a sub-assembly's recipe each time you use it. Define its method once on
  its own item, then drop it into as many parent BOMs as you like. Update it in one place and
  every parent picks up the change on its next job.
</Callout>

## Versions and revisions

Methods aren't frozen — parts change. Carbon keeps history so a change today doesn't rewrite
what you already built and shipped.

- A **revision** is a deliberate, named iteration of the part itself (Rev 0, Rev A, Rev B…),
  usually driven by an engineering change. The hook is at revision **0**. Each revision can
  carry its own method, so you can quote and build an old revision long after a new one
  exists.
- A **version** is Carbon's internal snapshot of a method. When a [job](/manual/manufacturing/jobs)
  is created, it takes its **own copy** of the method as it stands at that moment — a *job make
  method*. After that, editing the part's master method does **not** change a job already in
  flight.

This copy-on-create behavior is the safety net behind the whole walkthrough: job `J000097`
made 500 hooks against the method exactly as it was the day it was released, even if
engineering tweaks the master method next month.

<Callout type="warn">
  **Editing the master method only affects future jobs.** If a hook already running on the
  shop floor needs a routing change, edit it on **that job's** method, not (only) on the part.
  Changing the part's master method updates the recipe new jobs will copy — it does not reach
  back into jobs already created.
</Callout>
