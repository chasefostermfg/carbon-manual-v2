What is Carbon?

ERP, MES and QMS in one system of record — and who uses each part.

Carbon is software for running a manufacturing business. It tracks your orders, parts, materials, and money in one place. Write something down once, and Carbon carries it everywhere. Nobody re-types anything.

Most shops glue this together from spreadsheets, an accounting program, paper travelers, and inspection forms. None of them talk to each other. Carbon replaces that pile with one system of record — one trusted place where the real state of the business lives.

Where this lives in Carbon

Carbon is organized into Modules — Sales, Purchasing, Items, Production, Inventory, Quality, Accounting, and more. Each Module is one job. You open a Module from the home screen, where every Module shows up as a tile.

You don't have to learn all of Carbon. Most people live in one or two Modules. Pick the tile for the work you do.

ERP, MES and QMS — three jobs, one system

People describe business software with three acronyms — ERP, MES, and QMS. Many shops buy three separate programs. Carbon does all three — not bolted together, but as three views of the same data.

ERP — run the business

The office side. Sales, purchasing, inventory and costing: who ordered what, what to buy, what's in stock, and what it all costs.

MES — run the shop floor

The plant side. Release jobs, clock onto operations, and record finished pieces — turning orders into real, made parts.

QMS — keep it good

The quality side. Inspections, non-conformances (NCRs) and full traceability — proving each part is right and tracing where it came from.

ERP — the office

ERP means Enterprise Resource Planning. In plain words, it's the office side. The Modules here let you:

  • Quote a customer, then turn the quote into a sales order when they say yes.
  • Raise purchase orders to suppliers for the materials you need.
  • Track inventory — what's on the shelf, where, and how much.
  • Roll up cost automatically, so you know if a price leaves a profit.

For Acme, ERP took Radiant Aerospace's order for 500 Counterweight Hooks (sales order SO000069, $36,820) and bought the steel from Ryerson (purchase order PO000057).

MES — the shop floor

MES means Manufacturing Execution System. It's what operators use on the floor. When the office releases a job (an order to make a quantity of a part), it shows up for an operator. They open it, clock on to an operation, and record finished pieces.

For Acme's hook, the floor ran three steps in order — Flame Cut, Sandblasting, then Inspection — all on job J000097.

QMS — quality

QMS means Quality Management System. It's how you make sure each part is right and can prove it later:

  • Inspections — a quality check, often built into a job as a step.
  • Non-conformances (NCRs) — a record you raise when something is wrong, so it gets fixed.
  • Traceability — the linked chain from finished part back to the exact batch of raw material. Carbon can answer "which heat of steel went into Radiant's hooks?" in seconds.

Who uses which Module

RoleSideDay to day
Estimators & salesERPBuild quotes, price work, turn won quotes into sales orders.
Buyers & purchasingERPRaise purchase orders, chase deliveries, receive material into stock.
Owners & managersERPWatch orders, costs, margins and cash across the business.
Machine operatorsMESOpen jobs on the floor, clock onto operations, record finished pieces.
Quality inspectorsQMSRun inspections, raise non-conformances, keep traceability intact.

Because it's one system, hand-offs are automatic. The estimator doesn't email the order to the floor — the order becomes a job. The inspector doesn't keep a separate binder — the inspection is a step on that same job.

One database underneath

The three sides feel like one tool because they sit on one database. A sales order, the job that fulfills it, the purchase order for its material, the inspection that passed it, and the invoice that bills it are all linked records.

What makes Carbon different

Everything lives together, so there's no re-keying and nothing drifts out of sync. A quote becomes a sales order becomes a job becomes a shipment becomes an invoice — each inheriting from the last. The walkthrough follows that chain for Acme's hook, from price request to paid invoice.

An API lets other software read and write your data. Carbon is API-first: every screen you click does the same thing a program could do through the API. So you can connect Carbon to your other tools and automate the boring parts. More in Using the API.

Carbon ships with a built-in MCP server — a safe doorway that lets an AI assistant operate Carbon for you (look things up, draft a quote, check a job) in plain language. The demo data in this manual was pulled through it. See MCP & AI.

Carbon's code is open source — anyone can read it, run it, and extend it. You can self-host, audit how it works, and shape it to your shop. More in Integrating with Carbon.

You don't need the API or AI features to get full value from Carbon. Day one, you can quote, order, make, buy, inspect and invoice entirely by clicking. New to a bolded term? Every one is defined in the glossary.

The one idea to remember

Carbon is one connected system of record for the whole business. ERP runs the office, MES runs the floor, QMS keeps it good — three views into the same data, organized as Modules. Information goes in once and follows the work from the first quote to the final payment.
