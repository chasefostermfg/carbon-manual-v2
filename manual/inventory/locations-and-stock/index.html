Locations & stock

Warehouses, shelves, on-hand quantities and the item ledger.

Carbon always knows two things about your stuff: where it is and how much you have. This page shows how the Inventory module tracks both.

Where this lives in Carbon

This all lives in the Inventory module. Open it, then go to Quantities to see on-hand stock for every item, with its reorder policy.

The four building blocks

Inventory is just four ideas stacked together:

Building blockWhat it is
LocationA physical site — a warehouse or plant. On-hand is always per location.
Storage unitA shelf, rack, or bin inside a location, like R02-B01-01.
On handHow much you physically have right now.
Item ledgerA log of every stock move, ever — the receipts and consumption behind the numbers.

We'll follow one item to make it real: Acme's stock of A36 Steel Plate 1″ (A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240), the raw material for the Counterweight Hook.

Locations

A location is a physical site where you hold stock. Small shops run one; bigger ones run several. Acme has two:

On-hand is always per location. The same item can have one amount at Headquarters and another in Texas. Carbon never mixes them. Acme builds the Counterweight Hook at Headquarters.

Moving stock between sites is a transfer, not an adjustment. Use a stock transfer so the amount that leaves one site is exactly the amount that arrives at the other.

Storage units (shelves & bins)

Inside a location, a storage unit pins down exactly where something sits — a shelf, rack, row, or bin. They can nest (a warehouse holds rows, a row holds bins).

Acme names bins by position. R02-B01-01 means Rack 02, Row B, Level 01, Position 01. The A36 Steel Plate lives in R02-B01-01. That bin is its default storage unit, so Carbon suggests it automatically when you receive or pick the plate.

Item vs. storage unit. An item is what the thing is (A36 Steel Plate). A storage unit is where a quantity of it sits (bin R02-B01-01). One item can live in several bins; one bin can hold several items.

On-hand quantities

On-hand is just one of several numbers Carbon tracks per item. Open the Counterweight Hook and check the Inventory tab: 100 are in stock, but 4,500 are already promised to Radiant Aerospace on open sales orders.

Here's what each number means:

QuantityWhat it means
On handPhysically in stock right now.
On sales-order demandPromised to customers, not yet shipped.
On production orderBeing made on an open job — becomes on hand when the job finishes.
On purchase orderOrdered from a supplier and inbound — not arrived yet.
On holdPresent but quarantined — e.g. failed inspection.

Available is the number to watch when promising work: roughly on hand minus committed, plus confidently inbound. For a buy-to-stock material like A36 plate, a low available number is your cue to reorder. The hook is Make-to-Order, so Carbon builds each batch on a job instead of stocking ahead.

The item ledger

Behind those live numbers sits the item ledger — a log of every stock move, ever. Receipts, job consumption, shipments, and adjustments each write one row. On-hand is just the running total of the ledger, so every number traces back to the move that caused it.

Every row names the document that caused it, so the ledger doubles as an audit trail: who moved this, when, and why.

Batch tracking. A36 Steel Plate has batch tracking on, so Carbon records a batch number (often the supplier's heat number) on receipt and remembers which batch went into which finished parts. That's genealogy — full detail in Tracking quality.

Fix a count: inventory adjustment

When the shelf and Carbon disagree, an inventory adjustment makes Carbon match reality. It writes one ledger entry and posts the value change to the books.

Open the item at the right location. Adjustments are per location — adjusting at Headquarters never touches Texas.

Start the adjustment. Choose Adjust quantity and pick the storage unit, e.g. R02-B01-01.

Enter the corrected quantity, not the difference. If Carbon says 17,507 LB but you counted 17,450, type the new on-hand — Carbon works out the −57 LB. If the item is batch-tracked, pick which batch.

Add a reason and post. Pick a reason (damaged, miscount, found stock). Posting updates on-hand, writes the ledger entry, and books the value change.

Adjustments are for corrections only. Don't adjust stock down to "use it up" (that's consuming material on a job) or to move it between sites (that's a transfer).

Verify counts: cycle count

A cycle count is a scheduled count of part of your inventory — a few bins at a time, rotating through everything — so you catch drift without stopping production.

Pick what to count. A rack, an item class, or your highest-value stock. Counting the costliest items most often (an "ABC" approach) catches the errors that matter most.

Pull the count sheet and walk the floor. A counter writes down what's actually in each bin — ideally without seeing the expected number, so the count stays honest.

Enter the counted quantities. Carbon compares each to the expected on-hand and flags any variance.

Post the variances. For each bin that differs, Carbon posts an adjustment so the ledger, on-hand, and books all move together.

Treat a bin that's always short as a signal, not just a number to fix — the cause is usually upstream (material used but never recorded on a job, or scrap that skipped the system).
