Advanced purchasing

Outside processing, RFQs, drop-ship and the supplier portal.

Purchasing basics covered the everyday loop: raise a PO, send it, receive the goods. This page covers the trickier buying jobs:

  • Send a part out for a coating (outside processing).
  • Shop several suppliers for the best price (RFQs).
  • Ship straight to your customer (drop-ship).
  • Let Carbon tell you what to buy (planning).

Where this lives in Carbon

Everything here lives in the Purchasing module. Open it from the home screen, then use the sub-nav for Purchase Orders, Supplier Quotes, and Planning.

Pick the tool you need today:

Outside processing

Send a part out for a service like anodizing, then take it back.

Supplier quotes (RFQs)

Ask several suppliers for a price and compare them.

Drop-ship

Have a supplier ship straight to your customer.

Outside processing

Some steps aren't done in-house. A part comes off your mill in great shape, but still needs anodizing or painting. You send it to a specialist, they do the work, and they ship it back. That round trip is outside processing.

In Carbon, it's just an operation in a part's routing — like Flame Cut or Inspection. The only difference: its type is Outside, not Inside.

  • Inside — runs at your own work center and costs you shop time.
  • Outside — runs at a supplier and costs you what the supplier charges.

Acme sends parts to two outside suppliers, both set as Subcontractors and Service Providers:

SupplierService
Allen's AnodizingAnodizing
Zebra PaintersPainting

Here an aluminum bracket is milled in-house, anodized at Allen's Anodizing, then inspected. Operation 20 has no work center and no labor minutes — it points at a supplier and carries a price.

Set up and run an outside operation

Add the operation and set its type to Outside. Open the part's Manufacturing tab, add the operation where the service belongs, and choose Outside as the type.

Point it at a supplier and a price. Pick the supplier — Allen's Anodizing — and enter what they charge. That price rolls up into the part's cost, just like in-house labor.

Release the job. The outside operation appears in the job's operation list with status Todo, in sequence between the inside steps.

Raise a PO for the service. When production reaches the outside step, create a purchase order to the supplier. Carbon links it back to the job, so it knows which parts left the building and why.

Ship out, then receive back. Send the parts to the supplier. When they return finished, record a receipt against the PO. That marks the outside operation complete and the job moves to the next step.

Outside parts are still your inventory. While parts are at the anodizer, they're work-in-progress that's temporarily off-site. Don't close the job until you've received them back, or your job costs and on-hand counts will be wrong.

Supplier quotes (RFQs)

Before you buy something expensive, you usually want more than one price. A Request for Quote (RFQ) does that: describe what you want, send it to several suppliers, collect their supplier quotes, compare, then convert the winner to a PO — no re-typing.

A supplier quote is the buying-side mirror of a sales quote. Carbon makes one per supplier you ask.

Example: Acme needs a big lot of 1″ A36 steel plate. The RFQ goes to four suppliers at once — Ryerson, Olympic Steel, Reliance Steel, and MetalMart.

Send an RFQ and compare the replies

Create the RFQ and add what you want priced. In the Purchasing module, create a new RFQ and add a line per item — here, one line for A36 Steel Plate 1″ (A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240).

Choose the suppliers and send. Add the four suppliers as recipients. Carbon makes a supplier quote for each and sends it — by email or through the supplier portal.

Collect the replies. Prices land back on each supplier quote. The list shows which are still outstanding.

Compare side by side. Open the comparison view. Carbon lines the suppliers up so the cheapest price and best lead time jump out. The lowest number isn't always the right one — a slightly higher price that arrives sooner can win when a job is waiting.

Convert the winner to a PO. Pick the quote and convert it to a purchase order in one click. Supplier, item, price, and quantity carry over, and the PO stays linked to the quote. That's exactly how Acme's PO000057 to Ryerson came to be: A36 plate at $0.25/LB.

Keep the prices for next time. A supplier quote can feed a supplier's price list for that item, so next time Carbon already knows the price — no RFQ needed unless you want to re-shop it.

Drop-ship

Normally goods you buy come to you — you receive them, then ship from your own dock. Sometimes the supplier should ship straight to your customer instead. That's a drop-shipment: you still buy it and still bill your customer, but the goods never touch your building.

In Carbon it's a flag that links a sales order and a purchase order. Acme's hero order SO000069 is normal — its dropShipment flag is false (Acme makes those hooks itself). A drop-ship order sets that flag to true.

Set up a drop-ship

Mark the sales order line as drop-ship. On the sales order, turn on drop-shipment for the line. Carbon now knows the part won't come from your inventory.

Generate the linked PO. Carbon creates a PO to your supplier with the ship-to set to your customer's address, not your warehouse. Confirm supplier, price, and quantity.

Send the PO; the supplier ships your customer. Nothing arrives at your shop, so there's no receipt and no inventory movement on your side.

Invoice the customer as usual. You still bill from the sales order and pay from the PO. Your margin is the difference — Carbon tracks both sides.

Drop-ship skips your inventory on purpose. The part never appears in your on-hand stock — by design. If you want the goods first (to inspect or kit), buy it normally and ship it yourself.

The supplier portal

How do suppliers actually reply to an RFQ or confirm a PO? Carbon gives each supplier a portal — a secure web link where they respond online, with no Carbon login of their own.

DocumentWhat the supplier can do
Supplier quote / RFQEnter unit prices, lead times, minimum quantities, and notes — then submit
Purchase orderReview and acknowledge the order, confirm dates, or flag a problem

Whatever the supplier types lands straight on the matching record in your Carbon — no copy-paste, no typos. Each link is private, so a supplier only sees the documents you sent them.

No account, no friction. The portal makes it easy for small shops — like an anodizer or painter — to reply fast. The faster they respond, the faster your RFQ comparison fills in.

Purchasing planning

How do you know what to buy in the first place? Purchasing planning watches your demand and your stock, works out where you'll run short, and hands you a list of buy suggestions you can turn into POs.

Demand comes from two places:

  • Jobs. When you release a job, its method lists the materials it needs. Not enough on hand = a shortage.
  • Reorder points. For stocked items (see Replenishment), Carbon flags anything that dips below its minimum.

This is how Acme's hero PO was born. Job J000097 needed 40 LB of A36 plate per piece — 20,000 LB for the batch of 500 — and Acme was short. Carbon flagged it, and that suggestion became PO000057 to Ryerson.

Turn suggestions into purchase orders

Open the planning view. In the Purchasing module, open Planning. Carbon lists every item about to run short, the quantity it suggests, and what's driving the need.

Review and adjust. Bump a quantity to hit a price break, change the supplier, or skip a line. Carbon's number is a starting point, not a command.

Generate the POs. Select the lines and let Carbon create the POs. It groups them by supplier, so one click can make several tidy orders — all the Ryerson items on one PO.

Approve and send. New POs land in Draft or Needs Approval (like PO000057 did). From there it's the normal purchasing basics loop.

Planning closes the loop. A job creates demand, planning surfaces the shortage, a PO fills it, the receipt restocks the shelf, and the job pulls what it needs. Run planning regularly and shortages stop sneaking up on you.
