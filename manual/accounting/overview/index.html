Accounting & invoicing

Sales and purchase invoices, payments and finance integrations.

The shop earns the money; the Accounting module counts it. This page covers the two kinds of invoice and how they get paid — no accounting background needed.

Where this lives in Carbon

Invoices live in the Accounting module. Carbon builds them from what already happened — what shipped, what you received — so the numbers always match the work.

You can also see an invoice's status right on the document it came from. Sales order SO000069 below is Invoiced and Paid — the same money that became sales invoice AR000025.

The two kinds of invoice

There are only two, and they mirror each other:

  • Sales invoice (AR) — bills a customer for what you shipped. AR means Accounts Receivable: money owed to you.
  • Purchase invoice (AP) — records a bill a supplier sent you. AP means Accounts Payable: money you owe.

That's it. Acme has 25 sales invoices (AR000001–AR000025) and 27 purchase invoices (AP000001–AP000027).

Sales invoices — billing the customer

A sales invoice is how you get paid. You rarely type one from scratch — you create it from a shipment, and Carbon copies the customer, parts, quantities and prices off what shipped.

When Acme shipped 500 Counterweight Hooks to Radiant Aerospace, that became sales invoice AR000025 for $36,820.00.

Open the source. Go to Sales Invoices and click New, then pick the shipment (or sales order) to bill — here, the shipment behind SO000069. Carbon pulls in the lines automatically.

Review the lines. Check the quantities and prices match what shipped. Last chance to catch a wrong price before the customer sees it.

Post it. Posting assigns the number (AR000025) and turns it into a live receivable — money the customer now owes.

Send it. Email or download the PDF. Carbon stamps the date and starts the payment-terms clock — here, Net 30.

Sales invoices use three statuses:

StatusWhat it means
DraftBeing prepared. Not on the books, not sent.
SubmittedPosted and sent. Waiting on payment.
PaidPaid in full. Closed.

Purchase invoices — recording what you owe

A purchase invoice is the mirror image: the bill a supplier sends you. When steel arrives from Ryerson, you enter their bill so the books know what's owed.

The key idea is matching. Carbon checks the supplier's bill against two things that already exist:

  • The purchase order — the price and quantity you agreed to.
  • The receipt — what actually showed up on the dock.

If they agree, approve with confidence. If not — they billed for 100 but you got 90 — Carbon shows the gap before you pay.

Start from the PO. Go to Purchase Invoices → New and select the purchase order — for example, Ryerson's A36 steel from PO000057. Carbon pre-fills supplier, lines and prices.

Match against the receipt. Confirm the billed quantities match what you received. Carbon flags any mismatch.

Enter the supplier's bill number. Type their invoice reference and date — what you'll quote if you call them about it.

Post it. The invoice moves to Open, records the payable, and sets the due date from the supplier's terms.

Purchase invoices add a couple of statuses the sales side doesn't have:

StatusWhat it means
DraftEntered, not posted. No payable yet.
OpenPosted and approved. You owe it; within terms.
Partially PaidPart paid, a balance remains.
OverduePast due and unpaid — needs attention.
PaidSettled in full. Closed.

Recording payments

An invoice says money should move; a payment records that it did. They're separate steps on purpose — the gap between them is the time an invoice is "outstanding."

Open the invoice you're settling — say AR000025, or a supplier bill like AP000023.

Record the payment. Enter the amount, the date, and the method — check, ACH, wire, card. Radiant paid the full $36,820.00 on 2026-06-11.

Carbon updates the status. A full payment flips it to Paid; a partial payment leaves a balance. The cash entry posts to the books automatically.

Payment terms drive the due date. Each invoice carries a payment term that Carbon uses to set when payment is due. Net 30 is Acme's default. Terms flow down from the customer or supplier record onto every order and invoice, so you set them once.

It all posts to the books for you

Every posted invoice and payment writes entries to the chart of accounts — the numbered buckets the business tracks money in (cash, receivables, payables, sales, cost of goods). Acme's chart has 93 accounts, and you never post to them by hand.

When Acme posts AR000025, two things move at once so the books always balance:

Accounts Receivable (1110)
+ $36,820
Radiant now owes Acme
Sales (4010)
+ $36,570
parts revenue earned; freight posts separately

When Radiant pays, the $36,820 moves out of Accounts Receivable and into Bank - Cash (1010). On the buying side it's the reverse.

You don't need to memorize any of this. Every shipment, receipt and payment lands in the financials automatically and correctly — no spreadsheet reconciliation, no double data entry.

Already using QuickBooks?

Keep it. Carbon can sync the invoices and payments it generates out to QuickBooks so your accountant stays in the tool they know. Carbon owns operations (what shipped, what was received); QuickBooks owns the financials.

Pick your books-of-record up front. Choose one financial source of truth — Carbon or QuickBooks — and set up the integration before posting real invoices. See Integrating with Carbon.
