Managing production

Schedule, track and plan work across the shop.

One job is easy to watch. Dozens at once is the real job. This page shows how Carbon turns customer demand into a plan, tells you what to make and what to buy, and shows you whether the shop is on track.

Where this lives in Carbon

This is the Production module. Open it from the home screen, then use Planning, Jobs, and the dashboard inside it.

The dashboard is your shop at a glance: Active Jobs, Work Center Utilization, and which machines are busy. Acme runs work through machines like the DMU 50, the Inspection Station, and its Burn Tables.

Step 1: Demand in, plan out

Carbon's planning compares two things:

  • Demand — what's needed: open sales orders, materials jobs will consume, safety stock.
  • Supply — what covers it: stock on hand, open purchase orders, jobs in progress.

Where demand beats supply, Carbon raises a suggestion. For a Buy item, that's a purchase order. For a Make item, it's a job.

Loading diagram...

Here's a real example. Sales order SO000069 needs 500 Counterweight Hooks. Each hook uses 40 LB of A36 steel plate, so that's 20,000 LB of steel. Acme doesn't have that on the shelf, so Carbon suggests buying it — which became PO000057 to Ryerson. The hook itself is a Make part, so the order became job J000097.

Make vs Buy is one setting on each item. The hook is Make; its steel is Buy. That flag decides whether a shortage becomes a job or a PO. See Replenishment.

Step 2: Review suggestions in Planning

Open Production → Planning. You get one row per item that needs attention — what's required, what's available, the shortage, and the suggested action.

Set the horizon. At the top, choose how far ahead to look. Carbon only raises suggestions for demand inside the horizon.

Read each row. When Short is above zero, Carbon proposes an action. 20,000 LB of steel with nothing on hand produces a Buy → PO.

Adjust before you commit. Change a quantity or pull a date earlier. Acme's PO000057 ended up at 54,001 LB — the buyer rounded up to cover the next two Radiant orders.

Convert what you accept. Buy suggestions become draft purchase orders; Make suggestions become jobs. Carbon links each one back to the demand that created it.

A suggestion is a proposal, never automatic. Carbon won't raise a PO or release a job on its own — you always confirm.

Step 3: Schedule jobs across work centers

Every operation in a job points at a work center — a machine or station like the DMU 50 or a Burn Table. Each operation carries a time, and operations run After Previous (step 20 waits for step 10). That's how Carbon builds a schedule.

Job J000097 ran three operations in sequence:

Find the bottleneck. The dashboard's Work Center Utilization shows load per machine. Acme has three Burn Tables but only one Sand Blasting Room — so sandblasting is the constraint.

Balance the load. When a process has more than one machine, move work to a less-busy one. Flame Cut can run on Burn Table PA1, PA2, or the Texas table.

Respect the sequence. Operations run After Previous, so Carbon keeps them in order. You decide where the whole chain fits against other jobs.

Work-center rates also cost your jobs. Time booked on Burn Table PA2 absorbs labor at $50/hr and machine at $25/hr — so J000097's cost came from recorded shop time, not a guess.

Step 4: Prioritize what runs first

When more work wants a machine than it can handle, priority and due date decide the order. Here's Acme's live Counterweight Hook backlog — three jobs for the same customer, stacked up:

Read it as a plan: J000097 finished first, J000098 is running now, J000099 is staged and Ready. Sequencing by due date keeps the single Sand Blasting Room from causing a missed deadline.

A job must be released before the floor can touch it. Statuses go in order: Draft → Planned → Ready → In Progress → Completed. Releasing is on the Jobs page.

Step 5: Track progress

The jobs list is your live status board. Completion updates itself as operators record finished pieces in the shop floor app — you never type a percentage by hand.

What to read off this screen:

  • Completion is real. J000097 shows 500 of 500 because operators clocked off all 500 pieces.
  • Status says where to look. In Progress jobs are on the floor now — J000095 and J000098 get today's attention.
  • Due dates flag risk. J000095 is due 2026-04-23 — already past — and still running. That's your cue to investigate.

The loop. Demand creates suggestions → you turn them into jobs and POs → you schedule jobs across work centers → operators run them → the dashboard updates in real time → new demand restarts it.
