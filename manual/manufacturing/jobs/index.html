Jobs

Create production jobs from a sales order or from scratch.

A job tells the shop floor to make a set quantity of a part by a set date.

We'll follow one real job: J000097 — 500 Counterweight Hooks for Radiant Aerospace.

Where this lives in Carbon

Jobs live in the Production module. Open it from the home screen, and the dashboard shows your live shop at a glance — Active Jobs, work center use, and more. Click View Active Jobs to see the full list.

What a job carries

One job answers five questions:

  • What are we making? The part — here, CWH-A36-15, the Counterweight Hook.
  • How many? The production quantity — 500 EA.
  • By when? The due date — 2026-06-26.
  • How is it made? The method — its materials and steps.
  • Where are we? The status, and how many good pieces are done.

The big idea: a job carries its own copy of the method. When you create it, Carbon snapshots the part's bill of materials and routing onto the job. Edit the part's master method later — this job keeps the recipe it was born with.

That's what keeps a finished job's cost and traceability trustworthy months later.

Method = BOM + Routing. The bill of materials (BOM) is what the part is made of; the routing is the ordered steps to make it. A job copies both. See Methods: BOM & routing.

How a job gets created

There are two ways.

1. Automatically, from a Make-to-Order sales order line. Carbon offers to spin up a job, pre-filled with the part, quantity, customer and due date — and keeps it linked to the line. That's how J000097 was born, straight from sales order SO000069.

The same link happens for Radiant's other orders: SO000070 made job J000098 (1,500 EA) and SO000071 made job J000099 (3,000 EA). One part, three orders, three jobs.

2. Manually, when a job stands on its own — building to stock, a rework batch, or a one-off. Here's the click path:

Open the Jobs list. In the Production module, click View Active Jobs (or open Jobs). Click New Job.

Pick the part. Choose a Make item like CWH-A36-15. Carbon pulls in its description and unit of measure (EA).

Set quantity, due date and priority. Enter how many to make, when they're due, and how urgent the job is.

Save. Carbon assigns the next job number and snapshots the method. The job opens as Planned, ready for review.

The fields that drive a job

Open J000097 and you land on its Details tab. A few fields do most of the work.

  • Production quantity — how many good pieces to finish. Here, 500.
  • Due date — when they must be done. The deadline type (Hard Deadline here) tells Carbon how firm that date is.
  • Priority — ranks this job against others for the same machines. J000097 is 1, the highest, so it jumps the queue.

Location matters. A job runs at one location. J000097 runs at Headquarters, so it uses Headquarters work centers (like Burn Table — PA2) and pulls material from Headquarters stock.

The method the job carries

Click the Method tab to see the recipe Carbon copied onto the job. For the Counterweight Hook it's short: one material, three operations.

Two things to notice:

  • The BOM has one material line: 40 LB of A36 steel plate per piece. Times 500, the job needs 20,000 LB — Carbon does the math.
  • The material is Pull from Inventory, so the job draws steel from stock. The purchase order to Ryerson makes sure that stock is there.

Because the job carries its own copy of the method, you can tweak it for this run only — swap a material, add a step, change a time — without touching the part's master method or any other job.

Operations: the steps, in order

The Operations tab lists the routing. Each operation runs at one work center and has a time per piece. They run in sequence — Sandblasting can't start until Flame Cut is done.

Labor time is per piece. As operators report finished pieces in the shop-floor app, the Complete and Status columns fill in — that's how all three reached Done.

The last operation, Inspection, is a quality gate: the parts aren't finished until they pass. See Tracking quality.

Materials: what the job consumes

The Materials tab is the job's shopping list. For J000097 it's one line.

The steel is batch-tracked, so Carbon records exactly which heat went into this batch of hooks — the foundation of Radiant's traceability. It pulls from bin R02-B01-01 at Headquarters.

Releasing a job to the floor

A new job is Planned — a draft you can edit freely. Nothing reaches the floor until you release it.

Review the job. Confirm quantity, due date and priority on Details, then skim Method, Operations and Materials.

Check material. Carbon flags shortages. For J000097, not all 20,000 LB of steel was on hand, so Acme raised PO000057 to Ryerson. Buying and making can run in parallel.

Release the job. Click Release. Carbon stamps the released date, moves the job into the active queue, and makes its operations available in the shop-floor app.

The floor takes over. Operators clock onto each operation and report pieces. When the last operation hits the production quantity, the job is Completed.

Job statuses

A job moves through four statuses, from idea to finished parts:

Loading diagram...
StatusWhat it means
PlannedCreated but not released. A draft — edit quantity, dates and method freely. Not on the shop floor yet.
ReadyReviewed and queued — material and capacity lined up, waiting its turn (e.g. J000099).
In ProgressReleased and being worked. Operators clock time and report pieces (e.g. J000098, J000095).
CompletedAll good pieces finished and received to stock. The job's true cost is final (e.g. J000097).

A Planned job does nothing on its own. It won't appear on the shop floor and operators can't book time until you release it. If a job seems "stuck," check whether it's still Planned.

Where the numbers go next

Once J000097 completed, its 500 hooks were received to stock, shipped to Radiant Aerospace, and billed on invoice AR000025. The job's labor and material rolled up into the cost of those parts.
